First Principles: Foundations

From physics to harmony

🔊 The Harmonic Series

The harmonic series is the foundation of all Western harmony. When a string vibrates, it oscillates not just at its fundamental frequency, but at whole-number multiples (harmonics). These frequency ratios determine which intervals sound consonant.

Partial Frequency Ratio Interval 1 1:1 Unison (C) 2 2:1 Octave (C) 3 3:1 Perfect 12th (G) 4 4:1 Two octaves (C) 5 5:1 Major 17th (E) 6 6:1 Perfect 19th (G)

Why these ratios matter: 2:1 (octave), 3:2 (perfect 5th), 5:4 (major 3rd), and 6:5 (minor 3rd) are all built from simple harmonic ratios. Complex ratios create dissonance.

🎶 Scales and Modes

Major Scale: W-W-H-W-W-W-H

Example in C Major: C–D–E–F–G–A–B–C

💡 Minor Scales (Natural, Harmonic, Melodic)

Natural Minor (Aeolian): W-H-W-W-H-W-W

Example: A–B–C–D–E–F–G–A

Harmonic Minor: W-H-W-W-H-W+H-H

Raises the 7th degree for a stronger leading tone. Example: A–B–C–D–E–F–G#–A

Melodic Minor: W-H-W-W-W-W-H ascending; reverse descending

Raises 6th and 7th when ascending for smoother melodies.

📏 Intervals

Intervals are distances between two pitches. They're classified by letter-name span (2nd, 3rd, 4th, etc.) and quality (major, minor, perfect, augmented, diminished).

Interval Semitones Quality Example (from C)
Unison 0 Perfect (P1) C–C
2nd 1-2 Minor (m2), Major (M2) C–D♭ (m2), C–D (M2)
3rd 3-4 Minor (m3), Major (M3) C–E♭ (m3), C–E (M3)
5th 7 Perfect (P5) C–G
Octave 12 Perfect (P8) C–C

🎼 Voice Leading Principles

The foundation of part-writing. These rules preserve voice independence and singability.

  • Parallel Fifths/Octaves: Two voices cannot move in the same direction to the same interval. This erases voice independence.
  • Tendency Tone Resolution: The leading tone (7̂) must resolve up to the tonic (8̂).
  • Doubling Rules: Prefer to double the root or fifth. Avoid doubling the third, especially tendency tones.
  • Spacing Rules: Soprano–Alto ≤ 1 octave, Alto–Tenor ≤ 1 octave. Bass can be further.
  • Voice Crossing: Generally avoid having a lower voice (alto) higher than an upper voice (soprano).

🔄 Circle of Fifths

The master organizational tool showing all 12 keys arranged by perfect fifth relationships. Moving clockwise adds sharps; counterclockwise adds flats.

  • Key signatures: Reading the circle tells you the number of sharps/flats
  • Chord progressions: Most natural progressions move around the circle
  • Modulation: Keys along the circle are closely related
  • Relative keys: Major and relative minor are inner and outer circles at the same position

Sample Exam Questions

With solutions and rubrics

Free Response Question 1: Part-Writing (Major Key)

FRQ 1 — 10 Points
Complete this four-part chorale in F Major by writing alto and tenor voices. Apply correct voice-leading principles: avoid parallel fifths/octaves, resolve tendency tones, use proper spacing and doubling.
Soprano: F A G F E D C Bass: F F E F C D F Chords: I IV6 I I6/4 V4/3 V I
Solution Approach: First identify each chord from the figured bass. Then fill in alto and tenor using common-tone connections when possible, stepwise motion otherwise. Check for parallel fifths (especially soprano/bass), ensure leading tone B resolves to C, and verify spacing (S–A ≤ octave, A–T ≤ octave).

Multiple Choice Question 1: Interval Identification

MCQ 1 — 1 Point
What is the interval from C to G♯?
A) Perfect 5th
B) Augmented 5th ✓
C) Diminished 5th
D) Augmented 4th
Explanation: C to G is a perfect 5th (7 semitones). G♯ is one semitone higher, making it an augmented 5th (8 semitones). The letter span is still a 5th, so the quality modifies the 5th.

Multiple Choice Question 2: Roman Numeral Analysis

MCQ 2 — 1 Point
In G Major, what chord is G–B–D–F?
A) I7
B) V7 in the context of C Major
C) vii°6/5 in G Major
D) Imaj7
Explanation: G–B–D is G major (the tonic in G Major). F natural creates a dominant 7th chord. In this context, this is the V7 chord that would resolve to C Major (V7 in secondary dominant analysis).

Common Misconceptions

And how to fix them

❌ "Parallel fifths are just a random rule"

The Fix: When two voices move in parallel fifths or octaves, they sound like ONE voice, not two independent voices. Western harmony is built on four equal, independent singers. If two always move together, one isn't really singing—they're echoing. That's bad orchestration.

❌ "I can't hear intervals"

The Fix: Use the "Song Reference" method. Anchor each interval to a famous song you already know: Perfect 5th = Star Wars Theme, Major 3rd = "When the Saints Go Marching In", Major 6th = NBC Chimes. Sing the song mentally, then compare it to the interval you hear. Practice 5 minutes daily for 2 weeks and you'll have muscle memory.

❌ "Figured bass is just random numbers"

The Fix: Numbers = intervals above the bass. "6" = first inversion (3rd in bass, 6th above bass). "6/4" = second inversion (5th in bass). Once you know the bass note and the figures, you can reconstruct the entire chord.

❌ "Rhythm dictation: compound vs. simple meter"

The Fix: Simple meter beats divide by 2 (two eighth notes per quarter-note beat). Compound meter beats divide by 3 (three eighth notes per dotted-quarter beat). Count the subdivision: groups of 2 = simple; groups of 3 = compound.

Deep Dive: The Hardest Parts

Step-by-step mastery

1. Part-Writing: The Step-by-Step Checklist

The Procedure:

  1. Identify each chord: Write out all pitch classes (root, third, fifth, seventh if applicable)
  2. Check bass against soprano: Do they both belong to the chord?
  3. Assign chord tones to alto & tenor: Use doubling rule (prefer root or 5th)
  4. Check voice-leading between chords: Common tone? Stepwise motion? No parallels?
  5. Check spacing: S–A ≤ octave, A–T ≤ octave, no voice crossing
  6. Final check: Play through mentally; is each voice singable?

2. Secondary Dominants: "The V of V"

A secondary dominant is a dominant chord applied to a chord other than the tonic. It temporarily "tonicizes" that chord before resolving back.

Example: V/V in G Major - The V in G Major is D major (D–F♯–A) - To tonicize D, we need the dominant of D: A major (A–C♯–E) - C♯ doesn't exist in G Major, so it's an accidental - V/V in G Major = A–C♯–E - Resolution: A–C♯–E → D major (C♯ resolves to D)

3. Hearing Harmonic Progressions in Dictation

Strategy: Focus on the bass line first. It's the easiest to isolate. Once you have the bass, the chords become obvious.

  • Step 1: Listen for the bass line and notate pitch + rhythm
  • Step 2: Identify harmonic rhythm (chord changes per beat?)
  • Step 3: Spot recognizable cadences (PAC = V–I, HC = I–V or IV–V)
  • Step 4: Determine Roman numerals and inversions

Chord Construction: "135" Method

All chords are built on 1–3–5 (root, third, fifth): Major: 1–3–5 (M3, P5) Minor: 1–♭3–5 (m3, P5) Diminished: 1–♭3–♭5 (m3, d5) Augmented: 1–3–♯5 (M3, A5) Seventh Chords: Add the 7th Maj7: 1–3–5–7 Dominant 7: 1–3–5–♭7 Minor 7: 1–♭3–5–♭7

Voice Leading Checklist: "SPACED"

  • S = Spacing: S–A ≤ octave, A–T ≤ octave, T–B can be further
  • P = Parallel Fifths/Octaves: Check S–B especially (forbidden!)
  • A = Augmented Intervals: Avoid
  • C = Crossed Voices: Soprano stays above alto, etc.
  • E = Even Distribution: Use all chord tones
  • D = Doubling: Double root or 5th, never 3rd or tendency tones

Physics Connections

For the curious scientist

🌊 The Harmonic Series = Standing Waves

When a string of length L vibrates, it creates standing waves. The fundamental frequency occurs when one half-wavelength fits the string.

Fundamental (1st harmonic): λ = 2L f₁ = v / 2L (where v = wave speed) 2nd Harmonic: λ = L (two half-wavelengths fit) f₂ = 2 × f₁ nth Harmonic: fₙ = n × f₁

This IS the harmonic series! Frequencies are integer multiples of the fundamental. Frequency ratios 2:1, 3:2, 5:4, 6:5 all come from nearby harmonics and sound consonant.

🎹 Just Intonation vs. Equal Temperament

Just Intonation: All intervals built from simple frequency ratios (5:4 = major 3rd, 3:2 = perfect 5th). Pure and beautiful, but can't modulate to distant keys without "wolf intervals."

Equal Temperament: The octave divided into 12 equal semitones (2^(1/12) ≈ 1.059463). Sacrifices purity for the ability to modulate to any key. All intervals equally "out of tune" by ~2 cents.

🔊 Acoustic Resonance

A Helmholtz resonator (like a guitar body or concert hall) resonates strongly at specific frequencies. These "sweet spots" determine timbre and acoustic color.

Why each guitar sounds different: Wood dimensions, shape, and mass determine the resonant frequency and damping. This is why acoustic instruments have personality!

🧠 Psychoacoustics: How Your Brain Hears

  • Octave Equivalence: Notes one octave apart sound "the same" (2:1 frequency ratio)
  • Beats: When two frequencies are close but not identical, you hear "wobbling." f_beat = |f₁ − f₂|
  • Masking: Loud frequencies "mask" nearby quiet frequencies (why MP3 compression works)
  • Dissonance: Dissonant intervals create beats and masking, heard as "unresolved" tension
