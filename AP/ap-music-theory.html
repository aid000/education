AP Music Theory: Overview

Complete exam format, structure, and strategy

📊 Exam Structure

Total Time: 150 minutes | Total Score: 70 points

Section I: Multiple Choice (80 minutes, 45% of score)

  • Part A (Aural): ~30-35 questions on interval, chord, and harmonic recognition by ear
  • Part B (Non-Aural): ~40-45 questions on written analysis, Roman numeral analysis, voice leading

Section II: Free Response (70 minutes, 55% of score)

  • Question 1-2: Melodic Dictation (aural, 10 pts each)
  • Question 3: Harmonic Dictation (aural, 10 pts)
  • Question 4-5: Part-Writing (10 pts each)
  • Question 6-7: Sight-Singing (10 pts each)

🎯 Key Skills Tested

  • Aural interval and chord identification
  • Melodic and harmonic dictation
  • Roman numeral analysis and figured bass
  • Four-part voice writing with correct voice leading
  • Form analysis (binary, ternary, rondo, sonata-allegro)
  • Sight-singing and rhythmic accuracy

First Principles: Foundations

From physics to harmony

🔊 The Harmonic Series

The harmonic series is the foundation of all Western harmony. When a string vibrates, it oscillates not just at its fundamental frequency, but at whole-number multiples (harmonics). These frequency ratios determine which intervals sound consonant.

Partial Frequency Ratio Interval from Fundamental
11:1Unison (C)
22:1Octave (C)
33:1Perfect 12th (G) — 3:2 = Perfect 5th
44:1Two octaves (C)
55:1Major 17th (E) — 5:4 = Major 3rd
66:1Perfect 19th (G) — 6:5 = Minor 3rd

Why these ratios matter: 2:1 (octave), 3:2 (perfect 5th), 5:4 (major 3rd), and 6:5 (minor 3rd) are all built from simple harmonic ratios. Complex ratios create dissonance.

🎶 Scales and Modes

Major Scale: W-W-H-W-W-W-H

Example in C Major: C–D–E–F–G–A–B–C

💡 Minor Scales (Natural, Harmonic, Melodic)

Natural Minor (Aeolian): W-H-W-W-H-W-W

Example: A–B–C–D–E–F–G–A

Harmonic Minor: W-H-W-W-H-W+H-H

Raises the 7th degree for a stronger leading tone. Example: A–B–C–D–E–F–G#–A

Melodic Minor: W-H-W-W-W-W-H ascending; reverse descending

Raises 6th and 7th when ascending for smoother melodies.

📏 Intervals

Intervals are distances between two pitches. They're classified by letter-name span (2nd, 3rd, 4th, etc.) and quality (major, minor, perfect, augmented, diminished).

Interval Semitones Quality Example (from C)
Unison 0 Perfect (P1) C–C
2nd 1-2 Minor (m2), Major (M2) C–D♭ (m2), C–D (M2)
3rd 3-4 Minor (m3), Major (M3) C–E♭ (m3), C–E (M3)
5th 7 Perfect (P5) C–G
Octave 12 Perfect (P8) C–C

🎼 Voice Leading Principles

The foundation of part-writing. These rules preserve voice independence and singability.

  • Parallel Fifths/Octaves: Two voices cannot move in the same direction to the same interval. This erases voice independence.
  • Tendency Tone Resolution: The leading tone (7̂) must resolve up to the tonic (8̂).
  • Doubling Rules: Prefer to double the root or fifth. Avoid doubling the third, especially tendency tones.
  • Spacing Rules: Soprano–Alto ≤ 1 octave, Alto–Tenor ≤ 1 octave. Bass can be further.
  • Voice Crossing: Generally avoid having a lower voice (alto) higher than an upper voice (soprano).

🔄 Circle of Fifths

The master organizational tool showing all 12 keys arranged by perfect fifth relationships. Moving clockwise adds sharps; counterclockwise adds flats.

  • Key signatures: Reading the circle tells you the number of sharps/flats
  • Chord progressions: Most natural progressions move around the circle
  • Modulation: Keys along the circle are closely related
  • Relative keys: Major and relative minor are inner and outer circles at the same position

Sample Exam Questions

With solutions and rubrics

Free Response Question 1: Part-Writing (Major Key)

FRQ 1 — 10 Points
Complete this four-part chorale in F Major by writing alto and tenor voices. Apply correct voice-leading principles: avoid parallel fifths/octaves, resolve tendency tones, use proper spacing and doubling.
Soprano: F A G F E D C Bass: F F E F C D F Chords: I IV6 I I6/4 V4/3 V I
Solution Approach: First identify each chord from the figured bass. Then fill in alto and tenor using common-tone connections when possible, stepwise motion otherwise. Check for parallel fifths (especially soprano/bass), ensure leading tone B resolves to C, and verify spacing (S–A ≤ octave, A–T ≤ octave).

Multiple Choice Question 1: Interval Identification

MCQ 1 — 1 Point
What is the interval from C to G♯?
A) Perfect 5th
B) Augmented 5th
C) Diminished 5th
D) Augmented 4th
Show Explanation
Explanation: C to G is a perfect 5th (7 semitones). G♯ is one semitone higher, making it an augmented 5th (8 semitones). The letter span is still a 5th, so the quality modifies the 5th.

Multiple Choice Question 2: Roman Numeral Analysis

MCQ 2 — 1 Point
In G Major, what chord is G–B–D–F?
A) I7
B) V7 in the context of C Major
C) vii°6/5 in G Major
D) Imaj7
Show Explanation
Explanation: G–B–D is G major (the tonic in G Major). F natural creates a dominant 7th chord. In this context, this is the V7 chord that would resolve to C Major (V7 in secondary dominant analysis).

Free Response Question 2: Part-Writing from Roman Numerals (Minor Key)

FRQ 2 — 10 Points
Write a four-part SATB chorale in D minor with the progression: i - iv⁶ - V - i. The soprano starts on D5. Apply correct voice-leading principles throughout.
Show Solution

Voice Assignments:

Bass: D (root of i) – D (root of iv⁶) – A (root of V) – D (root of i)
Tenor: F (5th) – F (common tone) – E (3rd, leading tone) – D (resolves to root)
Alto: A (3rd) – A (common tone) – C♯ (leading tone in harmonic minor) – A (resolves up to 3rd)
Soprano: D5 (root) – D5 (root, stay on common tone) – E5 (3rd) – D5 (resolves to root)

Key Points: Note the C♯ in the alto (leading tone in harmonic minor) resolves upward to D. The iv⁶ chord inverts the iv triad, placing the 6th (A) above the bass (D). Avoid parallel fifths/octaves between all voice pairs. Spacing: S–A ≤ octave (D5 to A4 = 6th ✓), A–T ≤ octave (A4 to F3 = 5th ✓).

Free Response Question 3: Melodic Dictation

FRQ 3 — 10 Points
A melody in G major (4/4 time, 4 measures) is played. Notate the pitch and rhythm on the staff provided. The melody is: G–A–B–D | C–B–A–G | A–B–C–A | B–A–G (whole note).
Show Solution

Transcription:

Measure 1: G (quarter) – A (quarter) – B (quarter) – D (quarter)
Measure 2: C (quarter) – B (quarter) – A (quarter) – G (quarter)
Measure 3: A (quarter) – B (quarter) – C (quarter) – A (quarter)
Measure 4: B (quarter) – A (quarter) – G (whole note)

Scoring Rubric: Up to 10 points total. 5 points for correct pitch sequence (all 16 notes). 3 points for correct rhythm. 2 points for proper key signature (1 sharp, F♯) and time signature (4/4). Partial credit awarded for sections with correct pitch/rhythm.

Multiple Choice Question 3: Key Signature Identification

MCQ 3 — 1 Point
A piece with 4 flats in the key signature is in which major key?
A) A♭ major
B) D♭ major
C) E♭ major
D) G♭ major
Show Solution
Explanation: Use the Circle of Fifths: 4 flats = B♭–E♭–A♭–D♭. Reading the mnemonic "Battle Ends And Down Goes Charles' Father" counterclockwise, the 4th flat is D♭. In major keys, the tonic is always 4th flat UP from the last flat (or one line/space above the last flat on the staff). D♭ is the last flat, so A♭ is the major key. Answer: A) A♭ major.

Multiple Choice Question 4: Non-Chord Tone Identification

MCQ 4 — 1 Point
In a C major chord (C–E–G), a D that occurs on a weak beat between C and E is called:
A) Suspension
B) Passing tone
C) Neighbor tone
D) Appoggiatura
Show Solution
Explanation: A passing tone is a non-chord tone that moves stepwise between two chord tones. Here, D steps from C → D → E, filling the gap. It occurs on a weak beat (not accented), which is typical for passing tones. A suspension requires preparation (chord tone in previous chord), while an appoggiatura is accented and non-stepwise. Answer: B) Passing tone.

Multiple Choice Question 5: Cadence Identification

MCQ 5 — 1 Point
A phrase ending with V → vi is called:
A) Perfect Authentic Cadence
B) Half Cadence
C) Deceptive Cadence
D) Plagal Cadence
Show Solution
Explanation: A deceptive cadence (also called a "false cadence") occurs when the listener expects V → I (perfect authentic cadence), but instead hears V → vi (the relative minor). This creates surprise and often continues the phrase rather than ending it conclusively. The harmonic expectation is thwarted, hence "deceptive." Answer: C) Deceptive Cadence.

Multiple Choice Question 6: Chord Quality

MCQ 6 — 1 Point
The chord B–D–F is:
A) Major
B) Minor
C) Diminished
D) Augmented
Show Solution
Explanation: B to D = 3 semitones (minor 3rd). D to F = 3 semitones (minor 3rd). Diminished triads are built with two minor 3rds stacked: m3 + m3. Total from root to 5th = 6 semitones (diminished 5th). This is B diminished, symbol: B°. Answer: C) Diminished.

Free Response Question 4: Harmonic Analysis

FRQ 4 — 10 Points
Analyze the harmonic progression in C major. Provide Roman numerals for each chord. Identify the cadence type and explain your reasoning.
Chord Progression (soprano–bass):
Chord 1: C–E–G (bass: C)
Chord 2: F–A–C (bass: F)
Chord 3: D–F–A (bass: D)
Chord 4: G–B–D (bass: G)
Chord 5: C–E–G (bass: C, soprano on C)
Show Solution

Roman Numeral Analysis:

  • Chord 1 (C–E–G, bass C) = I (root position)
  • Chord 2 (F–A–C, bass F) = IV (root position)
  • Chord 3 (D–F–A, bass D) = ii (root position, D–F–A = D minor triad)
  • Chord 4 (G–B–D, bass G) = V (root position)
  • Chord 5 (C–E–G, bass C, soprano C) = I (root position, authentic cadence resolution)

Progression: I – IV – ii – V – I

Cadence Type: Perfect Authentic Cadence (PAC) — V → I in root position with soprano ending on 1̂ (C). This is the strongest, most conclusive cadence type.

Scoring: 6 points for correct chord identifications (1 pt each). 2 points for correct cadence type. 2 points for clear explanation and voice-leading observations.

Common Misconceptions

And how to fix them

❌ "Parallel fifths are just a random rule"

The Fix: When two voices move in parallel fifths or octaves, they sound like ONE voice, not two independent voices. Western harmony is built on four equal, independent singers. If two always move together, one isn't really singing—they're echoing. That's bad orchestration.

❌ "I can't hear intervals"

The Fix: Use the "Song Reference" method. Anchor each interval to a famous song you already know: Perfect 5th = Star Wars Theme, Major 3rd = "When the Saints Go Marching In", Major 6th = NBC Chimes. Sing the song mentally, then compare it to the interval you hear. Practice 5 minutes daily for 2 weeks and you'll have muscle memory.

❌ "Figured bass is just random numbers"

The Fix: Numbers = intervals above the bass. "6" = first inversion (3rd in bass, 6th above bass). "6/4" = second inversion (5th in bass). Once you know the bass note and the figures, you can reconstruct the entire chord.

❌ "Rhythm dictation: compound vs. simple meter"

The Fix: Simple meter beats divide by 2 (two eighth notes per quarter-note beat). Compound meter beats divide by 3 (three eighth notes per dotted-quarter beat). Count the subdivision: groups of 2 = simple; groups of 3 = compound.

Deep Dive: The Hardest Parts

Step-by-step mastery

1. Part-Writing: The Step-by-Step Checklist

The Procedure:

  1. Identify each chord: Write out all pitch classes (root, third, fifth, seventh if applicable)
  2. Check bass against soprano: Do they both belong to the chord?
  3. Assign chord tones to alto & tenor: Use doubling rule (prefer root or 5th)
  4. Check voice-leading between chords: Common tone? Stepwise motion? No parallels?
  5. Check spacing: S–A ≤ octave, A–T ≤ octave, no voice crossing
  6. Final check: Play through mentally; is each voice singable?
Each voice is like a standing wave mode — independent frequencies that combine into a harmonious whole. Voice independence in music mirrors mode independence in physics.
2. Secondary Dominants: "The V of V" Demystified

A secondary dominant is a dominant chord applied to a chord other than the tonic. It temporarily "tonicizes" that chord.

Example: V/V in G Major

  • The V chord in G Major is D major (D–F♯–A)
  • To tonicize D, we need the dominant OF D: A major (A–C♯–E)
  • C♯ doesn't exist in G Major — it's a chromatic alteration (accidental)
  • So V/V in G Major = A–C♯–E
  • Resolution: A–C♯–E → D–F♯–A (the C♯ resolves up to D as a leading tone)

How to Identify Secondary Dominants:

  1. See an accidental? It might be a secondary dominant.
  2. Check: does the chord with the accidental resolve to a diatonic chord by moving up a P4 (or down a P5)?
  3. If yes → it's V/[that diatonic chord]
3. Hearing Harmonic Progressions in Dictation

Strategy: Focus on the bass line first. It's the easiest voice to isolate and determines the chord root and inversion.

Step-by-Step Dictation Method:

  1. First hearing: Listen for bass line — notate just pitch and rhythm of the lowest voice
  2. Second hearing: Identify harmonic rhythm (how often do chords change?) and spot cadences
  3. Third hearing: Assign Roman numerals based on bass notes + soprano contour. Listen for strong-beat vs. weak-beat chords.
  4. Final hearing: Verify inversions (bass note ≠ root? then it's inverted) and check for non-chord tones.

Cadence Recognition Shortcut:

  • PAC (Perfect Authentic): Bass moves 5̂→1̂ (down a 5th or up a 4th), soprano ends on 1̂. Feels "final."
  • HC (Half Cadence): Phrase ends ON V. Feels "unfinished" — like a question mark.
  • DC (Deceptive): You expect V→I but hear V→vi instead. Surprise!
  • PC (Plagal/Amen): IV→I. The "amen" cadence. Gentle, not dramatic.
4. Modulation: Identifying Key Changes

Modulation is when the music changes key. On the AP exam, you need to identify WHERE it happens and WHAT the new key is.

Common Modulation Types:

  • Common-chord (pivot) modulation: A chord that exists in BOTH keys serves as the "bridge." Most common type. Example: In C major, Am (vi) is also ii in G major.
  • Direct modulation: New key starts abruptly without a pivot chord. Often at a new phrase.
  • Chromatic modulation: A chromatic note signals the new key (often a new leading tone).

Detection Strategy:

  1. Listen for accidentals — a new sharp or flat often signals modulation
  2. The most common modulation is to the DOMINANT key (C→G) or relative major/minor
  3. Find the new leading tone: it's a half-step below the new tonic
5. Suspensions: Preparation–Dissonance–Resolution

A suspension is a non-chord tone that's held over from the previous chord, creating a dissonance that then resolves downward by step.

The Three Phases:

  1. Preparation: The note is a consonant chord tone in the first chord
  2. Suspension: The chord changes, but this note is HELD — creating dissonance against the new chord
  3. Resolution: The held note moves DOWN by step to become a chord tone of the new chord

Common Suspension Types (named by intervals above bass):

  • 4-3 suspension: Note hangs on the 4th above bass, resolves down to 3rd. Most common!
  • 7-6 suspension: 7th resolves to 6th. Common in first-inversion contexts.
  • 9-8 suspension: 9th (=2nd) resolves to octave. Dramatic effect.
  • 2-3 suspension (bass suspension): The BASS voice is suspended. Resolution goes UP (exception!).

Key rule: Suspensions ALWAYS resolve DOWN by step (except bass suspensions). If the "suspended" note resolves up, it's likely an appoggiatura or retardation, not a suspension.

Chord Construction: "135" Method

All chords are built on 1–3–5 (root, third, fifth):

  • Major: 1–3–5 (M3, P5)
  • Minor: 1–♭3–5 (m3, P5)
  • Diminished: 1–♭3–♭5 (m3, d5)
  • Augmented: 1–3–♯5 (M3, A5)

  • Maj7: 1–3–5–7
  • Dominant 7: 1–3–5–♭7
  • Minor 7: 1–♭3–5–♭7

Voice Leading Checklist: "SPACED"

  • S = Spacing: S–A ≤ octave, A–T ≤ octave, T–B can be further
  • P = Parallel Fifths/Octaves: Check S–B especially (forbidden!)
  • A = Augmented Intervals: Avoid
  • C = Crossed Voices: Soprano stays above alto, etc.
  • E = Even Distribution: Use all chord tones
  • D = Doubling: Double root or 5th, never 3rd or tendency tones

Physics Connections

For the curious scientist

🌊 The Harmonic Series = Standing Waves

When a string of length L vibrates, it creates standing waves. The fundamental frequency occurs when one half-wavelength fits the string.

Fundamental (1st harmonic):

Wavelength: λ = 2L

Frequency: f₁ = v / 2L  (where v = wave speed on the string)

2nd Harmonic:

Wavelength: λ = L  (two half-wavelengths fit the string)

Frequency: f₂ = 2 × f₁

nth Harmonic (general formula):

fₙ = n × f₁

Every harmonic is an integer multiple of the fundamental — this single equation generates the entire harmonic series.

This IS the harmonic series! Frequencies are integer multiples of the fundamental. Frequency ratios 2:1, 3:2, 5:4, 6:5 all come from nearby harmonics and sound consonant.

🎹 Just Intonation vs. Equal Temperament

Just Intonation: All intervals built from simple frequency ratios (5:4 = major 3rd, 3:2 = perfect 5th). Pure and beautiful, but can't modulate to distant keys without "wolf intervals."

Equal Temperament: The octave divided into 12 equal semitones (2^(1/12) ≈ 1.059463). Sacrifices purity for the ability to modulate to any key. All intervals equally "out of tune" by ~2 cents.

🔊 Acoustic Resonance

A Helmholtz resonator (like a guitar body or concert hall) resonates strongly at specific frequencies. These "sweet spots" determine timbre and acoustic color.

Why each guitar sounds different: Wood dimensions, shape, and mass determine the resonant frequency and damping. This is why acoustic instruments have personality!

🧠 Psychoacoustics: How Your Brain Hears

  • Octave Equivalence: Notes one octave apart sound "the same" (2:1 frequency ratio)
  • Beats: When two frequencies are close but not identical, you hear "wobbling." f_beat = |f₁ − f₂|
  • Masking: Loud frequencies "mask" nearby quiet frequencies (why MP3 compression works)
  • Dissonance: Dissonant intervals create beats and masking, heard as "unresolved" tension
Research Ideas:
  • Build an acoustic levitation rig using ultrasonic transducers (~40 kHz)
  • Measure guitar vibrational modes with accelerometers; correlate with timbre
  • Use FFT software to decompose instrument tones into harmonics
  • Design a psychoacoustics listening test for beat detection or masking
  • Build a synthesizer that plays in both just intonation and equal temperament

Final Exam Strategy & Advice

⏱️ Time Management Blueprint

Section I Part A — Aural MCQs (~40 min): You CANNOT control the pace — the recording plays and you answer. Listen actively on first playing, answer on second playing. If you miss a question, let it go — the next clip is already playing. Circle uncertain answers to revisit if time allows at the end of this section.

Section I Part B — Written MCQs (~40 min): ~1 min per question. Score-reading and Roman numeral analysis questions take longest — flag them and do interval/chord ID questions first (fastest points). Use process of elimination: if you can identify the key and the bass note, you can often eliminate 2 choices immediately.

Section II — Melodic Dictation (2 questions): You'll hear each melody multiple times. First hearing: get the rhythm and contour (up/down shape). Second hearing: lock in specific pitches, especially the first and last notes. Third hearing: fill in the middle. Write scale degrees above staff as a safety net.

Section II — Harmonic Dictation (1 question): Focus on the BASS LINE first — it determines the chord. Listen for cadences to anchor your analysis (V→I at the end, often IV or ii before V). Fill in Roman numerals based on bass + soprano movement.

Section II — Part-Writing (2 questions): ~8-10 min each. Follow the SPACED checklist for EVERY chord. Work from bass up: bass note → identify chord → add tenor/alto following voice-leading rules → check for errors. Double the root in root-position chords.

Section II — Sight-Singing (2 questions): You get 75 seconds to study, then record. Silently audiate (hear internally) before you sing. Find do, establish the key in your head, tap the rhythm, THEN sing. Start confidently — first impressions matter in scoring.

📝 Part-Writing Zero-Error Checklist

Run this check on EVERY chord transition before moving to the next. This single habit prevents 80% of part-writing errors:

  • S — Spacing: Adjacent upper voices (S-A, A-T) must be within an octave. T-B can exceed an octave.
  • P — Parallels: Check for parallel 5ths and octaves between ALL voice pairs (S-A, S-T, S-B, A-T, A-B, T-B). That's 6 pairs to check.
  • A — Augmented intervals: No augmented melodic intervals in any voice (especially A2 in minor keys).
  • C — Crossed voices: Alto must not go above soprano or below tenor. No voice crossings.
  • E — Even distribution: Keep voices in their comfortable ranges. Soprano: C4-G5, Alto: G3-D5, Tenor: C3-G4, Bass: E2-D4.
  • D — Doubling: In root position: double the root. In first inversion: double soprano note (usually). NEVER double the leading tone (scale degree 7̂).

🧠 Week-Before Game Plan

  • 7 days out: Daily ear training: 20 min interval recognition + 20 min chord quality identification. Use apps like Teoria.com or musictheory.net for free practice.
  • 5 days out: Complete one full part-writing exercise daily. Practice writing from both figured bass AND Roman numerals. Time yourself (10 min max per exercise).
  • 3 days out: Full practice exam under timed conditions. Focus on your weakest area (usually dictation or part-writing). Practice sight-singing 3-4 melodies daily.
  • 1 day before: Light review: Circle of Fifths, interval song references, SPACED checklist. Sing through a few sight-singing examples at a slow tempo. Get 8+ hours sleep — your ear is sharper when rested.
  • Exam morning: Warm up your ear: listen to a Bach chorale (e.g., BWV 271) and try to identify the Roman numerals. Sing a major scale and a harmonic minor scale. This 5-minute warmup dramatically improves aural section performance.

🏆 Score-Maximizing Tactics

  • Dictation survival: If you lose the melody mid-playback, DON'T panic. Write what you have, leave blanks, and fill in on the next hearing. Partial credit for correct pitches/rhythms adds up. Getting the first note, last note, and rhythm correct can earn 60%+ of the points even if the middle is incomplete.
  • Part-writing partial credit: Even if you make voice-leading errors, having the correct chords (right notes in each chord) earns significant points. Write all four voices for every chord, even if you're unsure about the voice leading. Never leave a chord blank.
  • MCQ elimination for chord questions: If you can identify just the ROOT and QUALITY (major/minor/diminished), you can eliminate 2-3 wrong answers. You don't always need to identify the exact inversion.
  • Sight-singing confidence: Start on pitch and maintain steady tempo. Rushing is the #1 mistake. If you make a wrong note, keep going — the rhythm accuracy is scored separately from pitch accuracy. A confident, rhythmically accurate performance with a few wrong pitches scores higher than a hesitant, rhythmically uncertain performance.
  • The cadence shortcut: In harmonic dictation, identify the cadence type FIRST (PAC, HC, etc.). This tells you the last 2 chords immediately and anchors your analysis backward through the progression.

🔬 Physics-Major Exam Edge

Your understanding of wave physics is a genuine advantage. When you hear intervals, think frequency ratios: a perfect fifth is 3:2, a perfect fourth is 4:3, a major third is 5:4. Consonance = simple ratios, dissonance = complex ratios. This physical understanding makes interval recognition more intuitive than rote memorization. For the harmonic series question that often appears on the MCQ section, you can derive the answer from physics (fₙ = n·f₁) rather than memorizing it. The overtone series IS the harmonic series — you already know it from acoustics.
