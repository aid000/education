First Principles: Building from Ground Up

1.1 Scarcity & Opportunity Cost

📖 The Foundation of All Economics

Every economic problem stems from one fact: resources are limited, wants are unlimited. This creates scarcity.

Scarcity → Choices must be made → Every choice has a cost

Opportunity Cost = the value of the next-best alternative foregone

Example: If you choose to study for 2 hours, your opportunity cost is the movie you didn't watch, or the sleep you sacrificed.

1.2 GDP & Economic Indicators

💰 Three Approaches to Measuring GDP

GDP = C + I + G + NX

Expenditure Approach (most commonly used)

Component Definition Example
C (Consumption) Household spending on final goods/services Buying a laptop, haircut, pizza
I (Investment) Business spending on capital goods Factory equipment, new buildings
G (Government) Government purchases (not transfers) Military, schools, roads
NX (Net Exports) Exports minus imports Apple sells iPhones abroad minus US imports

Real vs. Nominal GDP:

Nominal GDP = current prices (can be misleading due to inflation)

Real GDP = adjusted for inflation (in base-year dollars) ← USE THIS FOR COMPARISON

1.3 Unemployment & Inflation

📊 Types of Unemployment
Type Definition Cause
Frictional Between jobs (job searching) Normal labor market churn
Structural Skills don't match available jobs Technological change, industry decline
Cyclical Due to economic downturns Recession, AD falls

Unemployment Rate = (Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100

Natural Rate = Frictional + Structural

1.4 The AD/AS Model

📊 The Core Framework of Macroeconomics

Why does AD slope downward? THREE effects:

  • WEALTH EFFECT: Higher P → Real wealth falls → Consumption ↓
  • INTEREST RATE EFFECT: Higher P → Money demand ↑ → Interest rates ↑ → Investment ↓
  • EXCHANGE RATE EFFECT: Higher P → Exports less competitive → NX ↓

Short-Run vs. Long-Run AS:

Feature Short-Run AS (SRAS) Long-Run AS (LRAS)
Shape Upward sloping Vertical (at full employment output)
Wages Fixed/sticky Flexible (adjusted)
Time Horizon 0-2 years 2+ years (economy adjusts)

1.5 Fiscal Policy: Government Spending & Taxes

💵 Multiplier Effects & Crowding Out

Key Formulas:

  • Spending Multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC)
  • Tax Multiplier = -MPC / (1 - MPC)
  • Balanced Budget Multiplier = 1

Example: If MPC = 0.75, then:

Spending Multiplier = 1/(1-0.75) = 4

→ $100B government spending → $400B increase in GDP

1.6 Monetary Policy: The Federal Reserve

🏦 Money Creation & Fed Operations
Money Multiplier = 1 / Reserve Ratio

The Fed's Three Tools:

Tool Action Effect on Money Supply
Open Market Operations (OMO) Buy/sell government securities Most precise; most commonly used
Discount Rate Interest rate banks pay to borrow from Fed Lower rate → banks borrow more → M↑
Reserve Requirements Percentage of deposits banks must hold Lower requirement → more lending → M↑

1.7 Money Market vs. Loanable Funds Market

🔄 The CRITICAL Distinction
Aspect Money Market Loanable Funds Market
What's traded? Money (M1, M2) Loans/Bonds (credit)
Price? Interest rate (nominal) Interest rate (real)
Supply shifts when... Fed changes money supply (OMO, etc.) National saving rate changes OR government budget changes
Demand shifts when... Real GDP or price level changes Profitability expectations change

1.8 Phillips Curve: Inflation-Unemployment Tradeoff

📉 Short-Run vs. Long-Run

Short-Run Phillips Curve: There IS a tradeoff. Lower unemployment requires accepting higher inflation.

Long-Run Phillips Curve: NO tradeoff. Vertical at the natural rate of unemployment. Inflation can be ANY level.

Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve:

Inflation = Expected Inflation + k(Y - Y*)/Y*

1.9 International Trade & Exchange Rates

🌍 Global Economics Essentials

Comparative Advantage: Countries should specialize in what they can produce at the lowest opportunity cost, then trade.

Event Effect on Dollar Why?
US interest rates rise $ appreciates (strengthens) Foreign investors want US assets
US inflation rises $ depreciates (weakens) US exports become less competitive
Expansionary fiscal policy $ appreciates (short-term) Crowding out pushes rates up

Exam Questions with Complete Solutions

🔵 Free Response Questions (FRQs)

FRQ #1: AD/AS & Fiscal Policy Chain

Assume the US economy is currently at long-run equilibrium with unemployment at 4% and inflation at 2%.

The government enacts expansionary fiscal policy by increasing government spending by $200 billion. Assume MPC = 0.80.

  1. Calculate the government spending multiplier and explain its meaning.
  2. Using an AD/AS diagram, show the short-run equilibrium. Will there be a recessionary gap or inflationary gap?
  3. Explain how crowding out limits the fiscal stimulus impact.
  4. In the long run, what happens to the short-run aggregate supply curve and why?
📖 Complete Solution
Rubric (10 points total):
• 1 pt: Correctly calculates multiplier = 5 (1/(1-0.80) = 1/0.2 = 5)
• 1 pt: Explains multiplier: $200B × 5 = $1 trillion eventual increase in AD
• 2 pts: Correctly identifies INFLATIONARY gap (eqm to right of LRAS) and draws proper AD/AS diagram
• 2 pts: Explains crowding out: ↑G → budget deficit → Gov borrows → Interest rates ↑ → I falls → reduces AD shift
• 2 pts: SRAS shifts left in long run (due to nominal wage increases); new LR equilibrium on LRAS with higher price level
• 2 pts: Recognizes that in LR, fiscal policy affects price level, not output

Detailed Answer: The spending multiplier = 5, meaning each $1 of government spending generates $5 in total GDP increase. So $200B spending → $1 trillion AD expansion. In the short run, this creates an inflationary gap because actual output exceeds potential output. However, crowding out reduces the effect: higher government borrowing drives up interest rates in the loanable funds market, which discourages private investment. In the long run, the inflationary gap causes workers to demand higher nominal wages. Firms agree due to labor shortage, so production costs rise. SRAS shifts left until the economy returns to Y*. The final equilibrium is at a higher price level but the same real output.

FRQ #2: Money Multiplier & Money Market

The Federal Reserve implements expansionary monetary policy by purchasing $10 billion in government securities through open market operations. The reserve requirement is 25%.

  1. Calculate the money multiplier.
  2. Calculate the maximum increase in the money supply.
  3. Using a money market diagram, show how this Fed action affects the equilibrium interest rate.
📖 Complete Solution
Rubric (5 points total):
• 1 pt: Correctly calculates money multiplier = 4 (1/0.25 = 4)
• 1 pt: Correctly calculates max M increase = $40 billion ($10B × 4)
• 3 pts: Correctly draws money market: vertical M supply shifts RIGHT, downward-sloping Md stays fixed → interest rate FALLS to new equilibrium

Detailed Answer: Money Multiplier = 1/0.25 = 4. Maximum M increase = 4 × $10B = $40B. In the money market diagram, the vertical money supply curve shifts rightward (more money in economy). The demand for money curve remains unchanged (based on nominal GDP and price level). At the old interest rate, there's excess money, so interest rates fall until people willingly hold the larger money supply.

FRQ #3: Exchange Rates & International Trade

Assume the United States increases interest rates significantly above the rest of the world.

  1. How does the increase in US interest rates affect the demand for US dollars in the foreign exchange market?
  2. What happens to the US dollar's exchange rate? Does it appreciate or depreciate?
  3. How will the change in the dollar's exchange value affect US exports and imports?
  4. What will happen to US net exports (NX) and aggregate demand?
📖 Complete Solution
Rubric (5 points total):
• 1 pt: Correctly identifies that demand for dollars INCREASES (higher returns on US assets)
• 1 pt: Correctly identifies dollar APPRECIATES (exchange rate rises)
• 1 pt: Explains exports DECREASE (less competitive) and imports INCREASE (cheaper)
• 2 pts: Concludes NX DECREASES → AD DECREASES (leftward shift)

Detailed Answer: Higher US interest rates attract foreign investors seeking returns. To buy US financial assets, they need US dollars, so demand for dollars increases. In the FOREX market, demand curve shifts right → dollar appreciates. When the dollar appreciates (becomes stronger), US goods become more expensive to foreigners (exports fall) while foreign goods become cheaper to Americans (imports rise). Net exports decrease, so AD = C + I + G + NX shifts left, reducing real output and potentially causing recession.

🔵 Multiple Choice Questions (Examples)

MC Question 1: GDP Calculation

In Year 1, a country's GDP components are: Consumer spending = $6 trillion, Business investment = $1.5 trillion, Government spending = $2 trillion, Exports = $1.2 trillion, and Imports = $0.8 trillion. What is GDP?

(A) $10.7 trillion
(B) $9.9 trillion
(C) $11.0 trillion
(D) $10.2 trillion
(E) $9.5 trillion
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (D) $10.2 trillion

GDP = C + I + G + NX = $6T + $1.5T + $2T + ($1.2T - $0.8T) = $6T + $1.5T + $2T + $0.4T = $10.2 TRILLION
MC Question 2: AD/AS Model - Recessionary Gap

The economy is in short-run equilibrium with real output of $8 trillion and potential output of $9 trillion. Which of the following is true?

(A) There is an inflationary gap and prices will fall
(B) There is a recessionary gap and unemployment is above the natural rate
(C) There is a recessionary gap and inflation will accelerate
(D) The SRAS curve is vertical
(E) The economy is at long-run equilibrium
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (B)

Since actual output ($8T) < potential output ($9T), there is a RECESSIONARY GAP. In a recessionary gap, real output is below potential, so there is cyclical unemployment. Unemployment rate is ABOVE the natural rate.

MC Question 3: Fiscal Policy Multiplier

If the marginal propensity to consume (MPC) is 0.6, what is the spending multiplier, and what is the total change in GDP if government spending increases by $100 billion?

(A) Multiplier = 2.5; ΔY = $250B
(B) Multiplier = 1.67; ΔY = $167B
(C) Multiplier = 2.5; ΔY = $250B
(D) Multiplier = 3; ΔY = $300B
(E) Multiplier = 1.5; ΔY = $150B
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (A) Multiplier = 2.5; ΔY = $250B

Spending Multiplier = 1 / (1 - 0.6) = 1 / 0.4 = 2.5 Total change in GDP = 2.5 × $100B = $250B
MC Question 4: Phillips Curve

If expected inflation INCREASES while the economy is at the natural rate, the short-run Phillips Curve (SRPC) will:

(A) Shift left
(B) Shift right/up
(C) Not change
(D) Become vertical
(E) Shift down
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (B) Shift right/up

The short-run Phillips Curve is given by: Inflation = Expected Inflation + k(U - U*)

When expected inflation increases, at any given unemployment rate, actual inflation will be higher. This means the entire SRPC shifts upward (or rightward, since higher unemployment is associated with higher inflation). At the natural rate of unemployment, inflation increases, representing an upward shift of the curve.

MC Question 5: Exchange Rates & Interest Rates

If the Federal Reserve RAISES interest rates, the US dollar will:

(A) Depreciate
(B) Appreciate
(C) Not change
(D) Depends on trade balance
(E) Initially appreciate then depreciate
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (B) Appreciate

Higher interest rates in the US make US financial assets (Treasury bonds, corporate bonds) more attractive to foreign investors. To purchase these assets, foreign investors need US dollars. The demand for dollars in the foreign exchange market increases, causing the dollar to appreciate (strengthen). This is a direct relationship: higher US rates → capital inflows → currency appreciates.

MC Question 6: Money Multiplier

A bank receives a deposit of $10,000. The required reserve ratio is 20%. What is the maximum amount of NEW money created in the banking system?

(A) $10,000
(B) $40,000
(C) $50,000
(D) $2,000
(E) $8,000
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (B) $40,000

Key distinction: The question asks for NEW money created, not total money.

Money Multiplier = 1 / Reserve Ratio = 1 / 0.20 = 5 Total money created = 5 × $10,000 = $50,000 NEW money created = Total - Initial Deposit = $50,000 - $10,000 = $40,000

The initial $10,000 is the deposit that starts the process. Banks can lend out 80% of deposits. Through multiple rounds of lending (money multiplier effect), a total of $50,000 enters the money supply. But $10,000 was already there, so the NEW money created by the banking system is $40,000.

FRQ #4: Phillips Curve & Long-Run Adjustment (5 points)

Assume the economy is initially at full employment with an inflation rate of 2% and an unemployment rate of 5%. An unexpected oil price shock occurs, causing production costs to rise suddenly.

  1. (a) Use an AD/AS diagram to show the short-run effect of the oil shock on the short-run aggregate supply curve (SRAS). Identify whether there is an inflationary or recessionary gap. (2 points)
  2. (b) What happens to unemployment and inflation in the short run following the oil shock? (1 point)
  3. (c) Explain how the economy returns to long-run equilibrium with NO government intervention. (2 points)
📖 Complete Solution
Rubric (5 points total):
• 2 pts (part a): Correctly draws AD/AS diagram showing SRAS shifting LEFT. Marks initial equilibrium on LRAS at Yf. Shows new short-run equilibrium to the LEFT of LRAS. Correctly identifies this as a RECESSIONARY GAP (output falls below Yf). (1 pt for diagram quality/labels, 1 pt for correct identification)
• 1 pt (part b): Correctly states that BOTH unemployment INCREASES and inflation INCREASES (stagflation). This is the hallmark of a supply shock.
• 2 pts (part c): Explains that high unemployment puts downward pressure on wages. Workers accept lower nominal wages due to unemployment. As wages fall, production costs fall for firms. SRAS shifts back to the RIGHT over time, returning output to Yf. The long-run equilibrium is restored at the original price level (or lower). Economy self-corrects without government action. (1 pt for wage mechanism, 1 pt for SRAS shift and return to Yf)

Detailed Answer:

(a) AD/AS Diagram: The oil shock is a negative supply shock. It increases production costs, shifting the SRAS curve to the LEFT. The new short-run equilibrium is at the intersection of the leftward-shifted SRAS and the unchanged AD curve. This new equilibrium has lower real output than the original equilibrium (which was at Yf on the LRAS). Since actual output is now below potential output, there is a RECESSIONARY GAP.

(b) Short-Run Effects: The recessionary gap creates stagflation — inflation increases (due to the leftward SRAS shift) while unemployment increases (due to lower output and reduced labor demand). This seems contradictory to the Phillips Curve, but it's actually an expectations-augmented Phillips Curve effect: inflation ↑ due to supply shock, unemployment ↑ due to output falling.

(c) Long-Run Self-Correction: The high unemployment rate (above the natural rate) puts downward pressure on wages. Workers, facing job scarcity, accept lower nominal wages and firms face less pressure to maintain high wages. As wages fall, production costs fall. Lower production costs shift the SRAS curve back to the RIGHT. The rightward shift of SRAS continues until the economy returns to long-run equilibrium at Yf (the natural rate of unemployment). The final equilibrium is at the intersection of AD, the original SRAS, and LRAS — back at full employment. No government action is needed; the market self-corrects through wage and price flexibility.

Top 10 Student Misconceptions (CORRECTED)

❌ Misconception #1: "When the Fed raises interest rates, investment ALWAYS decreases."

CORRECT: It depends on WHICH graph! In the Money Market, Fed actions directly shift money supply, changing interest rates. Higher rates → lower investment. But different markets show this differently.

❌ Misconception #2: "Currency appreciation is always GOOD for a country."

CORRECT: It's a tradeoff! Appreciation makes imports cheaper (consumers benefit) but exports less competitive (manufacturing jobs decline).

❌ Misconception #3: "Crowding out means there's no fiscal policy effect."

CORRECT: Crowding out REDUCES but doesn't eliminate the effect. Fiscal stimulus still works; just by less than the full multiplier.

❌ Misconception #4: "The natural rate of unemployment = 0%."

CORRECT: Natural rate ≈ 4-5% in most economies. Even at full employment, there's frictional unemployment (people between jobs) and structural unemployment (skill mismatches).

❌ Misconception #5: "Loanable Funds is just the Money Market with different names."

CORRECT: They're fundamentally different markets! Money Market is controlled by Fed actions. Loanable Funds is determined by saving/investment behavior.

❌ Misconception #6: "The multiplier works instantly"

WRONG: Students think a $100B spending increase immediately creates $500B in GDP.

CORRECT: The multiplier effect unfolds over multiple rounds of spending over months/years. Each round, some money leaks out through savings, taxes, and imports. The full multiplier effect (1/(1-MPC)) is the theoretical maximum that's never fully realized in practice due to time lags and leakages.

❌ Misconception #7: "Inflation is always bad"

WRONG: Students assume any inflation hurts the economy.

CORRECT: Moderate inflation (2-3%) is actually the Fed's TARGET. It encourages spending (why save if money loses value?), allows real wages to adjust, and gives the Fed room to cut rates in a recession. DEflation is actually much more dangerous — it causes consumers to delay purchases, creating a deflationary spiral (Japan's "Lost Decade").

❌ Misconception #8: "A trade deficit means the economy is weak"

WRONG: Students think importing more than exporting = economic failure.

CORRECT: Trade deficits often occur during economic GROWTH — when consumers have money to buy imports. The US has run trade deficits for decades during periods of strong growth. What matters is the CAPITAL ACCOUNT: a trade deficit means foreigners are investing in US assets (buying Treasury bonds, stocks, real estate).

❌ Misconception #9: "Printing money always causes hyperinflation"

WRONG: Students think any increase in money supply leads to runaway inflation.

CORRECT: The Quantity Theory (MV=PY) shows that if velocity (V) falls or output (Y) rises, money supply (M) can increase without proportional price increases. During recessions, velocity drops sharply — the Fed can expand M significantly with minimal inflation (as seen in 2008-2015 QE). Hyperinflation requires sustained, massive money creation with no output growth.

❌ Misconception #10: "Government debt works like household debt"

WRONG: Students apply household budget logic to federal government.

CORRECT: The federal government can issue its own currency, roll over debt indefinitely, and borrow at the lowest rates in the economy. The relevant metric isn't total debt but DEBT-TO-GDP RATIO and the interest burden relative to GDP. Japan's debt exceeds 250% of GDP without default. What matters is whether borrowing finances productive investments that grow GDP.

The 5 Hardest Parts Explained

Part 1: Money Market vs. Loanable Funds

💭 Side-by-side explanation

💰 MONEY MARKET = "The Fed's Control"

  • Fed OWNS the supply of money (they print it!)
  • If Fed supplies MORE money → interest rate falls
  • ONLY Fed actions shift the supply

🏦 LOANABLE FUNDS = "The Bank's Coordination"

  • Banks coordinate SAVERS and BORROWERS
  • If more people save → interest rates fall
  • If more businesses want to borrow → interest rates rise

When to use which: Fed actions (OMO, discount rate) → Money Market. National saving or investment decisions → Loanable Funds. Short-term rates → Money Market. Long-term rates → Loanable Funds.

Part 2: Complete Chain of Expansionary Monetary Policy

📊 Step-by-step from Fed action to effect
  1. Fed buys securities (OMO) → Increases monetary base
  2. Banks receive more reserves → Money multiplier effect → Total M increases
  3. In Money Market: M ↑ → Interest rates fall to equilibrate
  4. Lower interest rates → Businesses invest more, Consumers spend more → I ↑, C ↑
  5. AD = C + I + G + NX → AD shifts RIGHT
  6. SR equilibrium moves right and up the SRAS → Y ↑, P ↑, U ↓
  7. Exchange rate effect: Lower rates → $ depreciates → NX may decrease (ambiguous)
  8. Long run: If Y was below Y*, expansion is good. If Y was at Y*, only prices rise.

Full chain: Fed action → M ↑ → rates ↓ → I ↑, C ↑ → AD ↑ → Y ↑, P ↑, U ↓

Part 3: Crowding Out Effect

📉 Why fiscal stimulus doesn't work as well as expected

Mechanism: Government needs to borrow to finance deficit → In loanable funds market: Demand for funds increases → But Supply of loanable funds doesn't change (same saving rate) → Result: Equilibrium interest rate RISES → Higher rates make borrowing expensive for businesses → Business investment FALLS (despite stimulus) → Net effect: ↑G is offset partially by ↓I.

Why this matters: Reduces fiscal multiplier effectiveness. If interest rates go way up, might even have NEGATIVE investment effect. In open economy, also affects exchange rates ($ strengthens, exports fall).

Part 4: Exchange Rate Effects

🌍 How policy changes affect the FOREX market

Example: US cuts taxes (expansionary fiscal policy)

📊 SHORT-RUN: Interest rate effect dominates

Tax cut → Fiscal deficit → Gov borrows more → Interest rates RISE
Higher rates attract foreign investors → Demand for $ increases → $ APPRECIATES
Exports fall, imports rise → NX decreases more → Crowding out is magnified in open economy!

📈 LONG-RUN: Income effect dominates

Income ↑ → Imports ↑ → Trade deficit widens → $ eventually depreciates

Part 5: Balanced Budget Multiplier = Exactly 1

🧮 Why it works out to 1

The Setup: Government increases spending by $100B AND increases taxes by $100B.

🧮 The Balanced Budget Multiplier Formula

Balanced Budget Multiplier = Spending Multiplier + Tax Multiplier
= 1/(1-MPC) - MPC/(1-MPC)
= [1 - MPC] / (1-MPC)
= 1

💡 Intuitive Explanation

  • When G ↑ by $100B (multiplier 5 if MPC=0.8) → $500B increase
  • When T ↑ by $100B → households lose $100B, cut consumption by $80B → $400B decrease via multiplier
  • NET: $500B − $400B = $100B
  • The initial increase in spending is NOT multiplied!

Final Exam Strategy & Advice

⏱️ Time Management Blueprint

Section I — 60 MCQs in 70 min (~1.1 min each): This is tight. Don't get bogged down on calculation questions — flag them and come back. Eliminate 2 wrong answers first, then choose. Graph-reading questions are usually fastest — do those first.

FRQ 1 — Long Question (25 min suggested): This is typically 5-6 parts covering AD/AS + policy + secondary effects. Draw your graphs FIRST and label everything (axes, curves, equilibrium points). Each correctly labeled graph element is a separate point.

FRQ 2 — Short Question (12.5 min): Usually covers banking/money creation OR Phillips Curve. SHOW YOUR WORK on calculations — partial credit is available. Write the money multiplier formula before plugging in numbers.

FRQ 3 — Short Question (12.5 min): Often covers international trade/exchange rates. Trace the COMPLETE chain: policy → interest rates → capital flows → exchange rate → net exports. Miss one link and you lose points.

📊 Graph-Drawing Checklist (Worth 40%+ of FRQ Points)

  • ALWAYS label axes: "Price Level" (or "PL") on Y-axis, "Real GDP" (or "RGDP") on X-axis for AD/AS. "Nominal Interest Rate" on Y-axis, "Quantity of Money" on X-axis for Money Market.
  • ALWAYS label curves: AD, SRAS, LRAS. If you shift a curve, label BOTH the original (AD₁) and new position (AD₂).
  • ALWAYS mark equilibrium: Show the intersection point, and draw dotted lines to both axes showing the equilibrium price level and output.
  • Use arrows for shifts: Draw an arrow on the curve showing the direction of shift. This eliminates ambiguity.
  • The LRAS is always vertical: At full-employment output (Yf). If you draw LRAS with any slope, you lose that point automatically.

🧠 Week-Before Game Plan

  • 7 days out: Draw every graph from memory: AD/AS, Money Market, Loanable Funds, Phillips Curve, Foreign Exchange. Do this daily until exam day.
  • 5 days out: Practice 2 full FRQs per day from released AP exams (College Board posts them). Grade yourself using the official rubrics.
  • 3 days out: Full practice exam under timed conditions. Review the complete monetary policy chain and fiscal policy chain end-to-end.
  • 1 day before: Review all multiplier formulas, the Money Market vs. Loanable Funds distinction, and the MR. DIME mnemonic. Light review only.
  • Exam morning: Draw one quick AD/AS graph, one Money Market graph, and one Phillips Curve from memory. This activates your "economics brain." Eat protein, not sugar.

🏆 Score-Maximizing Tactics

  • The "Define-Draw-Explain" method: For every FRQ part: (1) define the concept, (2) draw/reference the graph, (3) explain the mechanism. This triple approach maximizes partial credit.
  • Chain of reasoning earns full credit: "The Fed buys bonds → money supply increases → interest rates fall → investment increases → AD shifts right → real GDP increases and price level rises." Each arrow is a potential point. Don't skip steps.
  • Common graph mistakes that lose points: Drawing LRAS with a slope. Forgetting to label the new equilibrium. Shifting the wrong curve. Not showing the direction of change on axes.
  • MCQ elimination strategy: On any question about policy effects, mentally trace the chain. If a choice skips a step in the chain (e.g., "Fed buys bonds → prices rise" without the intermediate steps), it's wrong.
  • Never leave anything blank: No penalty for wrong MCQ answers. For FRQs, a partially correct graph or statement can earn 1-2 points that might push you to the next score level (3→4 or 4→5).

🔬 Physics-Major Exam Edge

If an FRQ involves technology/innovation, connect it to aggregate supply: technological advancement shifts LRAS right (increasing potential output) AND shifts SRAS right (reducing production costs). This is the rare "win-win" in macro — higher output AND lower prices. Use physics research as your example: GPS (from relativity), MRI (from nuclear physics), and the World Wide Web (from CERN) are all technology shocks that expanded the production possibilities frontier.
