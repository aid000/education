First Principles: Building E&M from Coulomb's Law

🔴 Electrostatics: Charge, Force & Field

The Fundamental Idea: Charges interact through electric fields. Rather than "action at a distance," we say charges create fields, and fields exert forces on other charges.

Coulomb's Law: The Foundation

Two point charges exert forces on each other. The force on charge q₂ due to q₁:

F = k·|q₁·q₂|/r² (magnitude) where k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C² (Coulomb's constant) or k = 1/(4πε₀) with ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m

Key Properties:

  • Inverse square law: Force decreases with distance squared
  • Superposition: Total force is vector sum of all individual forces
  • Same sign → repulsion; opposite sign → attraction
  • Direction: along the line connecting charges (radial)

Vector Form (most useful):

F⃗₁₂ = k·q₁·q₂/r² · r̂ where r̂ is unit vector pointing from q₁ to q₂
Coulomb's Law is analogous to Newton's gravitational force law (inverse square), but electromagnetic forces are much stronger and can be attractive or repulsive.
Electric Field: Force Per Unit Charge

Definition: The electric field E⃗ at a point is the force per unit positive test charge:

E⃗ = F⃗/q_test

For a point charge q:

E = k·|q|/r² (magnitude) Direction: radially outward for +q, inward for -q

Why This Matters:

  • E⃗ is a field—it has a value at every point in space
  • It's independent of the test charge (doesn't depend on q_test)
  • Superposition applies: E⃗_total = Σ E⃗ᵢ from all charges
  • Units: N/C or V/m

Field Lines: Visual representation where:

  • Density of lines ∝ field strength
  • Tangent direction = field direction
  • Start on + charges, end on − charges
The concept of field was revolutionary—rather than action at a distance, charges modify spacetime around them. This directly led to Maxwell's equations and modern field theory.
Gauss's Law: The Most Powerful Tool

Statement:

∮ E⃗·dA⃗ = Q_enc/ε₀ (flux through closed surface = enclosed charge / permittivity)

Why It's Powerful:

  • One of Maxwell's equations
  • For symmetric charge distributions, it directly gives E without integration
  • Left side: electric flux = ∮ E⃗·dA⃗ = E·A·cos(θ) for uniform field
  • Right side: depends only on enclosed charge (surface shape doesn't matter!)

Key Insight: Choosing the Gaussian Surface

To use Gauss's Law effectively, choose a surface where:

  • E is perpendicular to the surface (E⃗·dA⃗ = E·dA)
  • OR E is parallel to the surface (E⃗·dA⃗ = 0)
  • E is constant over parts of the surface

Example: Infinite Line Charge

For a line charge with linear density λ (C/m), find E at distance r:

Choose a cylindrical Gaussian surface:

- Curved surface: E⊥ to surface, contributes ∮ E dA = E·2πrL - End caps: E∥ to surface, contributes 0 - Enclosed charge: Q_enc = λL Therefore: E·2πrL = λL/ε₀ E = λ/(2πε₀r)

Example: Infinite Plane Sheet

For a uniform surface charge density σ (C/m²), find E:

Choose a cylindrical Gaussian surface perpendicular to the sheet:

- Two end caps (parallel to sheet): E⊥ to surface, each contributes E·A - Curved side: E∥ to surface, contributes 0 - Enclosed charge: Q_enc = σ·A Therefore: 2E·A = σA/ε₀ E = σ/(2ε₀) (remarkably independent of distance!)
Gauss's Law shows that electric fields depend only on the enclosed charge, not on how that charge is distributed within the surface. This is crucial for understanding conductors and capacitors.
Electric Potential: Energy Per Unit Charge

Definition: Electric potential V is the work per unit positive charge to bring the charge from infinity to that point:

V = U/q_test = W_to_point / q_test (Units: Volts = Joules/Coulomb)

Relationship to Electric Field:

V = -∫ E⃗·dr⃗ (from ∞ to point of interest) Conversely: E⃗ = -∇V = -dV/dr (in one dimension)

For a point charge q:

V = k·q/r (positive for + charge, negative for − charge)

Superposition applies: V_total = Σ V from each charge

Equipotential Surfaces:

  • Surfaces where V is constant
  • E⃗ is always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces
  • No work done moving along an equipotential (W = q·ΔV = 0)

Why Potential Matters:

  • Scalar quantity (easier to work with than vector E)
  • Directly relates to energy: U = q·V
  • Potential difference ΔV drives current in circuits
❌ "Electric field and electric force are the same thing"

The Difference: Electric field E⃗ is a property of space (force per unit charge). Electric force F⃗ is what a charge experiences in that field.

F⃗ = q·E⃗ — The force depends on BOTH the field AND the charge experiencing it.

Analogy: Temperature gradient (like E field) causes heat flow. Heat flow (like F) depends on the thermal properties of the material.

📦 Conductors, Capacitors & Dielectrics

Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

Key Property: In electrostatic equilibrium (no charges moving):

  • E⃗ = 0 inside the conductor
  • Charges exist ONLY on the surface
  • E⃗ is perpendicular to the surface just outside
  • Entire conductor is at same potential
  • Excess charge distributes to maximize repulsion (more dense at sharp points)

Why E = 0 Inside?

If E ≠ 0, charges would move (contradiction). So mobile charges redistribute until E = 0. Surface charge creates the field that cancels any external field inside.

E Just Outside Surface:

E = σ/ε₀ where σ is surface charge density at that point

This is twice the field from an infinite plane (one side only touches the conductor).

Capacitance and Stored Energy

Definition: Capacitance is the ratio of charge to potential difference:

C = Q/V (Units: Farads = Coulombs/Volt)

For a Parallel Plate Capacitor:

C = ε₀·A/d where A = plate area, d = plate separation

With Dielectric (κ = relative permittivity):

C = κ·ε₀·A/d = κ·C₀

Energy Stored in Capacitor:

Work done to charge the capacitor:

U = (1/2)·Q·V = (1/2)·C·V² = Q²/(2C) (energy in joules)

Energy Density: Energy per unit volume in the field:

u = (1/2)·ε₀·E²
Capacitors are crucial in circuits (filtering, timing), power supplies, and energy storage systems. Supercapacitors in hybrid vehicles store massive energy in small volumes.
Dielectrics: Polarization & K Factor

What Happens When Dielectric Is Inserted:

  • External field polarizes molecules (aligns dipoles)
  • Polarization creates opposing field
  • Net field inside is reduced: E = E₀/κ
  • κ (kappa) = dielectric constant (κ ≥ 1 always)

Effect on Capacitor:

  • Capacitance increases: C = κ·C₀
  • For fixed charge: V decreases (V = Q/C)
  • For fixed voltage: Q increases (Q = C·V)
  • Stored energy increases (allows more charge storage)

Common Dielectric Constants:

  • Vacuum: κ = 1.0
  • Air: κ ≈ 1.0006 ≈ 1
  • Paper: κ ≈ 3.7
  • Water: κ ≈ 80 (very high!)
  • Ceramic: κ can be 10,000+

⚡ Electric Circuits: Current & Resistance

Current, Resistance & Ohm's Law

Electric Current: Charge flow rate

I = dQ/dt (Units: Amperes = Coulombs/second) Convention: Current flows in direction opposite to electron flow

Resistance: Opposition to current flow

R = ρ·L/A ρ = resistivity (material property) L = length, A = cross-sectional area

Ohm's Law:

V = I·R (Potential difference = current × resistance)

Or equivalently: I = V/R (more current for larger V or smaller R)

Power Dissipated:

P = I·V = I²·R = V²/R (Power in Watts = energy per unit time)

Resistors convert electrical energy to heat (Joule heating).

Kirchhoff's Laws: Analyzing Complex Circuits

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL):

Sum of currents entering a junction = sum leaving:

Σ I_in = Σ I_out

This follows from conservation of charge.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL):

Sum of potential changes around any closed loop = 0:

Σ ΔV_around_loop = 0

Sign Conventions:

  • Battery/EMF (ε): +ε going + to − terminal (energy source)
  • Resistor: −I·R in direction of current (energy loss)

Example: Series Circuit

One path for current. Same I through all components.

  • V_total = V₁ + V₂ + ... (voltages add)
  • R_total = R₁ + R₂ + ... (resistances add)

Example: Parallel Circuit

Multiple paths for current. Same V across all branches.

  • I_total = I₁ + I₂ + ... (currents add)
  • 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ... (reciprocals add)
RC Circuits: Charging and Discharging

RC Charging (Starting from Uncharged):

Closing switch connects capacitor to battery with resistance:

Q(t) = Q_max(1 - e^(-t/τ)) I(t) = I₀·e^(-t/τ) V_C(t) = V_max(1 - e^(-t/τ)) where τ = RC = time constant (seconds)

Physical Interpretation:

  • At t=0: Q=0, I=V/R (maximum)
  • At t=τ: Q ≈ 0.63·Q_max (63% charged)
  • At t=5τ: Q ≈ 0.99·Q_max (essentially full)

RC Discharging:

Q(t) = Q₀·e^(-t/τ) I(t) = -I₀·e^(-t/τ) V_C(t) = V₀·e^(-t/τ)

Why Time Constant Matters:

  • Larger τ (larger R or C): slower charging/discharging
  • Smaller τ: faster response
  • Used in timing circuits, filters
RC circuits model nerve impulse propagation, muscle cell firing, and are essential in timing circuits for computers and electronics.

🧲 Magnetic Fields & Lorentz Force

Magnetic Force on Moving Charges

Lorentz Force: Combined electric and magnetic force on a charge:

F⃗ = q(E⃗ + v⃗ × B⃗) The magnetic part depends on velocity!

Purely Magnetic Force:

F⃗_B = q·v⃗ × B⃗ Magnitude: F = q·v·B·sin(θ) where θ = angle between v and B

Key Properties of Magnetic Force:

  • Perpendicular to both velocity and field (right-hand rule)
  • NO work done on the charge (F⊥v always, so W=0)
  • Changes direction of motion, NOT speed
  • Only acts on moving charges

Right-Hand Rule for v × B:

  • Point fingers in direction of v
  • Curl toward B
  • Thumb points in direction of F (for positive charge)
  • For negative charge: reverse direction
The Lorentz force causes auroras (charged solar wind in Earth's magnetic field), particle acceleration in accelerators, and confinement in fusion reactors.
Circular Motion in Magnetic Field

Setup: Charge enters perpendicular to uniform magnetic field

Magnetic force provides centripetal force:

q·v·B = m·v²/r Solving for radius: r = m·v/(q·B)

Period of Circular Motion (Cyclotron Frequency):

T = 2πr/v = 2π·m/(q·B) f = 1/T = q·B/(2πm) (cyclotron frequency)

Remarkable Fact: Period is independent of velocity! A faster particle has larger radius but same period.

Applications:

  • Cyclotron: Particles spiral outward as they gain energy
  • Mass spectrometer: Different m/q ratios have different radii → separation
  • Particle traps: Confine charged particles with magnetic fields
Magnetic Force on Current-Carrying Wire

Force on Wire: A current-carrying wire experiences force in magnetic field:

F⃗ = I·L⃗ × B⃗ where L⃗ = vector pointing along wire with magnitude = length

Magnitude:

F = I·L·B·sin(θ)

Applications:

  • Electric motors: Coil in magnetic field experiences torque
  • Force between parallel wires: Currents in same direction attract, opposite repel

Force Between Parallel Wires:

F/L = μ₀·I₁·I₂/(2πd) μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A (permeability of free space) d = distance between wires
Sources of Magnetic Field: Biot-Savart & Ampère's Law

Biot-Savart Law: Magnetic field from current element:

dB⃗ = (μ₀/4π) · (I·dl⃗ × r̂)/r² dl⃗ = current element, r̂ = unit vector from current to field point

Integrate over entire current path to get total B.

Magnetic Field from Straight Wire:

B = μ₀·I/(2πr) (at perpendicular distance r from wire)

Direction: Right-hand rule—thumb along current, fingers curl around.

Ampère's Law (Most Useful for Symmetric Cases):

∮ B⃗·dl⃗ = μ₀·I_enc (circulation of B around closed path = μ₀ × enclosed current)

Magnetic Field from Solenoid:

For n turns per unit length:

B = μ₀·n·I (inside) B ≈ 0 (outside)

Direction: Right-hand rule with fingers through coil, thumb points to field direction.

Solenoids generate the magnetic fields in MRI machines (superconducting solenoids produce ~1.5-3 Tesla fields), particle accelerators, and electromagnets for industrial applications.

🌀 Electromagnetism: Faraday's Law & Maxwell's Equations

Faraday's Law: Changing Magnetic Flux Creates Electric Field

Key Discovery: A changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an EMF:

ε = -dΦ_B/dt where Φ_B = B·A·cos(θ) (magnetic flux through loop)

Induced EMF:

  • Proportional to rate of flux change
  • Negative sign indicates Lenz's Law (opposing the change)
  • Creates induced current: I = ε/R

Induced Electric Field (Without Resistance):

∮ E⃗·dl⃗ = -dΦ_B/dt (This is Faraday's Law in integral form—one of Maxwell's equations!)

Applications:

  • Transformers: AC through primary coil induces current in secondary
  • Generators: Rotating coil in magnetic field generates AC
  • Induction cooktops: Changing current creates changing flux → induced heat
Faraday's Law is central to ALL power generation: hydroelectric, fossil fuel, and wind power all work by rotating conductors in magnetic fields to induce EMF.
Lenz's Law: The Direction of Induced Effects

Statement: The induced current (or induced field) opposes the change in magnetic flux.

How to Apply:

  1. Determine if magnetic flux through loop is increasing or decreasing
  2. If increasing: induced field should oppose (point opposite to B)
  3. If decreasing: induced field should try to sustain (point same direction as B)
  4. Use right-hand rule to find induced current direction

Example: Pushing a magnet toward coil:

  • Flux through coil increasing
  • Induced field opposes: repels the magnet
  • You feel resistance pushing the magnet in

Why This Matters:

  • Explains energy conservation (opposing change costs energy)
  • Determines signs in equations
  • Crucial for transformer and generator design
❌ "Lenz's Law tells you the magnitude of induced current"
Lenz's Law only tells you the DIRECTION. The MAGNITUDE comes from Faraday's Law (ε = |dΦ/dt|) and Ohm's Law (I = ε/R). Lenz's Law is qualitative; Faraday's Law is quantitative.
Maxwell's Equations: Unifying E, B, & Light

The Four Equations (Integral Form):

1. Gauss's Law (Electric):

∮ E⃗·dA⃗ = Q_enc/ε₀ (Electric charges create E fields)

2. Gauss's Law for Magnetism:

∮ B⃗·dA⃗ = 0 (No magnetic monopoles—B field lines are closed loops)

3. Faraday's Law:

∮ E⃗·dl⃗ = -dΦ_B/dt (Changing magnetic flux creates electric field)

4. Ampère-Maxwell Law:

∮ B⃗·dl⃗ = μ₀·I_enc + μ₀·ε₀·dΦ_E/dt (Currents and changing electric flux create magnetic fields)

The Profound Consequence: Maxwell showed that E and B can support each other! A changing E field creates B, which creates E, etc. → Electromagnetic waves (light!).

Wave speed from Maxwell's equations:

c = 1/√(μ₀·ε₀) ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s (Speed of light!)
Maxwell's equations predicted electromagnetic waves decades before experimental confirmation. This was a triumph of theoretical physics and unified E&M into a single framework. Modern wireless communication, radar, and all electromagnetic phenomena follow from these four equations.

Free Response Question 1: Gauss's Law & Symmetry

Question: A uniformly charged sphere of radius R has total charge Q. Find the electric field at:

(a) A distance r > R from the center (outside)

(b) A distance r < R from the center (inside), assuming the charge is uniformly distributed throughout the volume

(c) What is the potential difference between the surface and the center?

📖 Complete Solution

(a) Outside the sphere (r > R):

Use Gauss's Law with spherical surface of radius r > R:

∮ E⃗·dA⃗ = Q_enc/ε₀ By symmetry, E is radial and constant on the surface: E·4πr² = Q/ε₀ E = Q/(4πε₀r²) = k·Q/r²

Same as point charge! All enclosed charge is "seen" as being at center.

(b) Inside the sphere (r < R):

Charge density: ρ = Q/(4πR³/3) = 3Q/(4πR³)

Enclosed charge in sphere of radius r:

Q_enc = ρ · (4πr³/3) = [3Q/(4πR³)] · (4πr³/3) = Q·(r/R)³

Apply Gauss's Law:

E·4πr² = Q·(r/R)³/ε₀ E = Q·r/(4πε₀R³) = (k·Q/R³)·r

Linear in r! At center: E = 0 (by symmetry)

(c) Potential Difference from surface to center:

Use V = -∫ E·dr (integrating from center to surface)

V_surface - V_center = -∫₀^R E(r)·dr = -∫₀^R (k·Q/R³)·r·dr = -(k·Q/R³) · [r²/2]₀^R = -(k·Q/R³) · (R²/2) = -k·Q/(2R)

Wait, this is the change. At surface: V_surface = kQ/R. At center: V_center = -(-kQ/2R) + kQ/R = 3kQ/(2R)

Actually: ΔV = V_surface - V_center = kQ/R - 3kQ/(2R) = -kQ/(2R)

So potential decreases from center outward (which makes sense for positive charge).

Free Response Question 2: RC Circuit Analysis

Question: A 10 μF capacitor initially uncharged is connected in series with a 1000 Ω resistor and a 12 V battery. Find:

(a) The time constant τ

(b) The initial current (at t = 0)

(c) The charge on the capacitor after time t = τ

(d) The current after time t = 3τ

📖 Complete Solution

(a) Time Constant:

τ = R·C = 1000 Ω × 10 × 10⁻⁶ F = 0.01 s = 10 ms

(b) Initial Current (t = 0):

At t=0, capacitor acts like short circuit (V_C = 0):

I₀ = V_battery/R = 12 V / 1000 Ω = 0.012 A = 12 mA

(c) Charge after τ:

Maximum charge: Q_max = C·V = 10×10⁻⁶ × 12 = 120 μC

Charging formula: Q(t) = Q_max(1 - e^(-t/τ))

Q(τ) = 120 μC × (1 - e^(-1)) = 120 μC × (1 - 0.368) = 120 μC × 0.632 = 75.8 μC ≈ 76 μC

Key point: After one time constant, capacitor is ~63% charged

(d) Current after 3τ:

I(t) = I₀·e^(-t/τ) I(3τ) = 12 mA × e^(-3) = 12 mA × 0.0498 = 0.60 mA

Current decays to ~5% of initial value.

Free Response Question 3: Faraday's Law with Moving Conductor

Question: A rectangular loop with length L, width w, and resistance R is pulled at velocity v out of a uniform magnetic field B (directed into the page). At time t, the loop has moved distance vt out of the field.

(a) Find the magnetic flux through the loop as a function of time

(b) Calculate the induced EMF

(c) Find the induced current and its direction (use Lenz's Law)

(d) What is the magnetic force on the loop, and what does it oppose?

📖 Complete Solution

(a) Magnetic Flux as Function of Time:

As loop exits, the area still in field decreases:

Area(t) = w × (L - vt) [until loop fully exits at t = L/v] Φ_B(t) = B × w × (L - vt) = Bw(L - vt)

(b) Induced EMF:

ε = -dΦ_B/dt = -d/dt[Bw(L - vt)] = -Bw·(-v) = B·w·v

Magnitude: ε = Bwv (independent of how far it's been pulled!)

(c) Induced Current:

I = ε/R = Bwv/R

Direction (Lenz's Law):

  • Flux into page is decreasing
  • Induced effect should try to maintain it
  • Induced current creates B field into page
  • By right-hand rule: current flows clockwise (viewed from above)

(d) Magnetic Force on Loop:

Only the right edge (length w) still in field experiences force:

F = I·w·B = (Bwv/R) × w × B = B²w²v/R Direction: By F⃗ = I·L⃗ × B⃗, force points LEFT (opposes motion!)

This force opposes the pulling! You must do work to pull it out. This work is dissipated as heat in the resistor.

This is how electromagnetic braking works—pulling a conductor out of a magnetic field requires work against the induced magnetic force. Eddy brakes in trains use this principle.

(c) 4pts: Current formula (2 pts), direction with Lenz's reasoning (2 pts)
(d) 3pts: Force magnitude, direction using right-hand rule, physical interpretation
2pts: Communication and reasoning

MC Question 2: Gauss's Law Surface Choice

A long straight wire carries uniform charge density λ (C/m). Which Gaussian surface most directly gives the electric field?

(A) Spherical surface centered on the wire
(B) Cubic surface with wire along one edge
(C) Cylindrical surface coaxial with the wire
(D) Tetrahedral surface
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (C) Cylindrical surface

Why: Gauss's Law is most easily applied when the Gaussian surface matches the symmetry of the charge distribution.

  • Long straight wire has cylindrical symmetry (rotational symmetry around wire axis)
  • E field is perpendicular to wire, radially outward, same magnitude at any given distance
  • Cylindrical surface with axis along wire: E⊥ to curved surface (contributes), E∥ to end caps (contributes 0)
  • Gives: ∮ E dA = E·2πrL, set equal to λL/ε₀, solve for E directly

Why others are wrong:

  • (A) Spherical: E is not constant over sphere, and E is not perpendicular everywhere—harder to apply
  • (B) Cubic: No symmetry alignment; flux calculation is messy
  • (D) Tetrahedral: Arbitrary shape, no symmetry advantage

Key Principle: Choose Gaussian surface that matches the symmetry of the charge distribution. Then E is either perpendicular or parallel to the surface, making flux calculation trivial.

MC Question 3: Capacitor with Dielectric

A parallel plate capacitor with air between the plates has capacitance C₀. A dielectric with κ=4 is inserted between the plates while the capacitor remains connected to a battery at constant voltage V. What happens to the energy stored?

(A) Decreases to U₀/4
(B) Stays the same
(C) Increases to 2U₀
(D) Increases to 4U₀
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (D) Increases to 4U₀

Setup: Voltage is constant (battery connected), so V = const.

Initially (air):

C₀ = ε₀A/d Q₀ = C₀·V U₀ = (1/2)C₀V²

With dielectric (κ=4):

C = κ·C₀ = 4C₀ Q = C·V = 4C₀·V (charge increases!) U = (1/2)C·V² = (1/2)·4C₀·V² = 4U₀

Why Energy Increases:

  • Battery does work to push more charge onto the capacitor plates
  • More charge at same voltage → more energy stored
  • Work by battery = ∫ V dQ = V·ΔQ = V·3C₀V = 3C₀V²
  • Energy increase = 3U₀ (matches ΔU = 4U₀ - U₀ = 3U₀) ✓

CONTRAST: If capacitor disconnected (constant Q):

  • U = Q²/(2C) = Q²/(2·4C₀) = U₀/4 (energy DECREASES!)
  • Different scenario—key is whether voltage or charge is held constant

Why other answers are wrong:

  • (A) U₀/4: This is if charge were constant (disconnected), not the case here
  • (B) Same: Energy depends on capacitance when V is constant: U = (1/2)CV²
  • (C) 2U₀: Would require κ=2, not κ=4
MC Question 4: Lorentz Force Direction

An electron (charge -e) moves to the right with speed v. A magnetic field B points into the page. What is the direction of the magnetic force on the electron?

(A) Upward
(B) Downward
(C) Into the page
(D) Out of the page
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (B) Downward

Method: Right-Hand Rule for F⃗ = q(v⃗ × B⃗)

Step 1: Find v⃗ × B⃗ (right-hand rule without charge first)

  • Point fingers right (direction of v⃗)
  • Curl toward into-page (direction of B⃗)
  • Thumb points UP
  • So v⃗ × B⃗ points UP

Step 2: Apply charge q = -e (negative!)

  • F⃗ = q(v⃗ × B⃗) = (-e)(UP) = DOWN
  • Negative charge reverses direction

Physical Interpretation: Cathode ray tube (CRT) televisions used this! Electrons move right, deflected downward by magnetic field, creating the scanned image on screen.

Why other answers are wrong:

  • (A) Upward: Would be correct for a positive charge; electrons are negative
  • (C) & (D): F must be perpendicular to v; can't be parallel to or into the page when v is right and B is into page

Key Principle: Always remember the negative charge sign! It's the most common error in magnetic force problems.

MC Question 5: Faraday's Law & Lenz

A circular loop is pulled downward out of a uniform magnetic field pointing upward. As the loop exits the field, what is the direction of the induced current (viewed from above)?

(A) Clockwise
(B) Counterclockwise
(C) Alternating
(D) Cannot be determined without more information
📖 Explanation

✓ Correct Answer: (A) Clockwise

Apply Lenz's Law:

  1. Initial flux through loop: Φ = B·A, pointing UP (out of page, viewed from above)
  2. As loop exits: Flux DECREASES
  3. Lenz's Law says induced effect opposes this change → try to maintain upward flux
  4. Induced current should create upward magnetic field
  5. Right-hand rule: fingers curl in direction of current, thumb points up → CLOCKWISE (viewed from above)

Alternative Verification (Motional EMF):

  • As loop moves down, free charges in the bottom edge experience force
  • Bottom edge moves through upward B field at downward velocity v
  • F⃗ = q(v⃗ × B⃗) for positive charge: v DOWN, B UP → v × B points to the RIGHT (using right-hand rule)
  • Positive charges pushed to right side of loop → right side becomes positive
  • Current flows from + to − externally → flows LEFT through the rest of the loop → CLOCKWISE overall

Why this matters: Lenz's Law predicts which way the induced current flows—crucial for generators, transformers, and understanding electromagnetic induction.

Top Misconceptions: Why E&M Trips You Up

❌ #1: "Electric field direction is the same as force direction"

The Confusion: E⃗ is always defined as the force per positive test charge: F⃗ = q·E⃗.

For negative charges: F⃗ = (-e)·E⃗ points OPPOSITE to E⃗.

Example: Near a negative point charge, E⃗ points inward (toward the charge). But a test electron experiences force OUTWARD (repulsion).

Fix: E⃗ field direction is defined for positive charge. For electrons, reverse it.

❌ #2: "You can use any Gaussian surface for Gauss's Law"

The Truth: Gauss's Law works for ANY closed surface, but it's USEFUL only for symmetric ones.

For Example: If you pick a random shape, the integral ∮ E⃗·dA⃗ is incredibly hard to compute—E varies in magnitude and direction all over the surface.

Why Symmetry Matters: Choose a surface where E is either perpendicular or parallel everywhere, and constant in magnitude over parts of it.

Examples of Good Choices:

  • Spherical symmetry (point charge) → use sphere
  • Cylindrical symmetry (wire) → use cylinder coaxial with wire
  • Planar symmetry (infinite sheet) → use cylinder perpendicular to sheet

Fix: Match the surface to the symmetry of the charge distribution. The integral becomes trivial.

❌ #3: "Magnetic force does work on moving charges"

The Reality: F⃗_B = q·v⃗ × B⃗ is ALWAYS perpendicular to v⃗ → F⃗·v⃗ = 0 → Work = 0.

Why? By definition of cross product, v⃗ × B⃗ is perpendicular to v⃗.

Consequence: Magnetic forces can only change the DIRECTION of motion, never the SPEED or KINETIC ENERGY.

Example: Electron in magnetic field moves in circle at constant speed. All the energy goes into changing direction, not accelerating.

Contrast with Electric Force: F⃗_E = q·E⃗ is often NOT perpendicular to v⃗ → can do work → can change kinetic energy.

Fix: Remember: Magnetic forces deflect; electric forces energize.

❌ #4: "RC time constant τ has units of seconds, so larger τ means... what?"

What τ Means: τ = R·C is the characteristic time scale of the RC circuit.

After time τ:

  • Charging: Q(τ) = 0.632·Q_max (63% charged)
  • Discharging: Q(τ) = 0.368·Q₀ (37% remaining)
  • Current: I(τ) = 0.368·I₀

Larger τ: SLOWER charging/discharging (slower response)

Smaller τ: FASTER response

Fix: τ is a time scale. Larger τ means the circuit is slower; smaller τ means faster.

❌ #5: "Lenz's Law tells you the magnitude of induced current"

What Lenz's Law Does: Tells you the DIRECTION of induced current (opposes the change).

What Faraday's Law Does: Tells you the MAGNITUDE of induced EMF: ε = |dΦ_B/dt|

Combined: Faraday gives magnitude, Lenz gives direction.

For Current: I = ε/R (Ohm's Law gives magnitude from EMF)

Fix: Lenz ≠ magnitude. Use Lenz for direction, Faraday for magnitude.

❌ #6: "Parallel plate capacitors always have uniform E field between plates"

True for: Infinitely large plates (or far from edges)

Reality: Near the edges of finite plates, E field is NOT uniform—field lines curve outward (fringing effect).

For AP Exams: Usually assume ideal parallel plates (uniform field), unless edge effects are specifically mentioned.

Why Edge Effects Matter: In precision applications (precision capacitors, high-voltage designs), fringing effects cause errors.

Fix: For ideal problems: uniform field. For real devices: edge effects exist but usually small.

❌ #7: "Potential V and potential energy U are the same thing"

The Difference:

  • Potential V: Energy per unit charge (J/C = Volts). Property of the field at a location.
  • Potential Energy U: Actual energy of a charge q in the field (U = q·V, in Joules).

Analogy: V is like "gravitational field strength" (g). U is like "gravitational potential energy" (mgh).

Key Point: Two charges at the same potential have different energies if they have different charges!

Fix: V is the field property; U = q·V is the charge's energy in that field.

The Hardest Parts: Master These & You're Set

1. Choosing the Right Gaussian Surface (Why It Matters)

The Challenge: Gauss's Law works for any closed surface, but is useful only with symmetric ones. Choosing poorly leads to integrals you can't solve.

Why It's Hard:

  • Students memorize three cases (sphere, cylinder, plane) but don't understand the principle
  • Unfamiliar geometries require creative thinking
  • Common error: wrong surface → E isn't constant over surface → integral becomes messy

The Principle: Match surface to symmetry of charge distribution. For charge distribution with symmetry axis/center, the Gaussian surface should have E perpendicular (or parallel) everywhere and constant over symmetric parts.

Examples:

  • Point charge: Spherical symmetry → use sphere centered on charge
  • Infinite line charge: Cylindrical symmetry → use cylinder coaxial with line
  • Infinite plane: Planar symmetry → use cylinder perpendicular to plane
🎯 Memory Anchor: "Symmetry = Strategy"

Look at the charge geometry. If it has a symmetry axis or center, your Gaussian surface should align with it. Let the charge symmetry dictate your surface choice.

2. RC Circuit Transients (Why Exponentials Are Non-Intuitive)

The Challenge: Charging/discharging follow exponentials, not linear time dependence. Students often think "50% voltage in half the time."

Why It's Hard:

  • Exponential functions grow/decay slowly at first, fast later (counterintuitive for charging where initial rate is fastest)
  • After 1τ: 63% done; After 2τ: 86% done; After 5τ: 99% done (asymptotic)
  • Students mix up Q(t), V(t), and I(t)—all have different time constants

The Physics: Current is limited by resistance R and decreases as capacitor voltage opposes battery. As capacitor charges, voltage difference decreases, so current decreases exponentially.

Key Formulas:

  • Q(t) = Q_max(1 - e^(-t/RC))
  • I(t) = (V/R)e^(-t/RC)
  • V_C(t) = V(1 - e^(-t/RC))
🎯 Memory Anchor: "63-36-5 Rule"

After 1τ: capacitor is 63% charged, current is 37% of initial, 63% of charge remains when discharging

After 5τ: effectively fully charged/discharged (99.3%)

This is almost universal for exponential processes!

3. Faraday's Law with Moving Conductors (Sign Conventions!)

The Challenge: When a conductor moves through a magnetic field, determining the induced EMF sign is error-prone. Lenz's Law is qualitative; Faraday is quantitative.

Why It's Hard:

  • Flux change from geometry is hard to visualize (area decreasing, angle changing, field changing)
  • Lenz's Law opposes change but doesn't directly give magnitude
  • Sign conventions: Is the EMF positive or negative? Does current direction matter?

The Method:

  1. Parametrize the flux: Φ_B(t) = B·A(t)·cos(θ(t))
  2. Find dΦ/dt: account for changing A, θ, and B
  3. ε = -dΦ/dt: negative sign means opposes change (Lenz)
  4. I = ε/R: current magnitude
  5. Direction: Use Lenz—induced field opposes flux change
🎯 Memory Anchor: "Parametrize First"

Write Φ(t) explicitly with time dependencies. Take dΦ/dt. The negative sign handles direction automatically (Lenz).

Magnitude: |ε| = |dΦ/dt|. Direction: Induced effect opposes.

4. Superposition of Fields (Vectors! Vectors! Vectors!)

The Challenge: E and B fields are vectors. Superposition means vector addition. Many students forget this and add magnitudes.

Why It's Hard:

  • It's easy to compute magnitudes but forget to account for directions
  • Vector addition requires components—more bookkeeping
  • Fields can partially cancel (destructive interference)
  • Symmetry can make net field zero even with multiple charges

The Method:

  1. Find E⃗ (or B⃗) from each source separately (magnitude and direction)
  2. Resolve into components (x, y, z)
  3. Add components: E⃗_total_x = Σ E_i_x, etc.
  4. Recombine: E⃗_total = (ΣE_x)x̂ + (ΣE_y)ŷ + (ΣE_z)ẑ
  5. Magnitude: |E⃗| = √(E_x² + E_y² + E_z²)
🎯 Memory Anchor: "Components → Vectors → Magnitude"

Never add magnitudes directly. Always decompose into components, add, then recombine.

Example: E₁ = 3N/C east, E₂ = 4N/C north → E_total = 5N/C northeast (not 7N/C!)

5. Energy Stored in Fields (Capacitor vs. Inductor vs. Resistor Dissipation)

The Challenge: Three different energy scenarios in circuits. Students mix them up.

Capacitor (stores energy):

  • U_C = (1/2)CV² = (1/2)Q²/C (energy stored, recoverable)

Resistor (dissipates energy as heat):

  • P = I²R = V²/R (power dissipated, lost)
  • Energy dissipated: W = ∫ P dt

Inductor (stores energy in magnetic field):

  • U_L = (1/2)LI² (energy stored, recoverable)
  • Note: Similar form to capacitor but with current instead of voltage

Key Differences:

  • Capacitor: U depends on V → charged by potential difference
  • Inductor: U depends on I → opposes current changes
  • Resistor: Dissipates power P (not energy storage) → heat
🎯 Memory Anchor: "C has V, L has I"

U_C = (1/2)CV² → energy depends on voltage across capacitor

U_L = (1/2)LI² → energy depends on current through inductor

Resistor R: dissipates P = I²R, no stored energy

Memory Anchors: Mnemonics & Tricks

⚡ Lenz's Law: "Nature Opposes Change"

When flux through a loop changes: The induced current creates a magnetic field that opposes that change. Increasing flux? Induced field points opposite. Decreasing flux? Induced field tries to sustain it.

Application: Push magnet toward coil → flux increases → induced field repels magnet (you feel resistance). Pull magnet away → flux decreases → induced field tries to pull it back.

🧲 Right-Hand Rule for Lorentz Force

F⃗ = q(v⃗ × B⃗)

Method:

  • Point fingers in direction of v⃗ (velocity)
  • Curl fingers toward B⃗ (magnetic field)
  • Thumb points to F⃗ for positive charge
  • For negative charge: reverse direction

Mnemonic: Fingers = velocity, curl = magnetic field, thumb = force. (For electrons, reverse!)

⚡ RC Time Constant: τ = RC

After time τ: 63% charged/discharged, 37% remaining.

After 5τ: Essentially complete (99.3%).

Larger R or C: Larger τ → slower response

Smaller R or C: Smaller τ → faster response

🔮 Magnetic Field from Current: Right-Hand Rule (Thumb-Along-Current)

For straight wire: Point thumb along current direction. Fingers curl around wire in direction of B field.

For solenoid: Curl fingers in direction of current loops. Thumb points in direction of B field inside solenoid.

⚡ Potential Difference vs. EMF

EMF (ε): Voltage provided by battery/generator (source)

Potential Difference (V): Voltage drop across resistor or component

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law: ε - Σ(IR) = 0 around a loop

🧲 Parallel Wires with Current

Currents in same direction: Wires ATTRACT (magnetic fields aligned)

Currents in opposite directions: Wires REPEL

Force between wires: F/L = μ₀I₁I₂/(2πd) where d = distance

⚡ Gauss's Law Surface Selection: Match Symmetry

Point charge: Spherical surface (radial symmetry)

Line charge: Cylindrical surface coaxial with line (axial symmetry)

Plane charge: Cylinder perpendicular to plane (planar symmetry)

Principle: Choose surface where E is constant in magnitude and perpendicular (or parallel) to surface.

⚡ Energy Stored in Capacitor

Three Equivalent Forms:

  • U = (1/2)CV² (useful when you know V)
  • U = (1/2)Q²/C (useful when you know Q)
  • U = (1/2)QV (useful when you know both Q and V)

Dielectric increases C by κ: C_new = κ·C_old. If voltage constant (connected to battery), energy increases by κ. If charge constant (disconnected), energy decreases by 1/κ.

🔮 Maxwell's Equations (Unified Picture)

1. Gauss (E): Electric charges create E fields

2. Gauss (B): No magnetic monopoles (B field lines are closed)

3. Faraday: Changing B creates E (induction)

4. Ampère-Maxwell: Currents and changing E create B

The Profound Consequence: E and B can support each other → electromagnetic waves at speed c

Exam Strategy: Time Management & FRQ Approach

📝 Multiple Choice (35 questions, 45 minutes)

  • Pace: ~1.3 minutes per question (leave buffer for hard ones)
  • Strategy: Skim all questions first. Do easy ones (Coulomb's Law, definitions) immediately.
  • Guess intelligently: If unsure, eliminate obviously wrong answers. Magnetic force must be perpendicular to velocity (eliminates wrong answers instantly).
  • Watch for tricks: E field direction vs. force direction (opposite for negative charges). Potential vs. potential energy.
  • Flag questionable answers: Skip hard ones first, come back if time.

✍️ Free Response (3 questions, 45 minutes)

Time per question: ~15 minutes (roughly 25 min if one is simpler)

Standard FRQ Structure (Example):

  • (a) 2-3 pts: Setup or derive formula
  • (b) 2-3 pts: Apply formula to specific scenario
  • (c) 2-3 pts: Further analysis or extension
  • (d) 1-2 pts: Interpretation or reasoning (often free points!)

Approach for Each FRQ:

  1. Read Fully: Understand all parts before writing anything
  2. Identify Physics: Is this Gauss's Law? Circuits? Induction? Label it.
  3. Set Up Equations: Write relevant formulas with variable names (not just plug numbers)
  4. Derive or Apply: Show all steps. Partial credit for method even if final answer is wrong.
  5. Substitute Numbers: Only after symbolic work is clean
  6. Units & Reasoning: Always include units. Explain your reasoning (especially in parts (c)-(d) asking "why" or "explain")

Common FRQ Topics:

  • Gauss's Law → finding E field
  • RC circuits → charging/discharging equations
  • Magnetic force → circular motion or force on wire
  • Faraday's Law → induced EMF and current
  • Capacitor networks → series/parallel combinations

⏱️ What NOT to Do

  • Don't rush numeric. Show symbolic work first. Numeric errors give partial credit; missing method gives zero.
  • Don't forget signs. Negative charges, Lenz's Law direction, induced EMF negative sign—these are easy points lost.
  • Don't skip parts (c)-(d). These often ask for reasoning ("explain why," "what does this mean"). These are frequently easier points.
  • Don't skip units. Final answer without units often loses 1 point.
  • Don't overwrite. If you make an error, cross it out neatly and restart. AP readers can see your final answer.

Last-Minute Tips:

  • For Gauss's Law: Draw the geometry clearly. Mark which surface you choose and why.
  • For circuits: Label all currents and voltages. Use Kirchhoff's Laws systematically.
  • For magnetic problems: Use right-hand rule every time. Write down directions (left, up, into page), not just magnitudes.
  • For Faraday's Law: Parametrize flux first as Φ(t), then take derivative. This avoids sign errors.

Physics Applications: E&M in the Real World

Particle Accelerators & CERN

How it works: Charged particles (electrons, protons) accelerate through electric fields, gaining kinetic energy. Magnetic fields bend their path into circular orbits. Faster particles → larger orbits.

E&M Principles:

  • Electric field accelerates particles: ΔE_kinetic = q·ΔV
  • Magnetic field provides centripetal force: r = mv/(qB)
  • Cyclotron frequency f = qB/(2πm) is independent of velocity

Real Application: Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN is 27 km in circumference. Protons accelerate to 99.9999% speed of light, then collide. E&M fields accelerate and guide them.

Understanding E&M at accelerators led to discovery of new particles and verification of the Standard Model. The Higgs boson was confirmed using this principle.
MRI Machines (Medical Imaging)

How it works: Strong magnetic field (1.5-3 Tesla, much stronger than Earth's ~50 μT) aligns nuclear spins. Radiofrequency (RF) pulses at Larmor frequency (proportional to B) excite the spins. Relaxation creates signals → image.

E&M Principles:

  • Superconduct electromagnets create field: B = μ₀nI (solenoid)
  • Lorentz force aligns spins with field
  • Larmor frequency: f = qB/(2πm) (same as cyclotron frequency!)
  • Induced EMF detects relaxation signals (Faraday's Law)

Why MRI? Non-invasive, no ionizing radiation (unlike X-rays). Maps water content → excellent soft tissue imaging.

MRI was developed by physicists and chemists applying NMR (Nuclear Magnetic Resonance) to medicine. It's a direct application of quantum mechanics (spin) combined with classical E&M.
Power Grid & Transformers

How it works: Power plants generate AC voltage. Transformers step it up for transmission (lower current → less power loss), step it down for home use.

E&M Principles:

  • AC current in primary coil creates changing B field
  • Changing flux induces EMF in secondary coil (Faraday's Law)
  • Turns ratio determines voltage: V₁/V₂ = N₁/N₂
  • Power conservation: V₁I₁ = V₂I₂ (ideal transformer)

Real Numbers: Power plant outputs ~25 kV. Stepped up to ~765 kV for transmission (current drops, power loss ∝ I²). Stepped down to 120V/240V for homes.

Power Loss in Transmission: P_loss = I²R. Higher voltage (lower current) means dramatically lower losses.

Efficient power transmission is why we use AC (easy to transform with inductors) rather than DC. Tesla vs. Edison's DC system—Tesla won because transformers made AC practical.
Auroras (Northern/Southern Lights)

How it works: Solar wind (charged particles from the Sun) interacts with Earth's magnetic field. Particles are deflected toward poles (where field converges), collide with atmosphere → light.

E&M Principles:

  • Lorentz force deflects charged particles: F⃗ = q·v⃗ × B⃗
  • Earth's magnetic field (~30 μT) guides particles toward poles
  • Particles collide with O₂, N₂ molecules → excited states → photons (light)

Why Poles? Earth's magnetic field lines converge at the poles, creating a "funnel" that guides incoming particles.

Aurora Color:

  • Green/red: Oxygen (O₂, O) excitation
  • Blue/purple: Nitrogen (N₂)
Auroras are a visible reminder that Earth is immersed in the solar wind and protected by its magnetic field. Without Earth's magnetosphere, solar wind would strip away our atmosphere!
Radio & Wireless Communication

How it works: Oscillating current in transmitting antenna creates time-varying E and B fields. These fields propagate as electromagnetic waves (light, radio, etc.). Receiving antenna detects the wave → signal.

E&M Principles:

  • Ampère-Maxwell Law: Changing E creates B
  • Faraday's Law: Changing B creates E
  • E and B support each other → propagating wave at speed c
  • Wave equation: ∂²E/∂t² = c²∇²E (derived from Maxwell's equations)

Frequency & Wavelength: c = fλ. Lower frequency → longer wavelength (better penetration through obstacles, used for radio AM). Higher frequency → shorter wavelength (more directional, used for WiFi, cell phones).

Maxwell predicted EM waves in 1865. Hertz experimentally confirmed in 1887. Within decades: radio (Marconi), then TV, then modern wireless. All rooted in four equations!
Tokamak Fusion Reactors

How it works: Plasma (ionized gas) at 100+ million Kelvin is magnetically confined in a toroidal (doughnut-shaped) chamber. Lorentz force holds ions in place while they fuse.

E&M Principles:

  • Magnetic field confines plasma: F⃗ = q·v⃗ × B⃗ provides centripetal force
  • Field topology (toroidal) prevents particles from escaping
  • RF heating: Lorentz force accelerates particles, increasing temperature

Challenge: Plasma is unstable (kink instabilities, tearing modes). Requires sophisticated B-field control and active feedback.

Goal: Net positive fusion energy gain (more energy out than in). ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) aims to achieve this.

Fusion is the power source of the Sun. Creating controlled fusion on Earth would provide unlimited clean energy. E&M confinement is one of the main approaches (alongside laser confinement).

Final Thoughts: Master These 5 Things

  1. Gauss's Law is your superpower: Master symmetric charge distributions. Know when to use which surface.
  2. Vector directions matter: E field, B field, forces—always specify direction. Use right-hand rules religiously.
  3. Lenz's Law + Faraday's Law: Direction from Lenz, magnitude from Faraday. They work together.
  4. Circuits are everywhere: RC charging, parallel/series combinations, Kirchhoff's Laws. Get comfortable with complex circuits.
  5. Maxwell unified everything: E and B fields support each other. This is why light exists and why wireless communication works.
