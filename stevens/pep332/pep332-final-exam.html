Exam Overview

What is PEP 332 About?

PEP 332 — Mathematical Methods for Physics (Prof. Vladimir Lukic) teaches the techniques physicists use to get answers out of equations when clean closed-form solutions are rare. It is a hands-on, computer-friendly tour of six big mathematical "toolkits," each motivated by a real physical problem.

The course has six parts:

  • Part 1 Intro — tricks of the trade: dimensional analysis, Gaussian integrals, geometric & infinite sums.
  • Part 2 Linear algebra in physical problems: real/complex vector spaces, Hilbert space, eigenvalue problems, orthogonal functions, Fourier series/transform.
  • Part 3 Gamma function & N-dimensional sphere: $\Gamma(x)$, Stirling's approximation, volumes/surfaces in arbitrary dimensions.
  • Part 4 Probability & statistics: binomial → Poisson → Gaussian limits, least-squares fitting, $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit and contingency tables.
  • Part 5 Complex functions: $z = \rho e^{i\varphi}$, Fourier transforms, complex representations of $\cos,\sin,\cosh,\sinh$.
  • Part 6 Asymptotics, perturbations, variations: regular & singular perturbation theory, Laplace's method, stationary phase, calculus of variations, Euler-Lagrange.

📋 Exam Format & Ground Rules

  • Weight: Final = 40% of course grade (HW = 60%).
  • Where: Held in-class. There is also (as in F2025) a take-home portion for the probability/statistics problems that require Excel.

📝 Typical Exam Questions (from 5 HWs + F2025 Final)

  • Evaluate a Gaussian-type integral using completing the square or Feynman's trick.
  • Use a geometric series to solve a bouncing-ball or random-walk (lamppost) problem.
  • Find normal modes of a 2- or 3-mass spring system → $2\times 2$ or $3\times 3$ eigenvalue problem (CO$_2$ triatomic!).
  • Gram-Schmidt orthogonalize $\{1,x,x^2,x^3\}$ on a given interval → get Legendre-like polynomials.
  • Fourier series of $f(x)=x^2$ on $[-\pi,\pi]$; evaluate sum $\sum 1/n^2$ as a byproduct.
  • Fourier transform of a Gaussian, a cosine, a product of two functions.
  • Volume/surface of an $n$-dimensional sphere; derive recursive formula with $\Gamma(n/2+1)$.
  • Regular perturbation: solve $x^2 - 2\varepsilon x - 1 = 0$ or $\dot y = x^3 + \varepsilon y$ to O($\varepsilon^2$).
  • Singular perturbation: find "hidden" solutions that escape to infinity as $\varepsilon\to 0$.
  • Laplace's method / stationary phase: evaluate $\int f(t) e^{-\lambda\phi(t)}dt$ for large $\lambda$.
  • Least-squares: find Jupiter's mass from Kepler data (slope of $T^2$ vs $R^3$).
  • $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit (radioactivity detectors) and contingency table (two professors' reviews).
  • Binomial / fair-coin hypothesis testing.
  • Prove Levi-Civita identities like $\vec A\times(\vec B\times\vec C) = \vec B(\vec A\cdot\vec C) - \vec C(\vec A\cdot\vec B)$.

First Principles & Theory (by Part)

Part 1 Tricks of the Trade: Gaussian Integrals & Infinite Sums

Dimensional analysis — the physicist's Swiss-army knife. The units of both sides of any equation must match. By writing an ansatz $Q \sim A^\alpha B^\beta C^\gamma$ and equating powers of each irreducible unit, you get a small linear system in $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$.

  • Worked example (Planck length): $[L_P] = \mathrm{m}^1\mathrm{s}^0\mathrm{kg}^0 = [\hbar]^\alpha[G]^\beta[c]^\gamma$ → $\alpha=\beta=1/2,\ \gamma=-3/2$ → $L_P \sim \sqrt{\hbar G/c^3}$.
  • Hawking radiation: $L \sim \hbar c^6 /(GM)^2$ — bigger black hole radiates less.

The Gaussian integral is the single most important integral in the course. Derive it by squaring and going to polar coordinates:

$$I_\alpha^2 = \!\!\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\!\!e^{-\alpha x^2}dx\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\!\!e^{-\alpha y^2}dy = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\!\int_0^\infty\!\! e^{-\alpha r^2}\,r\,dr\,d\varphi = \frac{\pi}{\alpha} \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{I_\alpha = \sqrt{\pi/\alpha}} \quad\text{[sheet eq. (1)]}$$

Feynman's trick (differentiation under the integral sign): treat $\alpha$ as a variable parameter. Taking $-d/d\alpha$ of both sides of $I_\alpha=\sqrt{\pi/\alpha}$ generates $\int e^{-\alpha x^2}x^{2n}dx$ for any $n$ — formula (2).

Completing the square turns any quadratic exponent into the standard Gaussian form:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\!\! e^{-(ax^2+bx+c)}\,dx = e^{-c + b^2/4a}\sqrt{\pi/a} \quad\text{[eq. (3)]}$$

Geometric series: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty q^n = \frac{1}{1-q}$ for $|q|<1$ [eq. (4)]. Appears whenever "each step shrinks by the same factor":

  • Bouncing ball (Zeno-like, Lec 1A setup — ball thrown up from ground to max height $h_0$): energy loss fraction $\alpha$ per bounce → total distance $D = 2h_0/\alpha$, total time $t=\sqrt{8h_0/g}/(1-\sqrt{1-\alpha})$ — finite despite infinite bounces. (If the ball is instead dropped from $h_0$, the first leg is just $h_0$ and $D_{\text{drop}} = h_0(2-\alpha)/\alpha$.)
  • Lamppost (random-walk renormalization): $P_{LP} = P_S/(P_R+P_S)$. Multiple encounters geometrically amplify a small single-step probability.

Part 2 Linear Algebra, Hilbert Space & Orthogonal Functions

Complex scalar product puts a complex conjugate on the first slot so the norm stays real:

$$ (\vec a,\vec b)=\sum_k a_k^*\, b_k\,,\qquad (\vec a,\vec b) = (\vec b,\vec a)^*,\qquad \|\vec a\|^2 = (\vec a,\vec a)\ge 0 \quad\text{[eqs. (25)–(26)]}$$

Adjoint and unitary: $(\hat A \vec a, \vec b) = (\vec a, \hat A^+ \vec b) \Rightarrow \hat A^+ = (\hat A^T)^* $ [eq. (27)]. A matrix is unitary if $\hat U^+ = \hat U^{-1}$ [eq. (28)] — it preserves scalar products and lengths.

Eigenvalue problem: $\hat A\vec x^{(k)} = \lambda_k \vec x^{(k)}\Rightarrow \det(\hat A-\lambda\hat I)=0$ [eq. (29)].

  • Hermitian $\hat H=\hat H^+$ → $\lambda_k\in\mathbb{R}$ and eigenvectors orthogonal (physical observables in QM) [eq. (30)].
  • Unitary → $|\lambda_k|=1$ and eigenvectors orthogonal (rotations, time evolution) [eq. (31)].
  • Commuting Hermitians share a common eigenbasis.

Gram-Schmidt [eqs. (48)–(50)]: turn any linearly-independent $u_0,u_1,u_2,\ldots$ into orthonormal $f_0,f_1,f_2,\ldots$:

$$f_0 = u_0/\|u_0\|,\qquad g_n = u_n - \sum_{k

Applied to $\{1,x,x^2,x^3,\dots\}$ with different weight functions $w(x)$ on $[a,b]$:

Interval + weightResulting orthogonal family
$[-1,1],\ w=1$Legendre $P_n(x)$
$(-\infty,\infty),\ w=e^{-x^2}$Hermite $H_n(x)$
$[0,\infty),\ w=e^{-x}$Laguerre $L_n(x)$

Dirac $\delta$-function is the continuous analog of the Kronecker $\delta_{ij}$:

$$\int f(x')\,\delta(x-x')\,dx' = f(x),\quad \delta(ax)=\tfrac{1}{|a|}\delta(x),\quad \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\!e^{\pm ik(x-x')}dk = \delta(x-x') \quad\text{[eq. (35)]}$$

Fourier series & transform are just changes of basis in Hilbert space.

$$ \hat f(k)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\!\!f(x)e^{-ikx}\,dx,\quad f(x)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\!\!\hat f(k)e^{+ikx}\,dk \quad\text{[eqs. (37)–(38)]}$$ $$ f(x)=\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\bigl(a_n\cos nx + b_n\sin nx\bigr) \quad\text{[eq. (39)]}$$

Vector calculus & Levi-Civita [eqs. (6)–(16)]: Most identities in 3D follow from the single identity $\sum_k\epsilon_{ijk}\epsilon_{mnk} = \delta_{im}\delta_{jn}-\delta_{in}\delta_{jm}$. Example: BAC-CAB $\vec A\times(\vec B\times\vec C) = \vec B(\vec A\cdot\vec C)-\vec C(\vec A\cdot\vec B)$ falls out in two lines.

Part 3 Gamma Function & N-Dimensional Sphere

Gamma function [eq. (53)]:

$$\Gamma(x)=\int_0^\infty t^{x-1}e^{-t}\,dt,\qquad \Gamma(n+1)=n!\ (n\in\mathbb{N}),\qquad \Gamma(1/2)=\sqrt\pi$$

Stirling's approximation [eq. (54)] — the factorial for large $n$:

$$\Gamma(x+1)\approx \sqrt{2\pi x}\;e^{-x}\,x^x\qquad(x\gg 1)$$

Volume of an $n$-ball of radius $R$:

$$V_n(R) = \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2+1)}R^n,\qquad S_n(R) = \frac{dV_n}{dR}= \frac{n\,V_n(R)}{R}$$

Curiosities that make great exam problems: as $n\to\infty$, $V_n\to 0$ (almost all the volume is in a thin shell near the surface), and $V_n$ is maximized around $n\approx 5$ for fixed $R=1$.

Part 4 Probability & Statistics

Three distributions, one ladder — each limit is an exam topic:

  • Binomial: $P(k;N,p)=\binom{N}{k}p^k(1-p)^{N-k}$. Use for finite $N$ trials, fixed $p$ (fair-coin problem).
  • Binomial → Poisson (rare events, large $N$, $Np=\mu$ fixed): $P(k;\mu)=\mu^k e^{-\mu}/k!$.
  • Poisson → Gaussian (large $\mu$): $P(k)\approx \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\mu}}\exp\bigl(-(k-\mu)^2/2\mu\bigr)$.

Least squares — linear fit $y = ax+b$ minimizing $S=\sum(y_i-ax_i-b)^2$:

$$ a = \frac{\sum x_iy_i - N\bar x\bar y}{\sum x_i^2 - N\bar x^2},\qquad b = \bar y - a\bar x $$

Jupiter application: plot $T_i^2$ vs $R_i^3$ for its moons; slope $a = 4\pi^2/(GM) \Rightarrow M = 4\pi^2/(Ga)$.

Chi-square goodness-of-fit — compare observed vs expected:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_i \frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i},\qquad \text{df} = N-1\ \text{(1D)}\ \text{or}\ (r-1)(c-1)\ \text{(contingency)}$$

In Excel use CHISQ.DIST.RT(χ², df) for the right-tail $p$-value, or the one-shot CHISQ.TEST(observed, expected). A large $p$ means "the data is consistent with the null hypothesis" (you fail to reject — the chair may be right).

Part 5 Complex Functions Basics

Every complex number is a 2D vector with a built-in multiplication rule:

$$ z = x+iy = \rho e^{i\varphi},\qquad e^{i\varphi} = \cos\varphi + i\sin\varphi \quad\text{[eq. (34)]}$$ $$ \cos x = \tfrac{1}{2}(e^{ix}+e^{-ix}),\quad \sin x = \tfrac{1}{2i}(e^{ix}-e^{-ix})\quad\text{[eq. (52)]}$$ $$ \cosh x = \tfrac{1}{2}(e^x+e^{-x}),\quad \sinh x = \tfrac{1}{2}(e^x-e^{-x})\quad\text{[eq. (51)]}$$

Why it matters for the exam: many integrals (esp. Fourier, stationary-phase) simplify dramatically when you write trig as complex exponentials. The factor $e^{\pm i\pi/4}$ in the stationary-phase formulas comes from $\sqrt{\pm i}$.

Part 6 Asymptotics, Perturbations & Variations

Regular perturbation [eq. (30)]: expand solution as power series in small $\varepsilon$:

$$ x = X_0 + X_1\varepsilon + X_2\varepsilon^2 + X_3\varepsilon^3 + o(\varepsilon^3) $$

Substitute, collect powers of $\varepsilon$, solve order-by-order. The number of solutions is preserved.

Singular perturbation [eq. (32), repeated-root form]: when $\varepsilon$ multiplies the highest order or a coefficient that controls topology, some solutions escape to infinity as $\varepsilon\to 0$. Use rescaled expansion $x=X_0+X_1\varepsilon^\alpha+X_2\varepsilon^{2\alpha}+\cdots$ with $\alpha$ determined by dominant-balance.

Laplace's method (large-$\lambda$ asymptotics of integrals with a real exponent) [eqs. (40)–(43)]:

$$\int f(t)e^{-\lambda\phi(t)}dt \;\approx\; \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda\,\phi''(c)}}\,f(c)\,e^{-\lambda\phi(c)},\quad \phi'(c)=0,\ \phi''(c)>0 $$

The integral is dominated by the minimum of $\phi$. Expand $\phi$ quadratically about $c$, pull $f(c)$ and $e^{-\lambda\phi(c)}$ out, the remaining integral is a Gaussian.

Stationary phase (imaginary exponent, oscillatory integrals) [eqs. (44)–(47)]:

$$\int f(t)e^{i\lambda\phi(t)}dt \;\approx\; \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda|\phi''(c)|}}\,f(c)\,e^{i\lambda\phi(c)}\,e^{i\pi/4\,\mathrm{sgn}(\phi''(c))},\quad \phi'(c)=0$$

The extra $e^{\pm i\pi/4}$ is the hallmark — easy to forget and a classic deduction point.

Calculus of variations [eqs. (20)–(23)]: extremize $\displaystyle S[y] = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} f(x,y,y')\,dx$ → Euler–Lagrange equation

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - \frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial f}{\partial y'} = 0$$

Two shortcuts:

  • No explicit $x$ (i.e. $f=f(y,y')$) → first integral $f - y'\,\partial f/\partial y' = C$. (Brachistochrone, soap-film, minimum-surface problems.)
  • No explicit $y$ (i.e. $f=f(x,y')$) → $\partial f/\partial y' = C$. (Geodesics in special metrics.)
  • Constrained extremization (Lagrange multipliers): extremize $\tilde f = f + \lambda g$ where $g(x,y)=0$ is the constraint.

Lagrangian mechanics: $L=K-U$; equations of motion are Euler-Lagrange on $L$. Especially handy when the system has obvious coupled oscillators (two-masses-three-springs, CO$_2$ molecule).

Notice the unifying thread: projecting onto a well-chosen basis. Gaussian integrals project onto Gaussians, Fourier onto plane waves, Gram-Schmidt onto orthogonal polynomials, normal modes onto eigenvectors of the Hermitian stiffness matrix, least-squares onto the column space of the design matrix. Part 6 (Laplace, stationary phase) is "local projection onto a quadratic near a critical point." Learn to see projections everywhere.

Multiple-Choice (15 MCQ)

MCQ 1  ·  Part 1 (Gaussian)
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-3x^2+12x-7}\,dx$.
(A) $\sqrt{\pi/3}\,e^{-7}$
(B) $\sqrt{\pi/3}\,e^{5}$
(C) $\sqrt{\pi/12}\,e^{-7}$
(D) $\sqrt{\pi}\,e^{-12}$
Show Solution

Use formula (3) with $a=3,\ b=-12,\ c=7$: $\displaystyle\int = e^{-c\,+\,b^{2}/4a}\sqrt{\pi/a} = e^{-7\,+\,144/12}\sqrt{\pi/3} = e^{-7\,+\,12}\sqrt{\pi/3} = e^{5}\sqrt{\pi/3}$.

MCQ 2  ·  Part 1 (Geometric sum)
A ball is thrown vertically upward from the ground with initial speed $v_0$ such that it reaches a maximum height $h_0 = 1$ m. On every bounce it loses a fraction $\alpha = 0.2$ of its kinetic energy. The total distance the ball travels before coming to rest is:
(A) 2 m
(B) 5 m
(C) 10 m
(D) ∞ m
Show Solution

This is the exact setup from Lecture 1A. After each bounce the maximum height is reduced by factor $(1-\alpha)$, and each complete "up-and-down" contributes $2h_n = 2h_0(1-\alpha)^n$ to the total distance:

$$D = 2h_0 + 2h_0(1-\alpha) + 2h_0(1-\alpha)^2 + \cdots = \frac{2h_0}{1-(1-\alpha)} = \frac{2h_0}{\alpha}.$$

With $h_0=1$ m, $\alpha=0.2$: $D = 2/0.2 = 10$ m. Finite despite infinitely many bounces — Zeno's-paradox convergence of a geometric series.

⚠ Note on the problem setup: If instead the ball were dropped from $h_0$ (initial downward motion rather than thrown up), the first leg of travel is only $h_0$ (one drop) rather than $2h_0$ (up + down), and the correct expression becomes $D_{\text{drop}} = h_0(2-\alpha)/\alpha = 9$ m for these numbers. The course's convention (Lec 1A) is "thrown up," giving 10 m.

MCQ 3  ·  Part 2 (Complex inner product)
In a complex vector space, the adjoint $\hat A^+$ is defined so that $(\hat A \vec a, \vec b)=(\vec a, \hat A^+ \vec b)$. In components, $\hat A^+$ equals:
(A) $\hat A^T$ (transpose only)
(B) $\hat A^*$ (complex conjugate only)
(C) $(\hat A^T)^* $ (transpose + complex conjugate)
(D) $\hat A^{-1}$
Show Solution

From eq. (27) on the formula sheet: $\hat A^+ = (\hat A^T)^*$. Transpose alone fails for complex entries because the first slot of the scalar product gets complex-conjugated.

MCQ 4  ·  Part 2 (Hermitian matrices)
Which statement about a Hermitian matrix $\hat H = \hat H^+$ is false?
(A) All eigenvalues are real
(B) Eigenvectors belonging to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal
(C) $\hat H$ is diagonalizable by a unitary transformation
(D) $\hat H$ always has unit determinant
Show Solution

(A), (B), (C) are the three pillars of Hermitian matrices (eq. 30). The determinant is the product of eigenvalues; it can be anything real. Only unitary matrices have $|\det|=1$.

MCQ 5  ·  Part 2 (Orthogonal polynomials)
Applying Gram-Schmidt to $\{1,x,x^2,x^3,\ldots\}$ on $[-1,1]$ with weight $w(x)=1$ produces which family?
(A) Legendre polynomials
(B) Hermite polynomials
(C) Laguerre polynomials
(D) Chebyshev polynomials of the 1st kind
Show Solution

Interval $[-1,1]$, weight $1$ → Legendre. Hermite lives on $(-\infty,\infty)$ with $w=e^{-x^2}$; Laguerre on $[0,\infty)$ with $w=e^{-x}$.

MCQ 6  ·  Part 2 (Vector identities)
Using $\sum_k\epsilon_{ijk}\epsilon_{mnk}=\delta_{im}\delta_{jn}-\delta_{in}\delta_{jm}$ (eq. 6), the expression $\vec A\times(\vec B\times\vec C)$ equals:
(A) $\vec A(\vec B\cdot\vec C) - \vec B(\vec A\cdot\vec C)$
(B) $\vec B(\vec A\cdot\vec C) - \vec C(\vec A\cdot\vec B)$
(C) $\vec C(\vec A\cdot\vec B) - \vec B(\vec A\cdot\vec C)$
(D) $(\vec A\cdot\vec B)\vec C - (\vec B\cdot\vec C)\vec A$
Show Solution

"BAC minus CAB" — eq. (7). The middle vector of the "inner" cross product gets the inner product with the "outer" vector.

MCQ 7  ·  Part 3 (Gamma & sphere)
Using $V_n(R) = \pi^{n/2}R^n/\Gamma(n/2+1)$, the volume of the 4-ball of radius $R=1$ is:
(A) $\pi$
(B) $\pi^2/2$
(C) $4\pi/3$
(D) $8\pi^2/15$
Show Solution

$V_4 = \pi^{2}/\Gamma(3) = \pi^2/2! = \pi^2/2 \approx 4.93$. (For comparison, $V_2=\pi$, $V_3=4\pi/3\approx 4.19$ — the 4-ball is actually bigger.)

MCQ 8  ·  Part 3 (Stirling)
Stirling's approximation $\Gamma(x+1)\approx\sqrt{2\pi x}\,e^{-x}x^x$ is used because for large $n$:
(A) $n!$ is inexpressible in closed form
(B) $n!$ grows faster than any polynomial; Stirling gives a tractable asymptotic
(C) $n!$ becomes a continuous function only through Stirling
(D) The integral for $\Gamma(n)$ diverges
Show Solution

(B). Stirling is derived by applying Laplace's method to $\Gamma(x+1)=\int_0^\infty t^x e^{-t}dt$: the integrand peaks at $t=x$, expand quadratically, get the $\sqrt{2\pi x}$ prefactor.

MCQ 9  ·  Part 4 (χ² contingency)
For a $2\times 4$ contingency table (two professors, four review categories), the number of degrees of freedom is:
(A) 8
(B) 7
(C) 3
(D) 1
Show Solution

$\mathrm{df}=(r-1)(c-1)=(2-1)(4-1)=3$. This is exactly Problem 3 of the F2025 take-home.

MCQ 10  ·  Part 4 (Least squares)
In the Jupiter problem, you plot $T_i^2$ vs $R_i^3$ and get a best-fit slope $a = 3.146\times 10^{-16}\,\mathrm{s^2/m^3}$. Jupiter's mass follows from:
(A) $M = Ga/(4\pi^2)$
(B) $M = 4\pi^2/(Ga)$
(C) $M = a\cdot G$
(D) $M = 2\pi\sqrt{a/G}$
Show Solution

Kepler's 3rd: $T^2 = (4\pi^2/GM)R^3 \Rightarrow a=4\pi^2/(GM) \Rightarrow M=4\pi^2/(Ga)$. Plugging numbers with $G=6.67\times 10^{-11}$ gives $M\approx 1.9\times 10^{27}$ kg.

MCQ 11  ·  Part 5 (Fourier transform)
The Fourier transform of a Gaussian $f(x)=e^{-\alpha x^2}$ is:
(A) $\sqrt{\alpha/\pi}\,e^{-k^2/4\alpha}$
(B) $\sqrt{\pi/\alpha}\,e^{-k^2/4\alpha}$
(C) $(1/\alpha)\,e^{-\alpha k^2}$
(D) $\delta(k)$
Show Solution

$\hat f(k)=\int e^{-\alpha x^2} e^{-ikx}dx$. Complete the square in the exponent: $-\alpha x^2 - ikx = -\alpha(x+ik/2\alpha)^2 - k^2/(4\alpha)$. Apply formula (3) → $\sqrt{\pi/\alpha}\,e^{-k^2/4\alpha}$. The width of the FT is inverse to the width of the original (uncertainty principle!).

MCQ 12  ·  Part 6 (Regular perturbation)
To first order in $\varepsilon$, the roots of $x^2 - 2\varepsilon x - 1 = 0$ are:
(A) $x\approx\pm\varepsilon$
(B) $x\approx \pm 1 + \varepsilon$
(C) $x\approx \varepsilon\pm 1$ (with $\pm$ swapping)
(D) $x\approx 0,\ 2\varepsilon$
Show Solution

At $\varepsilon=0$: $x^2=1 \Rightarrow x=\pm1$. Write $x=\pm 1 + X_1\varepsilon$, plug in, collect $O(\varepsilon)$: $\pm 2X_1 - 2(\pm 1) = 0 \Rightarrow X_1=1$ for both roots. So $x_\pm \approx \pm 1 + \varepsilon$.

MCQ 13  ·  Part 6 (Laplace's method)
For Laplace's method applied to $\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f(t)e^{-\lambda\phi(t)}dt$ with $\lambda\gg 1$, the dominant contribution comes from:
(A) The endpoints of integration
(B) The maximum of $\phi$
(C) The minimum of $\phi$
(D) Any stationary point of $f$
Show Solution

Because of the minus sign in the exponent, $e^{-\lambda\phi}$ is largest where $\phi$ is smallest. Expand $\phi(t)\approx \phi(c)+\tfrac12\phi''(c)(t-c)^2$ and do a Gaussian — gives the $\sqrt{2\pi/\lambda\phi''(c)}$ prefactor from eq. (42).

MCQ 14  ·  Part 6 (Euler-Lagrange)
If $f(x,y,y')$ has no explicit dependence on $x$, the Euler-Lagrange equation reduces to the conserved quantity:
(A) $\partial f/\partial y = 0$
(B) $\partial f/\partial y' = C$
(C) $f - y'\,\partial f/\partial y' = C$
(D) $y' = C$
Show Solution

Eq. (22). This is analogous to "energy conservation when the Lagrangian has no explicit time." Used for brachistochrone, minimum-surface-of-revolution, etc.

MCQ 15  ·  Part 4 (Binomial)
A fair coin flipped 10 times. The probability of getting exactly 7 heads is:
(A) $7/10 = 0.70$
(B) $\binom{10}{7}/2^7 \approx 0.94$
(C) $\binom{10}{7}/2^{10} \approx 0.117$
(D) $1/\binom{10}{7} \approx 0.0083$
Show Solution

Binomial: $P(k=7;N=10,p=1/2)=\binom{10}{7}(1/2)^7(1/2)^3 = 120/1024 \approx 0.117$. HW 5 asks you to decide if an observed 7/10 heads is consistent with a fair coin — this tail probability is the key input.

Free-Response (5 FR)

FR 1  ·  Part 1 + Part 3 (Gaussian + Gamma)

(a) Starting from $\displaystyle I_\alpha=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\alpha x^2}dx=\sqrt{\pi/\alpha}$, use differentiation under the integral sign (Feynman's trick) to derive $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} x^{2n}e^{-\alpha x^2}dx = \frac{(2n-1)!!}{2^n\alpha^n}\sqrt{\pi/\alpha}$.

(b) Using $\Gamma(x)=\int_0^\infty t^{x-1}e^{-t}dt$ and the substitution $t=\alpha x^2$, re-express the integral in (a) in terms of $\Gamma$ and verify consistency.

5 pts Correct setup: $-dI_\alpha/d\alpha = \int x^2 e^{-\alpha x^2}dx = \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{\pi}\alpha^{-3/2}$.
5 pts Induction: each $-d/d\alpha$ brings down an extra $x^2$ and multiplies the RHS by $(n+1/2)/\alpha$ → $(2n-1)!!/(2^n\alpha^n)$ pattern.
5 pts Substitute $t=\alpha x^2$, $dt=2\alpha x\,dx$ → integral $= (\alpha^{-n-1/2}/2)\Gamma(n+1/2)$; use $\Gamma(n+1/2)=(2n-1)!!\sqrt\pi/2^n$. Match.
5 pts Clear notation, signs, even-function factor of 2 for $\int_0^\infty$.
FR 2  ·  Part 2 (Normal modes)

Two identical masses $m$ connected by three identical springs of constant $k$ between two fixed walls: wall—spring—$m$—spring—$m$—spring—wall.

(a) Write the equations of motion and cast them as $\ddot{\vec x} = -\hat K \vec x$. Identify the $2\times 2$ matrix $\hat K/m$.

(b) Find the normal-mode frequencies and the corresponding eigenvectors.

(c) Describe the physical motion of each mode.

5 pts EOM: $m\ddot x_1=-k x_1 + k(x_2-x_1)$ and analogue; matrix $\hat K/m = (k/m)\begin{pmatrix}2&-1\\-1&2\end{pmatrix}$.
5 pts Eigenvalues: $\det(\hat K/m - \omega^2 I)=0 \Rightarrow (2-\omega^2 m/k)^2-1=0 \Rightarrow \omega_1^2 = k/m,\ \omega_2^2 = 3k/m$.
5 pts Eigenvectors: $\omega_1$ → $(1,1)$ symmetric (in-phase); $\omega_2$ → $(1,-1)$ antisymmetric (out-of-phase).
5 pts Physical picture: symmetric mode compresses only the outer two springs, middle one unstretched → lower frequency. Antisymmetric mode compresses all three → higher frequency.
FR 3  ·  Part 2 / Part 5 (Fourier series)

Find the Fourier series of $f(x)=x^2$ on $[-\pi,\pi]$.

(a) Compute $a_0$, $a_n$, $b_n$.

(b) Evaluate the series at $x=\pi$ to obtain the famous sum $\sum_{n=1}^\infty 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$.

3 pts $f$ is even → all $b_n=0$.
4 pts $a_0 = (1/\pi)\int_{-\pi}^\pi x^2 dx = 2\pi^2/3$.
6 pts $a_n = (1/\pi)\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}x^2\cos(nx)dx = 4(-1)^n/n^2$ (integration by parts twice).
4 pts Series: $x^2 = \pi^2/3 + 4\sum_{n\ge 1}\frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}\cos(nx)$.
3 pts At $x=\pi$: $\pi^2 = \pi^2/3 + 4\sum 1/n^2 \Rightarrow \sum 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$. ✅
FR 4  ·  Part 4 (χ² goodness-of-fit) — F2025 Final Problem 2

Ten students measure a radioactive sample; each is expected to observe 20 decays/hr after detector correction. Their reported counts are: 23, 13, 16, 25, 23, 25, 22, 17, 15, 21.

(a) Compute the $\chi^2$ statistic $\sum_i(O_i-E_i)^2/E_i$ with $E_i=20$ for all $i$.

(b) State the number of degrees of freedom.

(c) Use CHISQ.DIST.RT(χ², df) or a table to find the $p$-value, and state whether the detectors are consistent with being equivalent at 95% confidence.

(d) Verify using CHISQ.TEST(observed, expected).

5 pts Table of $(O_i-20)^2/20$: 0.45, 2.45, 0.80, 1.25, 0.45, 1.25, 0.20, 0.45, 1.25, 0.05 → $\chi^2 \approx 8.6$.
3 pts df = $N-1 = 9$.
6 pts $p\approx 0.47$ → cannot reject $H_0$; detectors are statistically consistent.
3 pts CHISQ.TEST call should give the same $p$-value to within Excel rounding.
3 pts Plain-language interpretation: high $p$ means data is "too boring" to disprove equivalence, not that it proves it.
FR 5  ·  Part 6 (Laplace / Stirling derivation)

Derive Stirling's approximation for $n!$ by applying Laplace's method to the integral $\Gamma(n+1) = \int_0^\infty t^n e^{-t}\,dt$.

(a) Rewrite the integrand as $e^{n\ln t - t}$ and identify the "phase" $\phi(t)$.

(b) Find the critical point $c$ where $\phi'(c)=0$.

(c) Expand $\phi$ quadratically and evaluate the Gaussian to obtain $\Gamma(n+1)\approx\sqrt{2\pi n}\,n^n e^{-n}$.

4 pts Write $t^n e^{-t} = e^{n\ln t - t}$; identify $\phi(t)=t-n\ln t$ (with $\lambda=1$) or equivalently $\phi(t)=t/n - \ln t$ with $\lambda=n$.
5 pts $\phi'(t)=1 - n/t = 0 \Rightarrow c = n$. $\phi''(c) = n/c^2 = 1/n > 0$ (minimum).
6 pts Evaluate: $\phi(c) = n - n\ln n$ so $e^{-\phi(c)} = n^n e^{-n}$. Gaussian integral gives $\sqrt{2\pi/\phi''(c)} = \sqrt{2\pi n}$.
5 pts Combine: $\Gamma(n+1)\approx \sqrt{2\pi n}\,n^n e^{-n}$. ✅ Matches eq. (54).

Common Misconceptions

❌ "The real part of a complex scalar product is the norm."
Correct view: The scalar product $(\vec a,\vec b)=\sum_k a_k^*b_k$ is complex in general. The norm $\|\vec a\|^2 = (\vec a,\vec a)$ is always real because the conjugate automatically kills imaginary pieces. Don't confuse "taking real part" with "taking the norm" — they are not the same operation.
❌ "Regular and singular perturbation are basically the same thing."
Correct view: A regular perturbation preserves the number of solutions and their topology as $\varepsilon\to 0$. A singular perturbation is when $\varepsilon$ multiplies the highest derivative or the leading coefficient — new solutions appear out of nowhere (often at $x\sim 1/\varepsilon$). Diagnostic: if setting $\varepsilon=0$ lowers the order of the equation, you have a singular problem and need fractional powers $\varepsilon^\alpha$ in your ansatz.
❌ "A large chi-square means the data is inconsistent."
Correct view: You don't judge $\chi^2$ alone — you judge the $p$-value (probability of seeing a $\chi^2$ this large or larger under the null hypothesis). A large $\chi^2$ on 100 degrees of freedom may be routine. Always quote CHISQ.DIST.RT(χ², df). And remember: a large $p$ means you fail to reject $H_0$, not that $H_0$ is true.
❌ "Dropping the $e^{\pm i\pi/4}$ factor in stationary phase is a minor error."
Correct view: That factor is the signature of stationary phase (vs Laplace's method). Losing it changes the phase of a wave by 45° — enough to flip interference patterns. On the exam it's a "free" 3–5 points to quote it correctly from eqs. (44)–(47).
❌ "Gram-Schmidt gives unique answers."
Correct view: The output depends on the starting list order and on the inner-product weight $w(x)$. $\{1,x,x^2\}$ and $\{x^2,x,1\}$ produce different orthogonal sets. Different weights on the same $[a,b]$ produce Legendre vs Hermite vs Laguerre polynomials. When a problem says "orthogonalize," always check the interval and weight first.
❌ "The Dirac $\delta$ is a function."
Correct view: $\delta(x)$ is a distribution (a functional) — it only makes sense inside an integral. The Fourier-representation $\delta(x-x') = (1/2\pi)\int e^{ik(x-x')}dk$ illustrates this: the integral is divergent in the classical sense but perfectly well-defined as a tempered distribution. Treat $\delta$ like a "formal kernel," not a pointwise function.
❌ "Dimensional analysis gives exact answers."
Correct view: Dimensional analysis fixes the functional form up to a dimensionless prefactor. The prefactor can be $1$, or $1/(15360\pi)$ as in Hawking radiation. Always write $\sim$ or $\propto$, never $=$, when reporting a purely dimensional result.
❌ "In least squares, the intercept $b$ is meaningless if large."
Correct view: A huge $b$ just means the origin is far outside the data range (as in the Jupiter problem where $R^3\sim 10^{24}$–$10^{31}$). The slope $a$ is what matters for the physics. Don't be alarmed by large $b$ — evaluate it on the plot at $x=0$ and it should be tiny relative to the data range.
Every misconception above corresponds to a specific pattern of lost points on past exams. Internalizing the correction is cheap insurance.

How the Concepts Connect (in words)

  • Gaussian integrals (Part 1) are the universal glue. They appear in Laplace's method (Part 6), Fourier transform of a Gaussian (Part 5), Stirling (Part 3 via Part 6), and in the Gaussian limit of Poisson (Part 4).
  • Inner products underlie everything in Parts 2 & 5. Fourier series/transform is just "inner product with plane waves $e^{ikx}$." Gram-Schmidt is "inner product peels off previous basis vectors." Even least squares (Part 4) is "orthogonal projection onto the column space."
  • $\Gamma$ and Stirling (Part 3) are derived using Laplace's method (Part 6). The N-sphere uses $\Gamma$ to interpolate between integer dimensions.
  • Perturbation theory (Part 6) reduces to polynomial algebra until $\varepsilon$ multiplies the leading term — then fractional powers $\varepsilon^\alpha$ enter.
  • Laplace's method & stationary phase are twin siblings: same $\sqrt{2\pi/(\lambda|\phi''|)}$ skeleton; real vs imaginary exponent is the only branching.
  • $\chi^2$ (Part 4) is a Gaussian-in-disguise: $\sum((O_i-E_i)/\sqrt{E_i})^2$ is a sum of squared standard-normal-like variables. That's why the distribution has a known form.

The Hardest Parts Explained

🔹 1. Completing the Square Inside a Fourier or Gaussian Integral

Why it's hard: The algebra gets messy when the exponent mixes real and imaginary terms (e.g. $-\alpha x^2 - ikx$), and signs flip easily.

Clean recipe:

  1. Factor out the coefficient of $x^2$: $-\alpha x^2 + \beta x + \gamma = -\alpha\bigl(x^2 - \tfrac{\beta}{\alpha}x\bigr) + \gamma$.
  2. Complete the square: $x^2 - \tfrac{\beta}{\alpha}x = \bigl(x - \tfrac{\beta}{2\alpha}\bigr)^2 - \tfrac{\beta^2}{4\alpha^2}$.
  3. Pull the constant out of the integral (it's just a number).
  4. Shift $x\to x+\beta/(2\alpha)$; limits at $\pm\infty$ are unchanged.
  5. Remaining integral is the textbook Gaussian → $\sqrt{\pi/\alpha}$.

Sanity check: For $\beta=0$ you should recover formula (1). For $\alpha=1/(2\sigma^2)$ you should recover the standard normal normalization $1/\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}$ after including the normalization constant.

🔹 2. Choosing Between Laplace's Method and Stationary Phase

Why it's hard: They look almost identical on the formula sheet (eqs. 40–47). Students use the wrong one and lose all the phase information.

Decision tree:

  • Exponent is real negative ($e^{-\lambda\phi}$): Laplace. Dominant at minimum of $\phi$; no $i\pi/4$ factor.
  • Exponent is imaginary ($e^{i\lambda\phi}$): stationary phase. Dominant at any stationary point of $\phi$; include $e^{\pm i\pi/4}$ depending on sign of $\phi''$.
  • Exponent is real positive ($e^{+\lambda\phi}$): "Laplace on the maximum" — eq. (41) uses $|\phi''(c)|$ because $\phi''(c)<0$ at a maximum.

Sign of $\phi''$ at the critical point is what picks the sign of the phase $\pi/4$ vs $-\pi/4$.

🔹 3. Setting Up the $\chi^2$ Expected Values in a Contingency Table

Why it's hard: For a 1D goodness-of-fit (F2025 Problem 2) the expected counts are given (20 each). But in contingency tables (F2025 Problem 3) you have to compute them from row and column marginals.

Recipe:

$$E_{ij} = \frac{(\text{row}_i\ \text{total}) \times (\text{col}_j\ \text{total})}{N_{\text{grand total}}}$$

Example with the F2025 prof data: Prof A totals 7+12+31+5 = 55, Prof B totals 15+18+19+10 = 62, column totals 22/30/50/15, grand total $N=117$. Expected "Prof A, great" = $55\times 22/117 \approx 10.34$. Compute all 8 cells the same way.

Then: $\chi^2 = \sum_{i,j}(O_{ij}-E_{ij})^2/E_{ij}$ with df $= (2-1)(4-1) = 3$.

🔹 4. Regular vs Singular Perturbation: Dominant Balance

Why it's hard: Students try a plain series $x = X_0+\varepsilon X_1+\cdots$ and it fails — the algebraic system is inconsistent.

Diagnostic: If setting $\varepsilon=0$ reduces the degree of the polynomial (or the order of the ODE), you have a singular problem. At least one solution is "near infinity."

Recipe (for algebraic equations):

  1. Set $\varepsilon=0$ and solve → find the "regular" roots.
  2. For the missing roots, try the rescaling $x = \varepsilon^{-\alpha} X$ with $\alpha>0$ unknown. Plug in.
  3. Dominant balance: choose $\alpha$ so that at least two terms in the rescaled equation have the same power of $\varepsilon$ and dominate the rest.
  4. Solve the balanced (leading-order) equation for $X$. Then develop the series $X = X_0 + X_1\varepsilon^\beta + \cdots$.

This is exactly what the two formulas (30) "regular" and (32) "rep. roots" on the formula sheet are hinting at.

🔹 5. The "t = αx²" Substitution Between Gaussian Integrals and Γ

Why it's hard: The bookkeeping on $dx/dt$, the extra factor of $2$ (even function), and the $\alpha^{-n-1/2}$ power all compound.

Template:

$$\int_0^\infty x^{2n}e^{-\alpha x^2}dx \overset{t=\alpha x^2}{=} \frac{1}{2}\alpha^{-n-1/2}\int_0^\infty t^{n-1/2}e^{-t}dt = \frac{1}{2}\alpha^{-n-1/2}\,\Gamma\!\left(n+\tfrac12\right)$$

Use $\Gamma(n+1/2) = (2n-1)!!\sqrt\pi/2^n$ to convert to double-factorial form. Multiplying by 2 (because the original integral runs from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$) recovers formula (2).

🔹 6. Applying Euler-Lagrange Shortcuts Correctly

Why it's hard: Many students memorize $f - y'\partial f/\partial y' = C$ without knowing when it applies.

Checklist:

  • No explicit $x$ in $f(x,y,y')$ → use $f - y'\partial f/\partial y' = C$ (the "Beltrami identity").
  • No explicit $y$ → use $\partial f/\partial y' = C$ (the "cyclic coordinate" case).
  • Neither simplification holds → plug into the full Euler-Lagrange equation and just compute carefully.
  • Constraints → introduce Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$, extremize $\tilde f = f + \lambda g$ where $g(x,y)=0$.

Classic trap: on the brachistochrone $f = \sqrt{(1+y'^2)/(2gy)}$, $y$ appears explicitly, so use shortcut (a) — NOT (b).

Each "hard" item above is really a matter of classification. Correct classification (which formula applies? which limit am I in? which variable is explicit?) eliminates 90% of the difficulty. Practice diagnosing before computing.

Memory Anchors: Shortcuts & Mnemonics

📌 Anchor 1: "$\sqrt{\pi/\alpha}$" — The One Integral You Must Own

Every Gaussian integral on the exam reduces to $\int e^{-\alpha x^2}dx = \sqrt{\pi/\alpha}$ (formula 1). Everything else (Feynman, completing the square, Fourier of Gaussian, Stirling, Laplace's method) is an elaborate dressing of this fact. Repeat 10 times: square the integral, go to polar, done.

📌 Anchor 2: "3 Distributions, 2 Limits" — The Probability Ladder

Binomial $\xrightarrow{N\to\infty,\ p\to 0,\ Np=\mu\text{ fixed}}$ Poisson $\xrightarrow{\mu\to\infty}$ Gaussian.

Use Binomial for small $N$ (coin tosses), Poisson for counts of rare events (radioactive decay), Gaussian for large means (central limit theorem). The F2025 final uses all three.

📌 Anchor 3: "(r-1)(c-1)" — Contingency-Table df

$\mathrm{df}_{\text{contingency}} = (r-1)(c-1)$. For F2025 Problem 3 (2 profs × 4 review categories) df $= 3$. For 1D goodness-of-fit with $N$ bins, df $= N-1$.

📌 Anchor 4: "Hermitian = Real & Orthogonal, Unitary = Unit-Modulus"

Hermitian eigenvalues: real. Unitary eigenvalues: on the unit circle, $|\lambda|=1$. Eigenvectors of both are orthogonal. Common eigenbasis when matrices commute.

📌 Anchor 5: "BAC minus CAB" — Triple Cross Product

$\vec A\times(\vec B\times\vec C) = \vec B(\vec A\cdot\vec C) - \vec C(\vec A\cdot\vec B)$. Mnemonic: the middle vector (B) gets paired with the outer (A) and multiplied by the remaining (C). Then swap and subtract.

📌 Anchor 6: "$\sqrt{2\pi/(\lambda|\phi''(c)|)}\ f(c)\ e^{\pm i\pi/4\,\mathrm{sgn}(\phi''(c))}$" — Asymptotics Skeleton

All 8 asymptotic formulas (eqs. 40–47) share the same skeleton. Just plug in:

  • Real, minus sign ($e^{-\lambda\phi}$): pure prefactor, no phase.
  • Real, plus sign ($e^{+\lambda\phi}$): same but use $|\phi''(c)|$ (minus becomes absolute value).
  • Imaginary ($e^{i\lambda\phi}$): add $e^{+i\pi/4}$ if $\phi''(c)>0$, $e^{-i\pi/4}$ if $\phi''(c)<0$.
📌 Anchor 7: "FT of Gaussian is Gaussian" — Width Trade-off

$\mathcal{F}[e^{-\alpha x^2}] = \sqrt{\pi/\alpha}\,e^{-k^2/4\alpha}$. Narrow in $x$ → wide in $k$. This is the Heisenberg uncertainty principle in disguise: the product of widths $\sigma_x\sigma_k = 1/2$.

📌 Anchor 8: "$V_n = \pi^{n/2}R^n/\Gamma(n/2+1)$" — N-Sphere Volume Shortcut

Memorize first few: $V_1=2R$, $V_2=\pi R^2$, $V_3=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3$, $V_4=\tfrac{\pi^2}{2}R^4$, $V_5=\tfrac{8\pi^2}{15}R^5$. Ratio $V_{n+2}/V_n = 2\pi R^2/(n+2)$ → fast recursion.

📌 Anchor 9: "Beltrami for No $x$, Cyclic for No $y$" — E-L Shortcuts

If $f(x,y,y')$ has no $x$, use $f - y'\partial_{y'}f = C$ (eq. 22). If no $y$, use $\partial_{y'}f = C$ (eq. 21). Both are "conserved quantities" — like energy and momentum in Lagrangian mechanics.

📌 Anchor 10: "Stirling = Laplace on Γ" — The Derivation in Your Head

$\Gamma(n+1)=\int t^n e^{-t}dt = \int e^{n\ln t - t}dt$. Peak at $t=n$ (take derivative). Second derivative there is $1/n$. Laplace formula gives $\sqrt{2\pi n}\cdot n^n e^{-n}$. That's it. If asked to "derive Stirling," this 4-line story earns full credit.

❌ Top Mistakes to Avoid

  • Treating complex scalar product as symmetric — it's only conjugate-symmetric.
  • Forgetting the $e^{\pm i\pi/4}$ phase in stationary-phase answers.
  • Using df = $N$ instead of $N-1$ in $\chi^2$ tests.
  • Using the wrong formula for contingency df: $(r-1)(c-1)$, not $rc-1$.
  • Dropping the factor of 2 when converting $\int_0^\infty$ to $\int_{-\infty}^\infty$ or vice versa for even integrands.
  • Confusing $a$ (slope) with $M$ in the Jupiter least-squares problem.
  • Using $P_n(x)$ when the problem wants Hermite or Laguerre — always check the interval and weight first.
  • Mixing $\hbar$, $G$, $c$ exponents in dimensional analysis — set up the linear system cleanly.

CHISQ.INV.RT=CHISQ.INV.RT(α, df)Critical $\chi^2$ at significance level $\alpha$ (e.g. 0.05). Useful if the problem asks "at what confidence level…"
LINEST=LINEST(y_range, x_range)Alternative to the "add trendline + display equation" approach for least-squares — returns slope and intercept directly.
SLOPE / INTERCEPT=SLOPE(y, x), =INTERCEPT(y, x)Just the two coefficients of the best-fit line; Jupiter-mass calculation in one cell.
BINOM.DIST=BINOM.DIST(k, N, p, cumulative)Exact binomial probability; useful for HW 5 & F2025 Problem 4 (fair-coin test).
