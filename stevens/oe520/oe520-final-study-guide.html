What OE 520 Covers

OE 520 Ocean Wave Mechanics teaches you to mathematically describe ocean waves, derive their behavior from first principles (mass & momentum conservation), predict how waves transform as they travel from deep water into the surf zone, characterize random sea states statistically, and compute the forces waves exert on offshore structures.

The final exam covers the full course (Lectures 1–21), with emphasis on the second half (post-midterm): wave energy & group velocity, shoaling/refraction/diffraction/reflection, wave statistics (Rayleigh distribution), wave spectra (Pierson-Moskowitz, Bretschneider, JONSWAP), and the Morison equation for forces on slender structures.

First Principles & Theory Backbone

🔗 Connection to Prior Courses

Fluid Mechanics (prerequisite): You learned about continuity, Bernoulli, viscous flow, vector calculus (gradient, divergence, curl). OE 520 takes the Euler equations for inviscid flow and applies them to a free surface — that's all linear wave theory really is.

Calculus (integrals, derivatives, partial derivatives): Required throughout — especially for Leibniz rule, separation of variables in the Laplacian, and integrating particle velocities to find paths.

🪜 The Logical Path from "Water" to "Forces on Structures"

  1. Conservation of mass on a material volume + Leibniz rule → continuity equation ∂ρ/∂t + ∇·(ρ𝐮) = 0.
  2. Incompressibility (ρ = const) reduces this to ∇·𝐮 = 0.
  3. Irrotational flow (∇×𝐮 = 0, valid because waves are inviscid + start from rest) lets us define a velocity potential 𝐮 = ∇φ.
  4. Substituting into ∇·𝐮 = 0 gives the Laplace equation ∇²φ = 0 — the governing equation for waves.
  5. Conservation of momentum (Euler) + irrotationality + gravity body force → unsteady Bernoulli: ∂φ/∂t + p/ρ + ½|∇φ|² + gz = C(t).
  6. Apply three boundary conditions (BBC at z = −h; FSKBC and FSDBC at z = η).
  7. Linearize around small wave amplitude (a/L ≪ 1, a/h ≪ 1) — drop quadratic velocity terms; evaluate FS BCs at z = 0 instead of z = η.
  8. Separate variables φ(x,z,t) = f(θ)·Z(z) and apply periodicity → solution φ = (ag/ω)·[cosh k(z+h)/cosh kh]·sin(kx − ωt).
  9. Plug solution into combined surface BC → dispersion relation ω² = gk·tanh(kh).
  10. Differentiate φ → velocity, pressure; integrate → energy, group velocity, power.
  11. Apply conservation of energy flux between two depths → shoaling & refraction.
  12. Sum random Fourier components → spectra; integrate amplitudes² → statistics (Rayleigh).
  13. Combine inviscid pressure-gradient force + viscous drag → Morison equation on slender cylinders.

Every formula in this course is one of the steps above. If you can recite this 13-step ladder and identify which step a question is asking about, you can solve any problem.

🎚️ The Three Approximations (assumed throughout)

  1. Inviscid (μ = 0): Viscosity is negligible everywhere except near boundaries — justifies Bernoulli and potential flow.
  2. Incompressible (ρ = const): Water is essentially incompressible at wave pressures.
  3. Irrotational (∇×𝐮 = 0): Required to define a velocity potential. True if started from rest with no shear-generating body forces — gravity (the only body force) is conservative, so it cannot generate vorticity.

Plus the linearization: Wave amplitude is small compared to wavelength and depth (a/L ≪ 1, a/h ≪ 1), so we can drop second-order terms (e.g., ½|∇φ|² in Bernoulli; u·∂η/∂x in FSKBC).

When you watch surfers ride a wave, every part of what you see — the cresting shape, the orbital motion of foam, the increasing height as the wave reaches shore — is captured by linear theory. Even the fact that energy reaches the beach before the visible wave does is encoded in c_g = c/2 in deep water (group velocity = half the phase speed).

⏱️ Constancy of the Wave Period

Key principle: As a wave propagates from deep water into shallow water, its period T does not change (and therefore neither does ω). What changes is the wavelength L (and thus k, and the celerity c).

Why? Period is set by the source (the wind, storm, or earthquake) and is a property carried with the wave. Conservation of crests across a stationary observer requires T = const.

This is why every problem starts by computing ω from the given period — once you know ω, you use the dispersion relation ω² = gk·tanh(kh) to find k (and hence L) at any depth.

🌐 Reference-Frame Trick (deriving the traveling wave)

In a moving (surfer) frame, the wave is stationary: η(x′) = (H/2) cos(kx′).

In a fixed frame, the surfer (and the wave) moves at speed c: x = ct + x′, so x′ = x − ct.

η(x,t) = (H/2)·cos[k(x − ct)] = (H/2)·cos(kx − kc·t) = (H/2)·cos(kx − ωt) where ω = kc, equivalently c = ω/k = L/T.

This single derivation shows why all three forms — η = (H/2)cos(kx−ωt), η = (H/2)cos[2π(x/L − t/T)], η = (H/2)cos k(x − ct) — are equivalent.

📐 Order-of-Magnitude Estimates (used in linearization)

If the length scale is H (wave height) and the time scale is T (period):

These estimates justify dropping the ½|∇φ|² term (second-order in H) compared to ∂φ/∂t (first-order in H) when linearizing Bernoulli at the free surface.

The "constancy of wave period" is why we can put a wave gauge anywhere — at the buoy, near the shore, in the surf zone — and report a single peak period T_p. The wavelength may be 200 m offshore and 60 m near shore, but T = 10 s everywhere.

↔️ Dispersive vs. Non-Dispersive

Dispersive: phase speed depends on wavenumber/period. Different waves get sorted by speed. Deep-water gravity waves are dispersive: c = √(g/k), so longer waves (smaller k) move faster.

Non-dispersive: speed is independent of k. Shallow-water waves are non-dispersive: c = √(gh) depends only on depth.

Why this matters: Swells far from a storm are sorted versions of the original chaotic sea — long-period swells arrive first because they traveled fastest. This is why surfers know a "long-period swell" means clean, organized waves.

🔁 Traveling vs. Standing Waves

Traveling wave: Profile moves through the water — η = (H/2) cos(kx − ωt). Most ocean surface waves.

Standing wave: Profile oscillates in place — η = H·cos(kx)·cos(ωt). Result of two equal-and-opposite traveling waves superposed:

cos(kx − ωt) + cos(kx + ωt) = 2·cos(kx)·cos(ωt)

Standing waves arise from reflection in enclosed basins → seiches. The basin length B fits a whole number of half-wavelengths: B = n·L/2, giving period T = 2B / (n·√(gh)).

🌬️ Wind-Wave Prediction (JONSWAP, fetch & duration limited)

Wave growth from wind depends on wind speed U (at 10 m), fetch F, and duration t. Three nondimensional groups:

gH_s/U² = 0.0016·(gF/U²)^(1/2) [eq 1: H_s vs F] gT_p/U = 0.2857·(gF/U²)^(1/3) [eq 2: T_p vs F] gt_min/U = 68.8·(gF/U²)^(2/3) [eq 3: minimum duration]

Decision tree:

  1. Compute t_min from eq 3 using the actual fetch F.
  2. If actual duration t ≥ t_min → fetch-limited. Use eqs 1 & 2 directly with F.
  3. If t < t_min → duration-limited. Use eq 3 backward to find an "equivalent fetch" F_eq from t, then plug F_eq into eqs 1 & 2.
  4. If both F and t are huge, the result is capped by the fully-developed sea limits (gH_s/U² ≈ 0.243, gT_p/U ≈ 8.13).

Worked example (HW # 2 / midterm review): U = 35.8 m/s, F = 250 km, t = 30 hr → duration-limited → H_s ≈ 9.1 m, T_p ≈ 12.9 s.

NOAA's WAVEWATCH III model uses essentially these equations integrated over the global ocean grid every 3 hours, producing the wave forecasts surfers, shippers, and offshore-rig operators rely on daily.

Key identity: ∇·(∇×𝐀) = 0 (divergence of a curl is always zero) — so if 𝐮 = ∇φ, then ∇×𝐮 = ∇×(∇φ) ≡ 0 automatically. Irrotationality is the condition for φ to exist.

🔄 Leibniz Rule (Reynolds Transport Theorem)

The "rate of change of an integral over a moving (material) volume":

D/Dt ∫∫∫_V(t) F dV = ∫∫∫_V(t) ∂F/∂t dV + ∮∮_S(t) F (𝐮·𝐧̂) dS ↑ ↑ ↑ Rate of change Local unsteadiness Flux through moving surface

The surface integral is converted to a volume integral using the divergence theorem: ∮∮ 𝐅·𝐧̂ dS = ∫∫∫ ∇·𝐅 dV.

This is how we convert mass conservation D/Dt(∫ρ dV) = 0 into the differential continuity equation.

🪐 The Three Axioms (Conservation Laws)

  1. Conservation of mass: D/Dt ∫_V(t) ρ dV = 0
  2. Conservation of momentum: D/Dt ∫_V(t) ρ𝐮 dV = ∑forces (body + surface)
  3. Conservation of energy: D/Dt ∫_V(t) ρ(e + ½|𝐮|²) dV = work + heat

Apply mass conservation + Leibniz + divergence theorem. The integrand must vanish (because the volume V is arbitrary), giving the differential form:

∂ρ/∂t + ∇·(ρ𝐮) = 0 [continuity, general] ∇·𝐮 = 0 [continuity, incompressible]

📜 Unsteady Bernoulli Equation

Starting from the inviscid (Euler) momentum equation, with body force 𝐅 = −∇(gz) (gravity), and assuming irrotational flow 𝐮 = ∇φ:

∂φ/∂t + p/ρ + ½|∇φ|² + gz = C(t)

Three assumptions for unsteady Bernoulli:

  1. Inviscid flow (no viscous shear forces).
  2. Irrotational flow (∇×𝐮 = 0, so 𝐮 = ∇φ exists).
  3. Conservative body force (gravity only, derivable from a potential 𝐅 = −∇(gz)).

Note: Unsteady Bernoulli is derived from the momentum equation, not continuity. Continuity (∇²φ = 0) is solved separately and first for φ; pressure is then obtained from Bernoulli once φ is known. Function C(t) is absorbed into φ.

🚧 The Three Boundary Conditions

1. Bottom Boundary Condition (BBC) — Kinematic: No flow through the impermeable bottom at z = −h.

w |_{z=-h} = ∂φ/∂z |_{z=-h} = 0

2. Free-Surface Kinematic Boundary Condition (FSKBC): The vertical velocity of a particle on the surface equals the rate of change of the surface itself (particle stays on surface).

w |_{z=η} = Dη/Dt = ∂η/∂t + u·∂η/∂x [exact] ∂φ/∂z |_{z=η} = ∂η/∂t + (∂φ/∂x)(∂η/∂x)

3. Free-Surface Dynamic Boundary Condition (FSDBC): Pressure on the free surface equals atmospheric pressure (gauge pressure = 0). From Bernoulli at z = η with p = p_atm = 0 (gauge):

∂φ/∂t |_{z=η} + ½(|∇φ|²) |_{z=η} + gη = 0 [exact]

📏 Linearization (small-amplitude approximation)

Assume a/L ≪ 1, a/h ≪ 1. Then particle velocities (∼ a·ω) are small compared to celerity (∼ ω/k), so:

  • Drop the quadratic term ½|∇φ|² in Bernoulli (it is O(a²) compared to O(a)).
  • Drop u·∂η/∂x in FSKBC (product of two small quantities).
  • Evaluate FS BCs at z = 0 instead of z = η (Taylor-expand around z = 0, keep only zeroth order).

Linearized governing equations:

∇²φ = 0 (Laplace, valid for −h ≤ z ≤ 0) ∂φ/∂z |_{z=-h} = 0 (BBC) ∂φ/∂z |_{z=0} = ∂η/∂t (FSKBC, linearized) ∂φ/∂t |_{z=0} + g·η = 0 (FSDBC, linearized)

Combined surface BC (eliminate η): Differentiate FSDBC with respect to t and substitute FSKBC for ∂η/∂t:

∂²φ/∂t² + g·∂φ/∂z = 0 at z = 0

This is the single equation that, with periodicity, determines the dispersion relation (next topic).

Every numerical wave solver (CFD, FUNWAVE, OpenFOAM, etc.) starts from these same equations. Linear theory assumes a/L ≪ 1; for steep, breaking, or strongly nonlinear waves, you keep more terms — that's how Stokes second-order, fifth-order, and stream-function theories are built.

🔬 Solution by Separation of Variables

Assume φ(x,z,t) = f(θ)·Z(z) where θ = kx − ωt. Substitute into k²(∂²φ/∂θ²) + ∂²φ/∂z² = 0:

−k²f''(θ)/f(θ) = Z''(z)/Z(z) = λ² (separation constant) Solutions: f(θ) = C·sin(θ) (after periodicity → λ = k, n = 1) Z(z) = B₁·cosh[k(z+h)]/sinh(kh) → use BBC: Z'(−h)=0

Apply the dynamic surface BC ∂φ/∂t|_{z=0} = −g·η, with η = a·cos θ, to determine the constant. Final result:

Velocity Potential — THE CENTRAL RESULT
φ(x,z,t) = +(a·g/ω) · [cosh k(z+h) / cosh kh] · sin(kx − ωt)

🌊 The Dispersion Relation

Substitute the solution into the combined surface BC ω²(∂²φ/∂θ²) + g(∂φ/∂z) = 0 at z = 0:

Dispersion Relation
ω² = g·k·tanh(k·h)

Equivalent forms:

  • c² = (g/k)·tanh(kh)
  • L = (g·T²/2π)·tanh(2πh/L) ← solve iteratively for L given T and h

Iteration recipe (from Lecture 13):

  1. Start with deep-water guess L₀ = 1.56·T².
  2. Compute new L = (g·T²/2π)·tanh(2π·h / L_recent).
  3. Replace L_recent with new L. Repeat until |L_new − L_old| < tolerance (e.g., 10⁻⁵ m).
  4. Typically converges in 5–10 iterations.

🌀 Particle Kinematics

Velocities (differentiate φ):

u = ∂φ/∂x = (πH/T) · [cosh k(z+h) / sinh kh] · cos(kx − ωt) w = ∂φ/∂z = (πH/T) · [sinh k(z+h) / sinh kh] · sin(kx − ωt)

Accelerations (differentiate again):

a_x = ∂u/∂t = +(2π²H/T²) · [cosh k(z+h) / sinh kh] · sin(kx − ωt) a_z = ∂w/∂t = −(2π²H/T²) · [sinh k(z+h) / sinh kh] · cos(kx − ωt)

Phase relationships:

  • η ↔ u: in phase (largest u under crest, max forward).
  • η ↔ w: 90° out of phase (w = 0 under crest; max upward at zero up-crossing in front).
  • u ↔ a_x: 90° out of phase (drag & inertia separated → key to Morison equation in Topic 7).

Particle paths: Integrating velocity over time gives an ellipse:

(x − x₀)²/A² + (z − z₀)²/B² = 1 A(z₀) = (H/2)·cosh k(z₀+h)/sinh kh (horizontal semi-axis) B(z₀) = (H/2)·sinh k(z₀+h)/sinh kh (vertical semi-axis)
  • Deep water: A = B = (H/2)·e^{kz₀} → circular orbits that decay exponentially with depth (4.3% of surface value at z = −L/2).
  • Shallow water: A = (H/2)·(1/kh) (constant with depth), B = (H/2)·(z₀+h)/h (linear, zero at bottom) → flattened ellipses; particles slide back and forth near the bottom.

💧 Pressure under Waves

From linearized unsteady Bernoulli: p = −ρ(∂φ/∂t) − ρgz:

Total Pressure
p(x,z,t) = −ρgz + ρgη·[cosh k(z+h) / cosh kh]

p_hydrostatic = −ρgz = ρg·(h − s)   where s = depth below MWL
p_dynamic = ρgη·[cosh k(z+h) / cosh kh]

Pressure-response factor K_p(z) = cosh k(z+h)/cosh kh decays with depth — that's why a pressure gauge near the bottom sees a smaller wave-induced pressure than the actual surface elevation would suggest.

Pressure-gauge inversion (typical exam problem): Given p_meas at depth z below MWL, frequency f, find the surface H:

  1. p_dynamic = p_meas − p_hydrostatic = p_meas − ρg|z|.
  2. ω = 2π·f. Deep water (if h is "deep"): k = ω²/g.
  3. η_max = (p_dyn / ρg) · cosh kh / cosh k(z+h) = a → H = 2a.

Worked example (HW # 7 / midterm review): Gauge at z = −10 m in deep water; f = 0.08 Hz; max p = 125 kPa. → p_h = 100 kPa, p_dyn = 25 kPa, ω = 0.503 rad/s, k = ω²/g = 0.0258 → L = 244 m → H = 5 m.

⚡ Wave Energy & Power

Potential energy per unit area (averaged over a wavelength, subtracting the reference state):

dE_P/dA = ½·ρg·η̄² = (1/4)·ρ·g·a²

dE_K/dA = (1/4)·ρ·g·a²

Total energy density (equipartition: KE = PE):

Wave Energy Density

Energy flux (wave power per unit crest length):

Wave Power

Group velocity:

Group Velocity (general)
c_g = dω/dk = (1/2)·c · [1 + 2kh/sinh(2kh)] = n·c
Deep water: c_g = c/2 (n = 1/2)
Shallow water: c_g = c (n = 1)

Why group velocity? Wave energy travels at c_g, not c. That's why ripples move backward through a wave packet and why a tsunami's leading wave (small disturbance) reaches shore after the energy front in some cases. In deep water, you'd see individual crests moving twice as fast as the wave packet itself.

c₀ = 1.56·T = 15.6 m/s c_g = 7.8 m/s P = (1/2)·1000·1²·9.81 · 7.8 ≈ 38.2 kW/m

Topic 5: Wave Transformation (Shoaling, Refraction, Breaking, Diffraction, Reflection)

📌 Source: Lectures 16–17 · HW # 9

As a wave moves from deep into shallow water, it changes height, direction, and may break. These transformations are derived by conserving energy flux (power) and wave period.

🌊 Wave Shoaling (height change at normal incidence)

Assumptions:

  • Waves propagate perpendicular to straight, parallel depth contours (no refraction).
  • Depth varies slowly (no reflection).
  • Energy doesn't propagate across orthogonals.
  • No breaking, no friction.
  • Period T (and ω) are unchanged.

Energy flux conservation: P₁ = P₂, i.e., (E·c_g)₁ = (E·c_g)₂. Since E = (1/8)ρgH²:

Shoaling coefficient K_s
H/H₀ = √(c_g0 / c_g) = K_s

Pattern: As h decreases, c_g first slightly increases (so H decreases) before plummeting and forcing H to grow rapidly toward breaking.

↩️ Wave Refraction (Snell's Law)

When a wave approaches the coast at an angle, the part in shallow water slows down while the part still in deeper water keeps moving — the crest bends to align more nearly parallel to the bottom contours.

Same derivation as Snell's law in optics — period (frequency) is conserved across the depth change:

Snell's Law for Waves
sin θ₁ / c₁ = sin θ₂ / c₂ (angles measured from the orthogonal to depth contour)
Equivalently: k₁ sin θ₁ = k₂ sin θ₂

Combined shoaling + refraction (power between two orthogonal lines is conserved):

Combined Refraction-Shoaling
H₁²·n₁·c₁·cos θ₁ = H₂²·n₂·c₂·cos θ₂

H/H₀ = √(c_g0·cos θ₀ / (c_g·cos θ)) = K_s · K_r

where K_r = √(cos θ₀ / cos θ) is the refraction coefficient.

Worked example (HW # 9 / Lecture 17): T = 10 s wave, H = 2 m at h = 20 m, θ = 40°. At h = 5 m: solve dispersion → k = 0.094, sin θ = (0.055/0.094)·sin 40° → θ = 22°; combined → H ≈ 2.07 m.

💥 Wave Breaking

Linear theory predicts the wave keeps growing as it shoals — but real waves break when the particle velocity at the crest equals the celerity (u_max ≈ c).

Miche criterion (general):

Maximum steepness
(H/L)_max = 0.142 · tanh(kh)

Limits:

  • Deep water: (H₀/L₀)_max = 0.142 ≈ 1/7 → "white-capping" steepness limit.
  • Shallow water (regular waves): (H/h)_max ≈ 0.78–0.88 (the "depth-limited breaking" rule of thumb).
  • Shallow water (irregular waves, design): (H_s / h)_max ≈ 0.5.

Estimating breaker height H_b at depth h_b ≈ (5/3)H₀:

H_b = H₀ · √(c₀ / c_b) = H₀ · √(c₀ / √(g·h_b)) (using c_b = √(g·h_b) since shallow at breakpoint)

This appears in the regional-forecast exercise: highest breaker often comes from the longest period (deepest h_b, slowest c_b, largest shoaling coefficient).

Iribarren number ξ classifies breaker type:

Iribarren Number

Cut-off values vary slightly between references (e.g., Battjes 1974 vs. CEM); always confirm which convention is intended.

🎯 Diffraction

When a wave encounters a barrier with a gap or a sharp corner (e.g., a breakwater), wave energy spreads into the geometric shadow. This is the same physics as light diffracting through a slit.

  • Without diffraction, energy would be discontinuous behind the barrier — impossible.
  • Wave height in the shadow zone is reduced — characterized by a diffraction coefficient K_d < 1.
  • Important when D/L > 0.2: A structure of size D large compared to wavelength L significantly diffracts the waves — must use diffraction theory (not just Morison) for wave forces on large bodies (gravity-base platforms, ship hulls, monopiles in long waves).

↩️ Reflection & Seiches

A wave that fully reflects off a vertical wall produces a standing wave with twice the original amplitude:

cos(kx − ωt) + cos(kx + ωt) = 2·cos(kx)·cos(ωt)

Nodes (where η = 0 always) occur every L/2; antinodes (max η) also every L/2, offset.

Seiche: a resonant standing wave in an enclosed basin (lake, harbor). The basin length B fits n half-wavelengths: B = n·L/2 → L = 2B/n. With c = √(gh) (shallow):

Seiche period (Merian's formula)
T = 2B / (n·√(gh))

Example (Lake Baikal): B = 636 km, h̄ = 744 m → T₁ ≈ 14,900 s ≈ 4 hours.

Submerged breakwaters reflect part of the wave energy and dissipate some, reducing nearshore wave heights.

Coastal engineers use shoaling/refraction maps to identify "energy hotspots" along a shore. Underwater ridges focus wave energy (orthogonals converge), creating dangerous high waves at "rip" points; bays are zones of energy defocusing (orthogonals diverge), making them naturally calmer. Surfers know this intuitively — they look for headlands and reefs that focus energy.

Topic 7: Wave Forces on Structures (Morison Equation)

📌 Source: Lectures 20–21

For slender structures (D/L < 0.15–0.2), the wave is barely affected by the structure (no diffraction) — but the structure feels two forces from the moving water: drag (in phase with velocity) and inertia (in phase with acceleration).

⚙️ Three Force Mechanisms

1. Drag (steady-flow analog, viscous origin):

F_D = (1/2)·C_D·ρ·A·U² (steady) F_D(t) = (1/2)·C_D·ρ·A·u(t)·|u(t)| (oscillating; quadratic but signed)

The |u|·u keeps F_D in the direction of motion (so it reverses every half period).

2. Added Mass (body accelerating in quiescent fluid): The body must push surrounding water out of the way; the reaction force is opposite to acceleration.

F_a(t) = ρ·V·C_a·dU₀/dt where C_a is the added-mass coefficient (< 1 for cylinder)

3. Froude-Krylov (pressure-gradient force on a body in unsteady undisturbed flow): The undisturbed pressure gradient already accelerating the fluid acts on the body's volume.

F_FK(t) = ρ·V·dU₀/dt

Combined inertia force = added mass + Froude-Krylov:

F_I(t) = ρ·V·(1 + C_a)·dU₀/dt = ρ·V·C_M·dU₀/dt where C_M = 1 + C_a is the inertia coefficient (< 2 for cylinder).

🧮 Morison Equation

For a vertical cylinder (diameter D), the force per unit length is the sum of drag + inertia:

Morison Equation (force per unit length)
f(t) = C_M · ρ · (πD²/4) · (∂u/∂t) + C_D · (1/2) · ρ · D · u(t)·|u(t)|

Inertia term ↑ Drag term ↑
(∝ acceleration, ∝ D²) (∝ velocity², ∝ D)

Phase shift: u(t) and ∂u/∂t are 90° out of phase, so drag and inertia maximize at different times of the wave cycle. The total force has a complex shape — neither pure cosine nor pure sine.

📐 Re & KC Numbers — When Drag vs. Inertia Dominates

The coefficients C_D and C_M depend on:

  • Reynolds number Re = u_max·D/ν (ν = kinematic viscosity ≈ 10⁻⁶ m²/s for seawater).
  • Keulegan-Carpenter number KC = u_max·T/D = horizontal water displacement per period / diameter. In deep water at the surface, u_max = πH/T, so KC = πH/D.
  • Roughness ε/D (marine growth on the cylinder).

Drag-vs-inertia ratio:

F_D / F_I = (1/π)·(C_D/C_M)·KC ≈ KC/20 (taking C_D/C_M ≈ 1)
KC rangeRegimeWhat dominates
KC < 3Inertia-dominatedUse F = C_M·ρ·(πD²/4)·∂u/∂t only
3 < KC < 15Inertia + linearizable dragBoth, drag can be linearized
15 < KC < 45Both important; transitionalFull Morison, nonlinear drag
KC > 45Drag-dominatedF ≈ (1/2)C_D·ρ·D·u·|u|

Worked example (Lecture 21): D = 1 m jacket leg, H = 5 m, T = 10 s, h = 60 m (deep). At surface u_max = πH/T = 1.57 m/s.

KC = πH/D = π·5/1 ≈ 15.7 → inertia + drag, both important Re = 1.57·1/(10⁻⁶) ≈ 1.6 million → fully turbulent

📏 Total Force on a Vertical Pile (integrate over depth)

For the full pile from z = −h to z = η ≈ 0:

F_total(t) = ∫_{-h}^{0} f(z,t) dz = C_M·ρ·(πD²/4)·∫(∂u/∂t)dz + C_D·(1/2)·ρ·D·∫u·|u| dz

For deep water, where u and ∂u/∂t carry e^(kz):

∫_{-∞}^{0} e^(kz) dz = 1/k F_inertia,max = C_M·ρ·(πD²/4)·(2π²H/T²)·(1/k) = (C_M·ρ·g·πD²·H/4)·(1/2) F_drag,max = C_D·(1/2)·ρ·D·(πH/T)²·(1/2k) ·(scale factor)

Compute each component, then combine using time-stepping (since they're 90° out of phase, peak total ≠ peak inertia + peak drag).

🚧 When Morison FAILS

  • D/L > 0.2: The structure is no longer slender; diffraction matters. Use diffraction theory (e.g., MacCamy-Fuchs) or BEM/CFD instead.
  • Sharp-edged bodies: Vortex shedding and flow separation patterns are different from circular cylinders.
  • Resonance: If wave period coincides with structural natural period, dynamic amplification factors apply.
  • Marine growth: Roughness can shift C_D and C_M significantly (sometimes 50%+).
Every offshore wind turbine monopile, every oil-platform jacket leg, every pier piling is designed using Morison's equation. API and DNV codes specify standard C_D and C_M values along with safety factors. Coastal structures (mooring posts, navigational buoys) use it too. Knowing when Morison breaks down (D/L > 0.2) is what makes a great ocean engineer.

MCQ 1 — Constancy of Period
A wave with period T = 10 s and wavelength L₀ = 156 m in deep water moves into shallow water of depth h = 2 m. The new period is approximately:

(C) 14 s
(D) Depends on the wave height

Answer: (A) 10 s

The wave period is set by the source and is invariant as a wave moves through changing depths. What changes is the wavelength (and thus the celerity). Here L drops from 156 m to about 44.3 m and c drops from 15.6 m/s to 4.43 m/s, but T = 10 s throughout.

MCQ 2 — Deep-Water Test
A wave has T = 8 s. The minimum water depth at which it can be considered "deep water" is approximately:
(A) 8 m
(B) 25 m
(C) 50 m
(D) 100 m
Show Solution

Answer: (C) 50 m

L₀ = 1.56·T² = 1.56·64 ≈ 100 m. Deep-water criterion h ≥ L₀/2, so h ≥ 50 m.

MCQ 3 — Group Velocity in Deep Water
In deep water, the group velocity c_g equals:
(A) 2c
(B) c
(C) c/2
(D) c/4
Show Solution

Answer: (C) c/2

In deep water, n = ½, so c_g = (½)·c. Energy travels at half the phase speed, which is why ripples appear to move backward through a packet — the packet (energy) is being "left behind" by the individual crests.

MCQ 4 — Shallow Water Particle Velocity
In shallow water, the horizontal particle velocity u as a function of depth z is:
(A) Maximum at the bottom, zero at the surface
(B) Decreases exponentially with depth
(C) Approximately constant from surface to bottom
(D) Reverses direction at z = −h/2
Show Solution

Answer: (C) Constant with depth

In shallow water (kh → 0), cosh k(z+h)/sinh kh → 1/(kh), independent of z. So u = (πH/T)·(1/kh)·cos(kx−ωt) — uniform from surface to bottom. Vertical velocity w decreases linearly to zero at the bottom.

MCQ 5 — Particle Path in Deep Water
In deep water, a fluid particle traces:
(A) A straight horizontal line
(B) A circle that decays exponentially with depth
(C) An ellipse flattening near the bottom
(D) A figure-eight pattern
Show Solution

Answer: (B) Circular orbit, exponential decay

In deep water A = B = (H/2)·e^(kz), so the orbit is a circle. At z = −L/2 the radius is reduced to e^(−π) ≈ 4.3% of the surface value. This is why submarines below ~L/2 don't feel surface waves.

MCQ 6 — Energy Density
The total wave energy density per unit horizontal area for a regular wave is:
(A) ρgH²/4
(B) ρga²
(C) ρgH²/8 = ρga²/2
(D) ρgH/2

Answer: (C) ρgH²/8 = ρga²/2

Equipartition: KE = PE = ρga²/4 each, total E = ρga²/2 = ρgH²/8 (since a = H/2). Units: J/m².

MCQ 7 — Wave Power Scaling
If the wave height H is doubled (with all else constant), the wave power per unit width increases by a factor of:
(A) 2
(B) 4
(C) 8
(D) 16
Show Solution

Answer: (B) 4×

P = (1/8)ρgH²·c_g. With H → 2H: P → 4P. This quadratic dependence is why "rough seas" can deliver enormously more power than calm ones — a 4 m sea is 16× more powerful than a 1 m sea.

MCQ 8 — Linearization Assumption
Linear wave theory drops the term ½|∇φ|² in unsteady Bernoulli because:
(A) The flow is steady
(B) The fluid is inviscid
(C) It is second-order in amplitude (small compared to ∂φ/∂t)
(D) Gravity is the only body force
Show Solution

Answer: (C) Second-order in amplitude

∂φ/∂t = O(a) but |∇φ|² = O(a²). For small a, the quadratic term is much smaller, so we drop it. This is the essence of linearization (also why FS BCs are evaluated at z = 0 rather than z = η).

MCQ 9 — Velocity Potential Existence
A velocity potential φ such that 𝐮 = ∇φ can be defined when the flow is:
(A) Steady
(B) Inviscid
(C) Incompressible
(D) Irrotational (∇×𝐮 = 0)
Show Solution

Answer: (D) Irrotational

The vector identity ∇×(∇φ) ≡ 0 means any potential flow is automatically irrotational; conversely, ∇×𝐮 = 0 is the necessary and sufficient condition for 𝐮 to be expressible as ∇φ. Inviscid + started-from-rest implies irrotational by Kelvin's theorem.

MCQ 10 — Snell's Law for Waves
A wave with deep-water angle θ₀ = 30° propagates into shallower water where the celerity halves. The new angle θ from the depth-contour normal is:

Answer: (C) ~14.5°

MCQ 11 — Breaker Type

MCQ 16 — Reflection & Standing Wave

(A) 0
(B) a
(C) 2a
(D) a²
Show Solution

Answer: (C) 2a

MCQ 17 — Morison Drag vs. Inertia
In Morison's equation, drag and inertia force terms are 90° out of phase because:
(A) Drag is always positive but inertia changes sign
(B) Drag depends on u while inertia depends on ∂u/∂t
(C) Drag is dominant only in shallow water
(D) Inertia coefficient C_M is always 2
Show Solution

Answer: (B) Drag ∝ u, Inertia ∝ ∂u/∂t

For sinusoidal motion u = U₀cos ωt, ∂u/∂t = −U₀ω·sin ωt. Cosine and sine are 90° apart. So peak drag and peak inertia occur at different phases of the wave; the total maximum is found by phase-stepping or vector addition.

MCQ 19 — When Diffraction Matters

Common Misconceptions

❌ Misconception 1: "The wave period changes when the wave moves into shallow water"
FALSE. The period T is invariant — it's set by the source and conserved by counting crests at any fixed point. What changes are L (wavelength) and c (celerity). Always start a problem by computing ω = 2π/T and treat it as constant. Then use ω² = gk·tanh(kh) to find the local k (and L).
❌ Misconception 2: "Wave energy travels at the phase velocity c"
FALSE. Wave energy travels at the group velocity c_g. In deep water c_g = c/2 — half the phase speed! That's why a swell from a distant storm reaches you long before any individual wave does (well, the waves arrive at c, but new ones constantly emerge at the front and disappear at the back; the energy front moves at c_g). Wave power P = E·c_g, never E·c.
❌ Misconception 3: "Particles travel with the wave (mass transport)"
MOSTLY FALSE. For a regular linear wave, particles trace closed elliptical orbits and return to their starting position every period — no net transport. Stokes drift (a second-order effect) does cause a small mean transport, but it's tiny (~(a/L)² × c) and not part of linear theory. The famous quote: "waves transmit energy, not mass." A floating cork bobs in place; it doesn't ride the wave to shore (until it breaks).
❌ Misconception 4: "Wave amplitude is the same as wave height"
FALSE. H = wave height = crest-to-trough = 2a, where a = amplitude (the half-height from MWL to crest). Significant wave height H_s is yet another beast — average of the highest 1/3 wave heights in a record. Be careful: most engineering formulas are written in terms of H, but pressure-gauge problems often give amplitude a directly.
❌ Misconception 5: "Linear theory breaks down in shallow water"
PARTIALLY FALSE. Linear theory still works in shallow water as long as the amplitude is small compared to the depth (a/h ≪ 1). The deep/shallow classification is about kh, not about whether linear theory applies. Linear theory does break down when waves get steep — near breaking — at any depth.
❌ Misconception 6: "Velocity potential exists for all flows"
FALSE. A scalar φ such that 𝐮 = ∇φ exists only for irrotational flow (∇×𝐮 = 0). Most real-world fluid flows are rotational (turbulence, viscous boundary layers); we use potential flow only because waves are essentially inviscid + started-from-rest, so Kelvin's theorem says they stay irrotational.
❌ Misconception 7: "Drag and inertia in Morison can be added at peak"
FALSE. Drag (∝ u) and inertia (∝ ∂u/∂t) are 90° out of phase, so their peaks happen at different times. The maximum total force is found by phase-stepping, NOT by F_max = F_D,max + F_I,max. The actual peak total is roughly √(F_D² + F_I²) (when forces are balanced) — much less than the sum.
❌ Misconception 8: "Significant wave height H_s is the maximum wave"
FALSE. H_s is the average of the highest one-third of waves — it's a representative "what your eye sees" measurement. The actual H_max can be substantially higher (typically 1.6×–2× larger than H_s in long records). H_max = H_rms·√(ln N) gives a useful estimate based on Rayleigh statistics.
❌ Misconception 9: "Tsunamis are 'tidal waves'"
FALSE. Tsunamis are caused by seismic events (earthquakes, undersea landslides), NOT tidal forces. They have very long wavelengths (100s of km) and behave as shallow-water waves even in 4-km-deep ocean (because L ≫ h). Their celerity c = √(gh) makes them travel at jet-airliner speeds (~700 km/h in deep ocean). Tide waves are a totally separate phenomenon driven by Sun and Moon.
❌ Misconception 10: "All spectra are Pierson-Moskowitz"
FALSE. PM is for fully developed seas in the open ocean (one parameter, U). Use Bretschneider for fully developed seas with two parameters (H_s, T_p). Use JONSWAP for fetch-limited growing seas (e.g., the North Sea). Picking the wrong spectrum systematically biases your design loads.
The deepest pitfall in wave mechanics is forgetting that linear theory is an approximation. It works beautifully for swells in 50 m water, but starts to creak in extreme storms (steep waves), at the breakpoint (nonlinearities dominate), and around large structures (diffraction). Always do a sanity check on your assumptions.

The Hardest Parts Explained

🔹 1. Iterating the Dispersion Relation L = (gT²/2π)·tanh(2πh/L)

Why it's hard: L appears on both sides; algebra fails. Many students freeze.

Strategy:

  1. Always start with the deep-water guess L₀ = 1.56·T². If h > L₀/2, you're done — use L₀.
  2. Otherwise, evaluate the right-hand side: L_new = 1.56·T²·tanh(2πh/L_old).
  3. Set L_old = L_new and repeat.
  4. If iterations oscillate, take the average of the last two and continue.
  5. Typically converges in 5–10 iterations to within 1 m. Show 4–5 iterations on the exam to demonstrate the method.

Pro tip: If your scientific calculator has SOLVE/Numeric Solver, you can bypass iteration entirely. Otherwise, draw a small table of L vs. iteration number — it earns method points even if your final number is slightly off.

🔹 2. Knowing Which Regime to Use (Deep, Intermediate, Shallow)

Why it's hard: Picking the wrong regime gives a wrong answer; picking the right one but using the wrong formulas gives the wrong answer too.

Workflow:

  1. Compute L₀ = 1.56·T² (always start here).
  2. Form the ratio h/L₀ (NOT h/L — you don't know L yet).
  3. If h/L₀ > 1/2 (kh > π): deep water. Use L = L₀, c = c₀, c_g = c₀/2. Done.
  4. If h/L₀ < 1/20 (kh < π/10): shallow water. Use c = √(gh), L = T√(gh), c_g = c. Done.
  5. Otherwise (intermediate): iterate the dispersion relation. Use the general formulas with the full tanh and n = ½[1 + 2kh/sinh(2kh)].

Common error: Students compute h/L₀, see it's "small" (e.g., 0.1), and go straight to shallow water. But 0.1 is in the intermediate range — you can't use shallow-water formulas. Always check both bounds.

🔹 3. Linearizing Boundary Conditions

Why it's hard: Two simultaneous moves: (a) drop quadratic terms; (b) evaluate at z = 0 instead of z = η. Each requires order-of-magnitude reasoning.

Mental model: Treat amplitude a as a small parameter. Anything quadratic in a (like (∂φ/∂x)² or u·∂η/∂x) is "second order" and dropped. The Taylor expansion ∂φ/∂z|_{z=η} = ∂φ/∂z|_{z=0} + η·∂²φ/∂z²|_{z=0} + ... has each successive term smaller by O(a/L). Keep the first; drop the rest.

Show your work: When asked to derive the linearized BC, explicitly state "since a/L ≪ 1, terms of order (a/L)² are negligible compared to terms of order a/L." This earns full credit on conceptual derivation questions.

🔹 4. Pressure-Gauge Inversion (back-solving for H from p_meas)

Why it's hard: Multiple steps with different formulas: subtract hydrostatic, find ω and k, then use the depth-attenuation factor cosh k(z+h)/cosh kh.

Recipe:

  1. p_dyn,max = p_meas,max − ρg·s where s = depth of gauge below MWL.
  2. ω = 2π·f. If "deep" is implied, k = ω²/g. Otherwise iterate dispersion.
  3. Compute the response factor R = cosh k(z+h)/cosh kh evaluated AT THE GAUGE depth (where z+h is the height of the gauge above the bottom).
  4. a = p_dyn,max / (ρg·R). H = 2a.

Common error: Forgetting the response factor R or evaluating it at the wrong depth. Sign convention: z is measured upward from MWL, so z is negative for the gauge.

🔹 5. Combined Shoaling + Refraction

Why it's hard: Two physical effects acting simultaneously. The combined formula H₁²·n₁·c₁·cos θ₁ = H₂²·n₂·c₂·cos θ₂ has FOUR coupled quantities (H, n, c, θ) varying with depth.

Workflow:

  1. Compute c, c_g (= n·c), and k at both depths via the dispersion relation.
  2. Use Snell to get the new direction θ at the new depth.
  3. Plug into the combined formula to get the new H.
  4. Sanity check: at normal incidence (θ₀ = θ = 0), the formula reduces to pure shoaling H/H₀ = √(c_g0/c_g).

Pro tip: Refraction usually reduces H (energy is spread over a wider front as orthogonals diverge). Shoaling usually increases H near shore. The two effects partially cancel, so the net change at moderate angles is often small.

🔹 6. Setting Up Spectral Analysis (sampling, resolution, record length)

Why it's hard: Three coupled choices (f_s, T, Δf, N) constrained by Nyquist, target peak frequency, and statistical stability.

Decision sequence:

  1. What's f_max? Highest frequency you want to resolve. Pick Nyquist: f_s ≥ 2·f_max. Add safety: f_s = 3–4·f_max.
  2. What's the smallest peak width? Pick Δf so the peak is resolved with at least 5–10 bins.
  3. Record length: T = 1/Δf. Number of samples N = T·f_s.
  4. Statistical reliability: Repeat for ≥ 20 records (or use overlapping windows for ≥ 40 effective DOF).
🔹 7. Morison: Phase-Stepping to Find Maximum Force

Why it's hard: Drag and inertia peak at different times. Beginners erroneously add F_D,max + F_I,max.

Correct approach:

  1. Write F_total(θ) = F_I,max·sin θ + F_D,max·cos θ·|cos θ|, where θ = ωt.
  2. For inertia-dominated (F_I,max ≫ F_D,max), peak is near θ = π/2 with F_max ≈ F_I,max.
  3. For drag-dominated, peak is near θ = 0 or π with F_max ≈ F_D,max.
  4. For balanced cases, find the maximum numerically: differentiate w.r.t. θ and solve. Or compute F at θ = 0, π/4, π/2 and interpolate.

Quick check: When F_D = F_I (KC ≈ 15–20), the peak is roughly √2 × max(F_D, F_I), not 2× — be skeptical of any answer that adds them directly.

The hardest parts of OE 520 are not the formulas — they're knowing which regime you're in. Master the deep/shallow/intermediate decision tree, the linearization argument, and the phase-difference idea (drag vs. inertia, energy vs. phase) and 80% of exam questions become straightforward.

Memory Anchors & Mnemonics

📌 Anchor 1: "1.56·T² and 1.56·T" — Deep Water Quick Numbers

L₀ = 1.56·T² (m) · c₀ = 1.56·T (m/s)

The constant 1.56 = g/(2π) when g = 9.81 m/s². Memorize this — it appears in nearly every problem. Quick scan: T = 10 s → L₀ = 156 m, c₀ = 15.6 m/s.

📌 Anchor 2: "1/2 and 1/20" — Deep/Intermediate/Shallow Boundaries

Deep: h/L > 1/2 · Shallow: h/L < 1/20

Mnemonic: "Half is for the half-depth (deep), twenty is for the twenty-times-shorter wave (shallow)." If 1/20 < h/L < 1/2, you're in the intermediate range and must iterate.

📌 Anchor 3: "PERIOD doesn't change" — The Golden Rule

As a wave moves through any depth change, its period T (and angular frequency ω) is conserved. Wavelength L and celerity c change. Always begin every shoaling/refraction problem by writing "ω = 2π/T = const."

📌 Anchor 4: "ENERGY travels at the GROUP velocity" — Power Rule

Wave power per unit width: P = E · c_g. NOT E·c. Power is also written as P = (1/8)ρgH²·c_g.

In deep water, c_g = c/2 — energy moves at half the phase speed. In shallow water, c_g = c — they coincide.

📌 Anchor 5: "n = ½ deep, n = 1 shallow" — Group Velocity Factor

c_g = n·c where:

  • Deep: n = ½
  • Shallow: n = 1
  • Intermediate: n = ½·[1 + 2kh/sinh(2kh)]
📌 Anchor 6: "ρgH²/8" — Wave Energy Density

E = (1/8)·ρ·g·H² = (1/2)·ρ·g·a². Quadratic in H means doubling H gives 4× the energy — a key design insight.

📌 Anchor 7: "πH/T" — Surface Particle Velocity

At the surface in deep water, the maximum horizontal velocity is u_max = πH/T (also equals w_max). This appears in the Morison KC number: KC = u_max·T/D = πH/D in deep water.

📌 Anchor 8: "exp[−(H/H_rms)²]" — Rayleigh in One Line

P(height > H) = exp[−(H/H_rms)²]. Memorize this. Plus: H_s = √2·H_rms ≈ 1.414·H_rms; H_m0 = 4√m₀.

Quick check: P(H > H_rms) = e⁻¹ ≈ 0.37. P(H > 2·H_rms) = e⁻⁴ ≈ 0.018. P(H > 3·H_rms) ≈ 0.0001 (essentially zero).

📌 Anchor 9: "FETCH or DURATION limited" — Wind Wave Decision

Compute t_min from eq 3 with given F. Compare to actual t.

  • t ≥ t_min → fetch-limited, use eqs 1 & 2 with given F.
  • t < t_min → duration-limited, use eq 3 backward to find F_eq from t, then eqs 1 & 2 with F_eq.
📌 Anchor 10: "DRAG and INERTIA, 90° apart" — Morison

F = F_I·sin(ωt) + F_D·cos(ωt)·|cos(ωt)|. The two are 90° out of phase. Maximum total ≠ F_I,max + F_D,max. Phase-step or use F_max ≈ √(F_I² + F_D²) for balanced cases.

KC threshold: KC < 3 → inertia rules; KC > 45 → drag rules; in between, both matter.

📌 Anchor 11: "0.142 = 1/7" — Deep-Water Steepness Limit

Deep-water waves break when H/L > 0.142 ≈ 1/7. In shallow water, the depth-limited rule is H/h ≈ 0.78 (regular) or 0.5 (irregular).

📌 Anchor 12: "5/3 H₀" — Breaker Depth Rule

For a wave with deep-water height H₀, the depth at which it breaks is h_b ≈ (5/3)·H₀. Useful for back-of-envelope shoaling problems and the regional-forecast exercise.

Memorize the dozen formulas above and you've got 70% of the exam in your back pocket. The remaining 30% requires understanding (which regime, which assumption, which scaling) — that's where the time you spent on derivations pays off.

🎯 Problem-Solving Workflow (use this for every FRQ)

  1. Sketch the physical situation: Draw the wave, depth, gauge, structure. Label everything.
  2. List given quantities with units (T, h, H, etc.).
  3. Identify the regime: Deep, intermediate, or shallow? Compute h/L₀ first.

❌ Common Exam Killers — Avoid These

  • Plugging numbers into a wrong-regime formula. Always verify h/L₀ first.
  • Using c instead of c_g for wave power. P = E·c_g, period.

  • Using H instead of a (or vice versa). Read the problem carefully — H = 2a.
  • Adding F_D,max + F_I,max in Morison. They're 90° out of phase!
  • Ignoring units: kN/m³ vs N/m³, mm vs m, kPa vs Pa. Off by 1000×.
  • Skipping the boundary-condition derivation. Even if asked for a "discussion" question, write the equation. You get points for recognizing the right BC.
