Exam Overview

What Does This Unit Cover?

This unit spans Lectures 14 & 15 of PEP/EE 542 (Spring 2024, Prof. Overvig) and covers the foundations of Magnetostatics — Chapter 5 of Griffiths' Introduction to Electrodynamics. It is flagged as high importance for the final exam.

Two major pillars:

  • Lecture 14 — Magnetic Force & Biot-Savart Law: The Lorentz force, current types (line, surface, volume), steady currents, charge conservation, and the Biot-Savart law as the "magnetic Coulomb's law."
  • Lecture 15 — Ampère's Law & Vector Potential: Divergence and curl of B, Ampère's law in integral and differential form, the magnetostatic Maxwell equations, and configurations solvable by Ampère's law.

The Magnetostatics Triangle

Just as electrostatics is built on ρ → E → V, magnetostatics is built on the triangle:

J → B → A (Current density → Magnetic field → Vector potential)

The goal of magnetostatics: build out this triangle completely, analogous to what we did with ρ, E, V in electrostatics.

Key Equations — The Foundation

Lorentz Force: F = q(E + v × B) Biot-Savart Law: B(r) = (μ₀I/4π) ∫ (dl' × r̂) / r² Divergence of B: ∇·B = 0 (no magnetic monopoles) Curl of B: ∇×B = μ₀J (Ampère's law) Ampère's law (int): ∮ B·dl = μ₀ I_enc Continuity Eqn: ∇·J = −∂ρ/∂t (charge conservation)

First Principles & Theory

📘 Lecture 14 — Magnetic Force & Biot-Savart Law

🔗 The Lorentz Force Law

The total electromagnetic force on a charge q moving with velocity v is:

F = q(E + v × B)

The magnetic part alone is:

F_mag = q(v × B)
  • This is an experimentally determined force law — an axiom of the theory.
  • The cross product means the force is perpendicular to both v and B.
  • Follows the right-hand rule and depends on the sign of q.
  • Can result in circular trajectories (cyclotron motion).

⚡ Magnetic Forces Do No Work

Since F_mag = q(v × B) is always perpendicular to v:

dW_mag = F_mag · dl = q(v × B) · v dt = 0

Magnetic fields cannot speed up or slow down a charged particle. They only change the direction of motion — analogous to a fulcrum or a frictionless ramp.

🔌 Current — Types and Definitions

Current I is the charge per unit time passing a given point. It is a vector: I = λv.

TypeSymbolDefinitionGeometry
Line currentI = λvCharge per unit time in wire1D — wire
Surface currentK = σv = dI/dl⊥Current per unit width2D — sheet
Volume currentJ = ρv = dI/da⊥Current per unit area3D — bulk

🔄 Steady Currents & Charge Conservation

Magnetostatics requires steady currents: charge is moving but the charge density at every point is constant in time.

∂ρ/∂t = 0 and ∂J/∂t = 0 (magnetostatic conditions) J ≠ 0 but ∂ρ/∂t = 0 (charge flows but doesn't pile up)

The continuity equation expresses local charge conservation:

∇·J = −∂ρ/∂t

In magnetostatics, since ∂ρ/∂t = 0, we get ∇·J = 0.

🧲 The Biot-Savart Law

The Biot-Savart law is the "magnetic Coulomb's law" — it gives the magnetic field due to a steady current:

Line current: B(r) = (μ₀I/4π) ∫ (dl' × r̂) / r² Surface current: B(r) = (μ₀/4π) ∫ (K × r̂) / r² da' Volume current: B(r) = (μ₀/4π) ∫ (J × r̂) / r² dτ'

Where μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ N/A² is the permeability of free space.

Key comparison:

  • Coulomb's law: E points away from dq (radial)
  • Biot-Savart law: B points perpendicular to dq·v (azimuthal)

💡 Biot-Savart Example: Long Straight Wire

For an infinite straight wire carrying current I, the field at distance s is:

B = μ₀I / (2πs) (circling the wire per the right-hand rule)

For a finite segment from angle θ₁ to θ₂:

B = (μ₀I / 4πs)(sin θ₂ − sin θ₁)

📘 Lecture 15 — Ampère's Law & Vector Potential

📐 Divergence of B: ∇·B = 0

Starting from Biot-Savart and applying the divergence, one can show:

∇·B = 0 (always — a magnetostatic Maxwell equation)

Physical meaning:

  • Magnetic field lines cannot begin or end — they must form closed loops.
  • There are no magnetic monopoles (no isolated north or south poles).
  • The magnetic flux through any closed surface is zero: ∮ B·da = 0.

🌀 Curl of B: Ampère's Law

Starting from Biot-Savart and applying the curl, one obtains:

Differential form: ∇ × B = μ₀J Integral form: ∮ B·dl = μ₀ I_enc (via Stokes' theorem)

Physical meaning: Magnetic fields exist only where there is current (in magnetostatics). Currents are the source of B.

Analogy:

  • Gauss's law (divergence theorem): ∮ E·da = Q_enc / ε₀ — symmetric Gaussian surface
  • Ampère's law (Stokes' theorem): ∮ B·dl = μ₀ I_enc — symmetric Ampèrian loop

💡 Ampère's Law Examples

Infinite solenoid (n turns/length, current I):

B = μ₀nI ẑ (inside, uniform) B = 0 (outside)

Infinite plane surface current K = K x̂ in the xy-plane:

B = +(μ₀/2)K ŷ for z < 0 B = −(μ₀/2)K ŷ for z > 0

Field is independent of distance from the plane — just like a uniform surface charge for E!

Bar magnets must involve microscopic current loops. The "north" end is where an observer sees counterclockwise current; the "south" end sees clockwise current. Magnetism in everyday materials (iron, nickel, cobalt) arises from these atomic-scale current loops, not from magnetic charges.

Exam-Level Questions (20 MCQ + FRQs)

Multiple-Choice Questions (20 MCQ)

MCQ 1: Lorentz Force Direction
A positive charge moves in the +x direction through a uniform magnetic field pointing in the +z direction. The magnetic force on the charge is in what direction?
(A) +x
(B) −z
(C) −y
(D) +y
Show Solution

Answer: (C) −y

F = q(v × B) = q(v x̂ × B ẑ) = qvB(x̂ × ẑ) = qvB(−ŷ). Using the right-hand rule: fingers point in x, curl toward z, thumb points in −y.

MCQ 2: Magnetic Work
A charged particle moves through a static magnetic field. The work done by the magnetic force is:
(A) Positive, increasing kinetic energy
(B) Negative, decreasing kinetic energy
(C) Always zero
(D) Depends on the field geometry
Show Solution

Answer: (C) Always zero

Since F_mag = q(v × B) is perpendicular to v, the dot product F·v = 0 always. Magnetic forces change direction, never speed.

MCQ 3: Divergence of B
The equation ∇·B = 0 implies which of the following?
(A) B is always zero
(B) Magnetic field lines are always straight
(C) There are no magnetic monopoles
(D) Magnetic fields are conservative
Show Solution

Answer: (C) There are no magnetic monopoles

A nonzero divergence would indicate a source or sink of field lines (a monopole). ∇·B = 0 means magnetic field lines have no beginning or end — they must form closed loops.

MCQ 4: Steady Current Condition
In magnetostatics, a "steady current" requires:
(A) J = 0 everywhere
(B) ∂J/∂t ≠ 0 but ∂ρ/∂t = 0
(C) ∂ρ/∂t = 0 and ∂J/∂t = 0, with J ≠ 0
(D) Charges must be stationary
Show Solution

Answer: (C)

Steady currents mean charges are moving (J ≠ 0) but the configuration is constant in time: both ∂ρ/∂t = 0 and ∂J/∂t = 0. Charges are constantly being replaced so nothing changes.

MCQ 5: Biot-Savart vs. Coulomb
The key difference between Coulomb's law and the Biot-Savart law is:
(A) Different distance dependence (1/r² vs. 1/r³)
(B) E points radially from dq; B points perpendicular to the current element
(C) Biot-Savart applies to accelerating charges
(D) Coulomb's law involves a cross product
Show Solution

Answer: (B)

Both have 1/r² dependence. Coulomb: E points away from dq (dot-product-like). Biot-Savart: B points perpendicular to the current element (cross-product). This is the fundamental structural difference: electrostatics is "divergency," magnetostatics is "curly."

MCQ 6: Infinite Wire Field
The magnetic field at distance s from an infinite straight wire carrying current I is:
(A) μ₀I/(4πs²)
(B) μ₀I/(2πs)
(C) μ₀I/(4πs)
(D) μ₀I·s/(2π)
Show Solution

Answer: (B) μ₀I/(2πs)

This standard result can be derived either from Biot-Savart (setting θ₁ = −π/2, θ₂ = π/2) or from Ampère's law with a circular Ampèrian loop of radius s: B(2πs) = μ₀I.

MCQ 7: Parallel Wire Forces
Two parallel wires carry currents in the same direction. They:
(A) Attract each other
(B) Repel each other
(C) Exert no force on each other
(D) Twist toward each other
Show Solution

Answer: (A) Attract

The field of wire 1 at wire 2 points toward wire 1 (by right-hand rule). The force on wire 2 is I₂ × B₁, which points toward wire 1. Parallel currents attract; antiparallel currents repel.

MCQ 8: Solenoid Field
The magnetic field inside an ideal infinite solenoid (n turns/length, current I) is:
(A) μ₀nI/(2πs), circling the axis
(B) μ₀nI, uniform and parallel to axis
(C) μ₀I/(2πR), where R is the solenoid radius
(D) Zero
Show Solution

Answer: (B) μ₀nI, uniform and parallel to axis

By Ampère's law with a rectangular loop half inside and half outside: BL = μ₀nIL, so B = μ₀nI. The field outside is zero, and the field inside is uniform — independent of position within the solenoid.

MCQ 9: Solenoid Outside Field
The magnetic field outside an ideal infinite solenoid is:
(A) μ₀nI, same as inside
(B) μ₀nI/2, half of inside
(C) Falls off as 1/s
(D) Exactly zero
Show Solution

Answer: (D) Exactly zero

An Ampèrian loop entirely outside the solenoid encloses no current: B(a) = B(b) for any two distances. Since B → 0 at infinity, it must be zero everywhere outside.

MCQ 10: Ampère's Law Basis
Ampère's law in integral form is derived from Stokes' theorem applied to:
(A) ∇·B = 0
(B) ∇·E = ρ/ε₀
(C) ∇ × B = μ₀J
(D) ∇ × E = 0
Show Solution

Answer: (C) ∇ × B = μ₀J

Apply Stokes' theorem: ∫(∇×B)·da = ∮B·dl = μ₀∫J·da = μ₀I_enc. Gauss's law comes from the divergence theorem on ∇·E; Ampère's law comes from Stokes' theorem on ∇×B.

MCQ 11: Continuity Equation
The continuity equation ∇·J = −∂ρ/∂t is a mathematical statement of:
(A) Gauss's law
(B) Newton's third law
(C) Local conservation of charge
(D) Ampère's law
Show Solution

Answer: (C) Local conservation of charge

If current flows out of a region (positive ∇·J), the charge density inside must decrease (negative ∂ρ/∂t). This is the precise mathematical statement that charge can't just appear or disappear — it must flow continuously.

MCQ 12: Surface Current Density
A surface current density K has units of:
(A) A/m² (current per area)
(B) A/m (current per width)
(C) C/m² (charge per area)
(D) A (current)
Show Solution

Answer: (B) A/m

K = dI/dl⊥ = σv. It is the current per unit width of a "ribbon" of current. By contrast, J = dI/da⊥ is current per unit cross-sectional area (A/m²).

MCQ 13: Cyclotron Radius
A charged particle of momentum p and charge Q moves in a circle of radius R in a uniform field B. What is p?
(A) p = QB/R
(B) p = QR/B
(C) p = QBR
(D) p = Q/(BR)
Show Solution

Answer: (C) p = QBR

Balance centripetal force with magnetic force: QvB = mv²/R, so p = mv = QBR. This is the cyclotron formula — fundamental for particle accelerators and momentum measurements.

MCQ 14: Infinite Plane Current
The magnetic field of an infinite uniform surface current does not depend on:
(A) The current density K
(B) The permeability μ₀
(C) The distance from the plane
(D) The direction of K
Show Solution

Answer: (C) The distance from the plane

B = (μ₀/2)K — constant everywhere above (or below) the plane. This is perfectly analogous to the electric field of an infinite plane of charge: E = σ/(2ε₀), which also doesn't depend on distance.

MCQ 15: Magnetic Force on a Line Current
The magnetic force on a straight segment of wire carrying current I in a uniform field B is:
(A) F = qvB
(B) F = IL × B
(C) F = I∮B·dl
(D) F = μ₀I²/(2πd)
Show Solution

Answer: (B) F = IL × B

For a straight wire of length L in a uniform field, the force is F = I(L × B). L points in the direction of the current. (D) gives the force per unit length between two parallel wires, not the general force formula.

MCQ 16: Permeability of Free Space
The value of μ₀ is:
(A) 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²)
(B) 4π × 10⁻⁷ N/A²
(C) 1/(4πε₀)
(D) 3 × 10⁸ m/s
Show Solution

Answer: (B) 4π × 10⁻⁷ N/A²

(A) is ε₀. (C) is Coulomb's constant. (D) is the speed of light. The permeability of free space μ₀ is an exact defined constant: μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ N/A². Together with ε₀, it determines the speed of light: c = 1/√(ε₀μ₀).

MCQ 17: Toroid Field
Inside a toroidal coil of N total turns carrying current I, at distance s from the center, the field is:
(A) μ₀NI (uniform, like solenoid)
(B) μ₀NI/(2πs)
(C) μ₀nI (with n = N/L)
(D) Zero
Show Solution

Answer: (B) μ₀NI/(2πs)

Ampère's law on a circle of radius s inside the toroid: B(2πs) = μ₀NI. The field is circumferential and depends on distance from the center. Outside the toroid, the field is zero (the Ampèrian loop encloses zero net current).

MCQ 18: Moving Point Charge
A single point charge moving at constant velocity:
(A) Constitutes a steady current to which Biot-Savart applies exactly
(B) Does NOT constitute a steady current; Biot-Savart is only approximate (v ≪ c)
(C) Produces no magnetic field whatsoever
(D) Produces a uniform magnetic field
Show Solution

Answer: (B)

A point charge is here one instant and gone the next — it cannot produce a static field. Biot-Savart applies only to steady currents. For a moving point charge, the approximate formula B ≈ (μ₀/4π)(qv × r̂)/r² is valid only for v ≪ c. The exact result requires Chapter 10 (retarded fields).

MCQ 19: Magnetostatics Character
Compared to electrostatics, magnetostatics is fundamentally more:
(A) "Divergency" — based on scalar quantities and dot products
(B) "Curly" — based on vector quantities and cross products
(C) Simple — fewer equations
(D) None of these
Show Solution

Answer: (B) "Curly"

Electrostatics uses divergence (Gauss's law), scalars (V, ρ), and dot products. Magnetostatics uses curl (Ampère's law), vectors (A, J), and cross products. The source (current) is a vector, the potential (A) is a vector, and the fundamental law (Biot-Savart) involves a cross product.

MCQ 20: Force Per Unit Length Between Wires
The force per unit length between two long parallel wires separated by distance d, carrying currents I₁ and I₂, is:
(A) μ₀I₁I₂/(4πd²)
(B) μ₀I₁I₂/(2πd)
(C) μ₀I₁I₂d/(2π)
(D) μ₀I₁I₂/(4πd)
Show Solution

Answer: (B) μ₀I₁I₂/(2πd)

The field of wire 1 at wire 2 is B₁ = μ₀I₁/(2πd). The force per unit length on wire 2 is f = I₂B₁ = μ₀I₁I₂/(2πd). This result is used to define the ampere in the SI system.

Free-Response Questions (FRQs)

FRQ 1: Biot-Savart — Finite Wire Segment (8 pts)
A straight wire segment extends from z = −L to z = +L along the z-axis and carries a steady current I in the +z direction.
  1. (3 pts) Using the Biot-Savart law, show that the magnetic field at a point P a perpendicular distance s from the wire in the xy-plane is B = (μ₀I/4πs) · 2L/√(s² + L²), directed azimuthally.
  2. (2 pts) Show that in the limit L → ∞, this reduces to the infinite-wire result B = μ₀I/(2πs).
  3. (3 pts) Two such finite segments are placed parallel, separated by distance d, carrying equal currents in the same direction. Find the force per unit length at the midpoint of each wire.
Show Solution
Part (a):Set up coordinates with the wire on the z-axis. For a current element at position z': dl' = dz' ẑ, separation vector r = s ŝ − z' ẑ, magnitude |r| = √(s² + z'²). The cross product dz' ẑ × r̂ gives the φ̂ component: dz' · s / (s² + z'²). Integrate from −L to +L: B = (μ₀I/4π) ∫ s dz' / (s² + z'²)^(3/2) = (μ₀I/4πs) · 2L/√(s² + L²).
Part (b):As L → ∞: 2L/√(s² + L²) → 2L/L = 2, so B → μ₀I/(2πs). ✓
Part (c):Field of wire 1 at wire 2: B₁ = (μ₀I/4πd) · 2L/√(d² + L²). Force per unit length at midpoint: f = IB₁ = μ₀I²/(4πd) · 2L/√(d² + L²). For L ≫ d this approaches the infinite-wire result μ₀I²/(2πd).
FRQ 2: Ampère's Law — Thick Wire (9 pts)
A long cylindrical wire of radius a carries a total current I distributed so that the volume current density is proportional to the distance from the axis: J = ks, for some constant k.
  1. (2 pts) Find the constant k in terms of I and a.
  2. (4 pts) Using Ampère's law, find B for s < a (inside the wire).
  3. (3 pts) Find B for s > a (outside the wire) and verify it matches the infinite-wire result.
Show Solution
Part (a):I = ∫J·da = ∫₀ᵃ (ks)(s ds dφ) = 2πk ∫₀ᵃ s² ds = 2πka³/3. So k = 3I/(2πa³).
Part (b):Draw Ampèrian loop of radius s < a. Enclosed current: I_enc = 2πk ∫₀ˢ s'² ds' = 2πks³/3 = I(s/a)³. Ampère's law: B(2πs) = μ₀I(s/a)³, so B = μ₀Is²/(2πa³) φ̂.
Part (c):For s > a, all current I is enclosed: B(2πs) = μ₀I, so B = μ₀I/(2πs) φ̂ — exactly the infinite straight wire result, as expected. ✓
FRQ 3: Solenoid with Two Windings (8 pts)
Two long coaxial solenoids each carry current I but in opposite directions. The inner solenoid has radius a and n₁ turns/length. The outer has radius b and n₂ turns/length.
  1. (3 pts) Find B inside the inner solenoid (s < a).
  2. (3 pts) Find B between the solenoids (a < s < b).
  3. (2 pts) Find B outside both solenoids (s > b).
Show Solution
Part (a):Use a rectangular Ampèrian loop with one long side inside (at radius s < a) and one far outside. The loop's radial legs cross both sets of windings. For each unit length along the solenoid axis, the inner winding contributes n₁I threading through the loop in one direction, and the outer winding contributes n₂I in the opposite direction. The net enclosed current per unit length is (n₁ − n₂)I. Ampère's law gives: BL = μ₀(n₁ − n₂)IL, so B = μ₀(n₁ − n₂)I ẑ.
Part (b):For a < s < b, the rectangular loop's radial legs now cross only the outer winding (the inner solenoid is entirely inside the loop, so its windings are not pierced by the radial legs). The net current threading through the loop per unit length is n₂I (from the outer solenoid only, in the negative direction): B = −μ₀n₂I ẑ.
Part (c):For s > b, the Ampèrian loop is entirely outside both solenoids. By the standard solenoid argument, B = 0.
FRQ 4: Circular Loop on Axis (7 pts)
A circular loop of radius R in the xy-plane, centered at the origin, carries steady current I.
  1. (4 pts) Using the Biot-Savart law, derive the magnetic field on the z-axis: B(z) = (μ₀I/2) R²/(R² + z²)^(3/2).
  2. (3 pts) Show that far from the loop (z ≫ R), the field looks like that of a magnetic dipole with moment m = IπR².
Show Solution
Part (a):By symmetry, horizontal components cancel around the loop. For each element dl': |r| = √(R² + z²), and dl' ⊥ r̂. The vertical component has factor cos θ = R/√(R² + z²). Integrating dl' = 2πR: B = (μ₀I/4π) · (2πR)·R / (R² + z²)^(3/2) = (μ₀IR²) / (2(R² + z²)^(3/2)) ẑ. ✓
Part (b):For z ≫ R: (R² + z²)^(3/2) ≈ z³, so B ≈ μ₀IR²/(2z³) = μ₀(2m)/(4πz³) where m = IπR². This is the on-axis field of a magnetic dipole: B = (μ₀/4π)(2m/z³). ✓
FRQ 5: Composite Wire Loop — Biot-Savart (Griffiths Prob 5.9 style) (8 pts)
A steady current I flows around the loop shown: two straight segments along the x-axis (from −b to −a and from a to b), connected by two semicircular arcs — an inner arc of radius a and an outer arc of radius b, both centered at the origin in the xy-plane. Find the magnetic field at the origin.
  1. (2 pts) Argue that the straight segments contribute nothing to B at the origin.
  2. (3 pts) Find the contribution of the inner semicircular arc (radius a).
  3. (3 pts) Find the total field at the origin from the entire loop.
Show Solution
Part (a):For the straight segments along the x-axis, dl' is parallel (or antiparallel) to r̂ (which points from the source to the origin along the x-axis). Since dl' × r̂ = 0 when they are parallel, the straight segments contribute nothing.
Part (b):For the inner semicircle (radius a), every element dl' is perpendicular to r̂, and |r| = a is constant. The cross product |dl' × r̂| = dl'. By the right-hand rule, dB points in the −ẑ direction (into the page if current goes counterclockwise in the upper half). The integral over the semicircle gives:
B_inner = (μ₀I / 4πa²) · πa = μ₀I / (4a) in the −ẑ direction.
Part (c):The outer semicircle (radius b) contributes similarly but with opposite orientation (current flows the other way around): B_outer = μ₀I / (4b) in the +ẑ direction. Total:
B = (μ₀I/4)(1/b − 1/a) ẑ
or equivalently B = (μ₀I/4)(1/a − 1/b)(−ẑ). The direction depends on convention for current direction; the magnitude is (μ₀I/4)|1/a − 1/b|.
FRQ 6: Thick Infinite Slab — Ampère's Law (Griffiths Prob 5.15 style) (9 pts)
A thick infinite slab extends from z = −a to z = +a and carries a uniform volume current density J = J₀ x̂.
  1. (2 pts) What is the direction of B? Argue using symmetry.
  2. (4 pts) Using Ampère's law, find B inside the slab (|z| < a).
  3. (3 pts) Find B outside the slab (|z| > a), and verify continuity at z = a.
Show Solution
Part (a):Current flows in the x-direction. By right-hand rule, B must be in the −ŷ direction for z > 0 and +ŷ for z < 0. There can be no x or z component by symmetry: the slab is infinite in x and y, so B cannot depend on x or y, and it cannot point along x (parallel to J) or z (perpendicular but wrong parity). Thus B = B(z) ŷ.
Part (b):Draw a rectangular Ampèrian loop in the yz-plane of width l, extending from −z to +z (with z < a). The top and bottom sides are parallel to ŷ; the left and right sides are parallel to ẑ (B ⊥ ẑ, so these contribute nothing).
∮ B·dl = B(z)l − B(−z)l = −2B(z)l (B flips sign, so B(−z) = −B(z)).
Wait — more carefully: with B pointing in −ŷ for z > 0, the top contributes −B(z)l going in the +ŷ direction if our loop runs counterclockwise.
Enclosed current: I_enc = J₀ · l · 2z (area = l × 2z).
Ampère's law: 2|B|l = μ₀J₀(2z)l, so B = −μ₀J₀z ŷ for |z| < a.
Part (c):For z > a, the entire slab is enclosed: I_enc = J₀ · l · 2a.
2|B|l = μ₀J₀(2a)l, so B = −μ₀J₀a ŷ for z > a and B = +μ₀J₀a ŷ for z < −a.
Check continuity at z = a: inside formula gives −μ₀J₀a ŷ, outside formula gives −μ₀J₀a ŷ. ✓ Continuous.
FRQ 7: Finite Solenoid On-Axis (Griffiths Prob 5.11 style) (7 pts)
A solenoid of radius R with n turns per unit length carries current I. It extends from z = −L/2 to z = +L/2. Using the result for a single circular loop on its axis (Ex 5.6), find the magnetic field at a point P on the z-axis.
  1. (2 pts) Model a thin slice of the solenoid at position z' of thickness dz' as a single loop. What is its effective current?
  2. (3 pts) Write down and evaluate the integral for B(z) at an arbitrary point z on the axis. Express your answer in terms of the angles θ₁ and θ₂ subtended at P by the two ends of the solenoid.
  3. (2 pts) Show that for an infinite solenoid (L → ∞), this reduces to B = μ₀nI.
Show Solution
Part (a):A thin slice at z' of width dz' contains n dz' turns, so its effective current is dI = nI dz'. It acts like a circular loop of radius R carrying current nI dz'.
Part (b):From Ex 5.6, the field of a single loop on axis at distance d = z − z' is:
dB = (μ₀R²nI dz') / [2(R² + (z−z')²)^(3/2)]
Integrate from z' = −L/2 to z' = +L/2. Substitute z − z' = R tan α, so dz' = −R sec²α dα and (R² + (z−z')²)^(3/2) = R³ sec³α:
B = (μ₀nI/2) ∫ cos α dα = (μ₀nI/2)(sin θ₂ − sin θ₁)
where θ₂ and θ₁ are the angles from P to the top and bottom ends respectively, measured from the perpendicular. More precisely, if we define θ measured from the axis: B = (μ₀nI/2)(cos θ₂ − cos θ₁).
Part (c):For L → ∞: P is deep inside, so θ₂ → 0 (top end directly above) and θ₁ → π (bottom end directly below):
B = (μ₀nI/2)(cos 0 − cos π) = (μ₀nI/2)(1 − (−1)) = μ₀nI. ✓
FRQ 8: Cyclotron Motion (Griffiths Ex 5.1 / Lecture 14 style) (6 pts)
A proton (mass m, charge q) enters a region of uniform magnetic field B = B₀ ẑ with velocity v = v₀ x̂.
  1. (2 pts) Describe the subsequent motion. In what plane does the proton orbit? What is the radius?
  2. (2 pts) Find the cyclotron frequency (angular velocity of the orbit). Does it depend on the speed?
  3. (2 pts) Now suppose the proton enters with v = v₀ x̂ + v_z ẑ. Describe the trajectory and find the pitch of the helix.
Show Solution
Part (a):The magnetic force F = qv × B is perpendicular to both v and B. Since v is in x̂ and B in ẑ, the force initially points in −ŷ. The proton executes uniform circular motion in the xy-plane. Balance centripetal force: qv₀B₀ = mv₀²/R, so R = mv₀/(qB₀).
Part (b):The cyclotron frequency: ω = v₀/R = qB₀/m. Remarkably, ω does NOT depend on the speed — this is why cyclotrons work: particles at different speeds all orbit at the same frequency, just with different radii.
Part (c):The z-component of velocity is unaffected by B (since v_z ẑ × B₀ ẑ = 0). The proton moves in a helix: circular motion in the xy-plane with radius R = mv₀/(qB₀), plus uniform drift in the z-direction at speed v_z. The pitch (distance along z per revolution) is: p = v_z · T = v_z · 2π/ω = 2πmv_z/(qB₀).

Struggling Student — Common Misconceptions

❌ "The Biot-Savart law applies to any moving charge."
FALSE. The Biot-Savart law applies only to steady currents. A single moving point charge does NOT constitute a steady current (ρ changes in time). The approximate formula B ≈ (μ₀/4π)(qv × r̂)/r² is valid only for v ≪ c. For a proper treatment of moving charges, you need Chapter 10 (retarded potentials). This is explicitly flagged in both lectures.
❌ "Magnetic forces can do work on a charge."
FALSE. Since F_mag = q(v × B) is always perpendicular to v, the power F·v = 0. Magnetic forces change direction, never speed. When it looks like a magnetic crane does work, it's actually the electric field (induced by the changing B) or the battery maintaining the current that does the work.
❌ "∇·B = 0 means B is always zero."
FALSE. Zero divergence just means no sources or sinks — field lines never begin or end. They form closed loops. A solenoid has a strong B field inside, yet ∇·B = 0 everywhere. Compare: an incompressible fluid has ∇·v = 0, but v is certainly not zero.
❌ "Ampère's law always gives you B directly."
FALSE. Ampère's law is always true, but it's only useful for calculating B when there is sufficient symmetry to pull B outside the line integral. Just like Gauss's law: always true, but only useful with spherical, cylindrical, or planar symmetry. Ampère's law works for infinite straight lines, infinite planes, infinite solenoids, and toroids.
❌ "The current in the wire moves at the speed of light."
FALSE. The drift velocity of electrons in a typical wire is absurdly slow (mm/s — literally a snail's pace). What propagates at nearly the speed of light is the electromagnetic signal (the field pattern), not the electrons themselves. Negative charges moving left is equivalent to positive charges moving right.
❌ "The field outside a solenoid is always exactly zero."
Partially true, with caveats. This is exactly true only for an ideal infinite solenoid. A real finite solenoid has fringing fields near the ends. Also, an ideal solenoid has a net current along its axis (since the wire spirals), which contributes a tiny azimuthal B outside. But for exam purposes with "infinite solenoids," B = 0 outside is the correct answer.
The most important thing to internalize: Electrostatics is about ∇· (divergence, sources, scalars), Magnetostatics is about ∇× (curl, circulation, vectors). If you try to think about B the way you think about E, you'll make systematic errors. Always ask: "Is this a dot-product situation or a cross-product situation?"

Concept Map

How the Concepts Connect

  • Steady currents → B: Currents are the sole source of magnetic fields in magnetostatics.
  • Biot-Savart: Always valid; the "hard way" to compute B from any current distribution.
  • Ampère's Law: The "easy way" when symmetry permits — analogous to Gauss's law in electrostatics.
  • ∇·B = 0: No monopoles. Guarantees B = ∇×A for some vector potential A.
  • Lorentz Force: How B affects charges — perpendicular to velocity, does no work.
  • Continuity equation: Constraint on sources (J and ρ) — ensures charge conservation.

The Hardest Parts Explained

🔹 1. Setting Up Biot-Savart Integrals

Why it's hard: You must handle primed coordinates (source) vs. unprimed (field point), the separation vector r = r − r', and the cross product simultaneously.

Step-by-step recipe:

  1. Choose coordinates and parameterize the current path: dl' = ... dλ.
  2. Write the separation vector: r = r_field − r_source.
  3. Compute |r| and r̂.
  4. Compute the cross product dl' × r̂.
  5. Integrate. Use symmetry to identify components that cancel.

Key tip: Always check which components cancel by symmetry BEFORE integrating. For a circular loop on the z-axis, horizontal components cancel, leaving only the z-component.

🔹 2. Choosing the Right Ampèrian Loop

Why it's hard: The loop must exploit the symmetry so that B is constant along (or perpendicular to) each segment. A poor choice makes B impossible to extract.

Rules:

  • Infinite wire: Circle centered on wire. B is constant and parallel to dl around the loop.
  • Infinite plane: Rectangle straddling the surface. B is parallel to dl on top and bottom.
  • Solenoid: Rectangle with one side inside, one side outside. B·dl = 0 on the outside side.
  • Toroid: Circle concentric with the ring. B is azimuthal and constant on the loop.
🔹 3. The Cross Product in Biot-Savart

Why it's hard: Unlike Coulomb's law (which points radially), the Biot-Savart integrand involves a cross product, making the direction of dB constantly change as you integrate around the source.

Strategy: Convert to Cartesian components early. Compute the cross product component by component. Then use symmetry to kill terms before integrating. The azimuthal direction (φ̂) is typically what survives for a straight wire; the axial direction (ẑ) survives for a loop on-axis.

🔹 4. Distinguishing J, K, and I

Why it's hard: There are three different ways to describe current, and you need the right one for each geometry.

  • I (A) — total current. Use for wires (1D).
  • K (A/m) — current per unit width. Use for sheets (2D). K = σv.
  • J (A/m²) — current per unit area. Use for volumes (3D). J = ρv.

Translation rule: I dl ↔ K da ↔ J dτ. When in doubt, think dimensionally: all three have the same "dimensions" when multiplied by the appropriate geometric element.

🔹 5. Why the Derivation of ∇×B = μ₀J Is Tricky

Why it's hard: In the Biot-Savart integral, the derivatives (∇) act on unprimed coordinates while J depends on primed coordinates. You need product rules for ∇ acting on composite expressions like J(r') × r̂/r².

Key insight: The derivation uses ∇·(r̂/r²) = 4πδ³(r) (from Chapter 1) to extract J from under the integral, yielding the delta function that gives ∇×B = μ₀J(r). This is the exact same mathematical trick as in proving Gauss's law from Coulomb's law.

Memory Anchors

📌 "E is divergency, B is curly"

Electrostatics: sources are charges (scalars), field diverges from them (∇·E = ρ/ε₀), and the curl is zero (∇×E = 0). Uses dot products.

Magnetostatics: sources are currents (vectors), field curls around them (∇×B = μ₀J), and the divergence is zero (∇·B = 0). Uses cross products.

Mnemonic: "E Diverges, B Curls" — the initials spell "EDBC," which you can remember as "Every Day, B Circles."

📌 "Magnetic forces: change direction, never speed"

Since F_mag ⊥ v always, magnetic forces are like a frictionless wall or ramp: they redirect motion without adding or removing kinetic energy. The speed |v| is constant; only the direction changes. This is why charged particles in uniform B follow circles (cyclotron motion).

📌 Right-Hand Rule — Three Uses
  • Cross product: Fingers curl from first vector to second; thumb gives direction.
  • Wire: Thumb in direction of current; fingers curl in direction of B.
  • Ampèrian loop: Fingers in direction of integration; thumb gives positive direction for enclosed current.
📌 Key Numbers to Memorize
  • Infinite wire: B = μ₀I/(2πs)
  • Solenoid: B = μ₀nI (inside), 0 (outside)
  • Infinite plane: B = μ₀K/2 (constant!)
  • Toroid: B = μ₀NI/(2πs) (inside), 0 (outside)
  • Loop on axis: B = μ₀IR²/[2(R² + z²)^(3/2)]
  • Force between wires: f = μ₀I₁I₂/(2πd) per unit length
  • Cyclotron: p = QBR
📌 "Capacitors ↔ Inductors" Analogy

Parallel plates produce uniform E (capacitor stores energy in E). Solenoids produce uniform B (inductor stores energy in B). This is the bridge from statics to circuits — and ultimately to electromagnetic waves, where oscillating E and B feed each other.
