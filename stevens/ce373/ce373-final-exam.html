🧭 Roadmap of the Course

Each chapter builds on the previous. The following progression captures the logic of structural analysis:

  1. Loads (Ch 1) → identify what acts on the structure.
  2. Reactions (Ch 2) → find external support forces from equilibrium.
  3. Internal forces (Ch 4) → compute V and M throughout each member.
  4. Moving loads (Ch 6) → use influence lines to find maximum effects.
  5. Deflections (Ch 8) → quantify how much the structure bends.
  6. Indeterminate analysis (Ch 10, 12) → handle structures with redundant supports using force or displacement methods.

🔑 The Three Pillars of Structural Analysis

Every problem—determinate or indeterminate—reduces to enforcing some combination of these three requirements:

CompatibilityDisplacements at supports/joints must be consistentForce method, all indeterminate analyses

Real-world structural engineering uses these same three pillars in commercial software (SAP2000, ETABS, RISA). Mastering these classical methods gives you the intuition to spot when computer output is wrong—a critical skill for protecting public safety.

Ch 1: Structures & LoadsHibbeler 1.1–1.4

🏗️ Classification of Structures

  • Tie rods: Slender members carrying tension only (bracing struts).
  • Beams: Carry transverse loads, primarily resist bending. Wide-flange shapes are most efficient.
  • Columns: Carry axial compression. Beam-columns carry combined axial + bending.
  • Trusses: Slender pin-connected members forming triangles; carry axial loads only.
  • Cables & Arches: Cables carry tension; arches carry compression — both ideal for long spans.
  • Frames: Beam-column assemblies with rigid joints, often statically indeterminate.
  • Surface structures: Shells, membranes, plates (out of CE 373 scope).

  • Allowed when influence area KLL·AT ≥ 400 ft² (37.2 m²).

🔧 Design Methods (ASD vs LRFD)

Hibbeler §1.4 (pp. 28–29) introduces both methods conceptually. Detailed load combinations are specified by the governing design codes (AISC, ACI, ASCE 7) — the textbook emphasizes analysis, not code-specific combinations.

  • ASD (Allowable Stress Design): Single safety factor on materials. e.g., DL + 0.6·WL.
  • LRFD (Load & Resistance Factor Design): Separate factors for loads and resistance. e.g., 1.2·DL + 1.6·LL + 0.5·SL.
Code requirements like ASCE 7-10 evolved from real-world failures (e.g., the Hyatt Regency walkway collapse in 1981 was traced to a faulty connection that doubled the design load on washers — see Hibbeler §2.6). Always design conservatively when load uncertainty is high.

Ch 2: FBDs & ReactionsHibbeler 2.1–2.6

🔧 Idealized Supports (memorize!)

Support TypeReactionsUnknowns
Light cable / weightless linkForce along link1
Roller / RockerForce ⊥ to surface1
Smooth contact / pin-connected collarForce ⊥ to surface1
Smooth pin / hingeFx, Fy2
Slider (fixed-connected collar)F + M2
Fixed supportFx, Fy, M3

📊 Equilibrium Equations (Coplanar)

ΣFx = 0 ΣFy = 0 ΣMO = 0

For each rigid body, three equations are available. Compound structures (with internal pins) require dismembering and writing 3 equations per piece.

🔍 Determinacy & Stability

For a coplanar structure with r reactions and n parts (members):

r = 3n → Statically determinate r > 3n → Statically indeterminate (degree = r − 3n) r < 3n → Unstable

Even when r ≥ 3n, instability can occur if:

  • All reaction forces are concurrent at a point (no moment resistance).
  • All reaction forces are parallel (no horizontal resistance).
  • Members form a collapsible mechanism.

🏢 Tributary Loadings (Floor Systems)

  • One-way slab: When L2/L1 > 2, load goes only to the long beams. Tributary width = spacing.
  • Two-way slab: When L2/L1 ≤ 2, load distributes to all 4 sides. Cracks form at 45° from corners → triangular and trapezoidal tributary areas.

Example: A 100 lb/ft² load on a square 10×10 slab (two-way) creates a triangular load on each beam with peak intensity (100 psf)(5 ft) = 500 lb/ft.

📋 Procedure for Finding Reactions

  1. Draw the FBD of the entire structure (or each piece if compound).
  2. Label all unknown reactions according to the support type table.
  3. Apply ΣMO = 0 about a point that eliminates the most unknowns.
  4. Use ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0 for the remaining unknowns.
  5. For compound beams, work from the end with the most known information toward the more constrained end.
📌 Worked Example: Compound Beam (from Exam I, Problem 1a)

The bent frame in Exam I had a horizontal arm (10 ft, with 1.5 k/ft uniform load) connected to a vertical column (12 ft, with 2 k point load). Reactions were given:

  • At A: 8.7 k upward (vertical force at fixed-pin top)
  • At C: 6.3 k upward, 2 k horizontal (base reactions)
  • Internal moment at B: 12 k·ft

The FBD of member AB shows: 1.5 k/ft over 10 ft, 8.7 k upward at A, 6.3 k upward and 12 k·ft moment at B. Verify: ΣFy = 8.7 + 6.3 − 1.5(10) = 0 ✓

Compound beams (with internal hinges) appear in bridge spans (e.g., Gerber bridge), allowing thermal expansion while keeping the beam continuous. The internal pin/hinge transmits force but no moment — that's the key analytical insight.

Ch 4: Shear & Moment DiagramsHibbeler 4.1–4.5

🔧 Internal Loadings at a Section

When a beam is "cut" by an imaginary section, three resultant internal loadings can develop:

  • Normal force (N): Axial along the beam (often = 0 for transversely loaded beams).
  • Shear force (V): Perpendicular to the beam axis.
  • Bending moment (M): Couple that resists bending.

✚ Sign Convention (memorize!)

The Hibbeler sign convention defines positive internal loads on a small beam segment as:

  • Positive N: Pulls the segment apart (tension).
  • Positive V: Rotates the segment clockwise ("V down on right face, V up on left face").
  • Positive M: Bends the segment concave upward ("smiles, holds water").

📐 Key Differential Relations

dV/dx = w(x) (slope of V diagram = load intensity, +up) dM/dx = V (slope of M diagram = shear at point) ΔV = ∫ w dx (change in V = area under loading) ΔM = ∫ V dx (change in M = area under shear)

Useful corollaries:

  • Where V = 0 → M is at a local extremum (often the maximum).
  • A concentrated downward force creates a downward jump in V.
  • A clockwise applied couple creates an upward jump in M.
  • If w is degree n, V is degree n+1, M is degree n+2.

📋 Procedure: Drawing V and M Diagrams

  1. Determine all support reactions.
  2. Plot V starting at the left support: jumps for point loads, slopes for distributed loads.
  3. Compute key values of V at load discontinuities (just left and right of each load).
  4. Locate where V = 0 (this is where M peaks).
  5. Plot M starting at zero (for a simply supported beam) using areas under V curve.
  6. Mark the peak moment value, label sign, and inflection points.

🏗️ Frames: Shear & Moment Diagrams

For frame analysis:

  1. Determine reactions from FBD of the entire frame.
  2. Section the frame at each joint to find internal joint forces.
  3. For each member, draw V and M diagrams using the local axis system.
  4. Use the convention that positive moment causes the inside (compression) face to be on a specific side. Always note which side is the "compression face" so reinforcement can be placed correctly.

➕ Method of Superposition

For a beam with multiple loadings, the moment diagram can often be drawn as the sum of simpler "cantilever" or "simply supported" components. This is especially useful in deflection problems (Ch 8).

Common simple cases:

  • Cantilever + tip load P → triangular M diagram, peak = PL.
  • Cantilever + UDL w → parabolic M diagram, peak = wL²/2.
  • Simply supported + center P → triangular M, peak = PL/4.
  • Simply supported + UDL w → parabolic M, peak = wL²/8.
📌 Worked Example: Simply Supported Beam with UDL (from Exam II, Problem 4)

Consider a simply supported beam of length L = 10 m with UDL w = 50 kN/m. Find V and M diagrams.

Reactions: Ay = By = wL/2 = 250 kN.

Shear: V(x) = 250 − 50x. Linear, V(0) = +250, V(10) = −250, V = 0 at x = 5 m.

Moment: M(x) = 250x − 25x². Parabolic, M(0) = M(10) = 0, Mmax = M(5) = 625 kN·m.

Check formula: Mmax = wL²/8 = 50(100)/8 = 625 kN·m ✓

Ch 6: Influence LinesHibbeler 6.1–6.3, 6.6–6.7

🎯 What Is an Influence Line?

An influence line represents the variation of a single function (reaction, shear, or moment) at a specific point in a structure as a unit load (P = 1) moves across the entire span.

Key contrast with shear/moment diagrams:

  • V/M diagram: Shows internal force at all points for fixed loading.
  • Influence line: Shows function at one point as load moves.

📐 Two Methods to Construct ILs

1. Tabulate Values: Place P = 1 at successive positions x along the beam. Compute the desired function (reaction, shear, or moment) at each position. Plot vs. x.

2. Müller-Breslau Principle (qualitative shape):

  • For a reaction R: Remove the support, apply unit upward displacement → deflected shape is the IL for R.
  • For shear V at point C: Insert a roller/sliding device at C, displace ends in opposite directions by 1 unit → deflected shape is the IL for V.
  • For moment M at point C: Insert a hinge at C, apply equal and opposite unit rotations → deflected shape is the IL for M.

📊 Using Influence Lines for Loads

For concentrated load F at position x: Function = F × (IL ordinate at x) For uniform distributed load w over a region: Function = w × (area under IL within the region)

To maximize a function value:

  • Place point loads at peak ordinates of the IL.
  • Place uniform live load over all positive (or all negative) regions.
  • Apply uniform dead load over the entire structure (it must always be present).

🚂 Series of Concentrated Moving Loads

For a series of wheel loads (like a truck), the maximum value occurs when one of the loads is positioned at the peak of the IL. Use the change-in-function method:

  1. Place the heaviest load at the peak first.
  2. Compute change ΔV (or ΔM) when the load group shifts by one wheel spacing.
  3. Continue while ΔV stays positive (still increasing the function).
  4. When ΔV becomes negative, the previous configuration was optimal.

🎯 Absolute Maximum Moment (Series of Loads)

The absolute maximum bending moment in a simply supported beam under a moving series of loads occurs under one of the loads (call it Pi) when:

  1. The resultant force FR of the load group is computed (with location x̄).
  2. Pi and FR are positioned equidistant from the centerline of the beam.
  3. Mmax occurs under Pi, not under FR.

Repeat for each load in the series, then take the largest result.

Procedure (Hibbeler §6.7, Example 6.21): 1. Compute resultant FR and its location x̄ from the load group. 2. For each candidate load Pi, position the load group so that the beam centerline is exactly midway between Pi and FR. 3. Find support reactions, then compute M directly under Pi using the method of sections. 4. Compare M values for all candidate loads — the largest is Mabs,max. Note: As a general rule, the absolute maximum moment usually occurs under the largest force lying nearest the resultant force of the system.

🌉 Influence Lines for Floor Girders

When a deck transmits load to a girder via floor beams (panel points), the IL for shear/moment in the girder consists of straight line segments between panel points. Compute the IL ordinates at each panel point (place unit load at each panel point successively) and connect with straight lines.

📌 Worked Example: IL for Reaction at D (from Exam II, Problem 2)

Consider the continuous beam with hinge at C and supports at A, D, E (from the sample exam):

Ch 8: DeflectionsHibbeler 8.1–8.5

🎯 Three Classical Methods

For statically determinate beams (Ch 8 scope), CE 373 covers three deflection methods:

  1. Double Integration: Integrate EI·v″ = M(x) twice; apply boundary conditions to find constants.
  2. Moment-Area Method: Two geometric theorems based on the M/EI diagram.
  3. Conjugate-Beam Method: Treat M/EI as a load on a transformed "conjugate" beam.

📐 The Fundamental Beam Equation

EI · d²v/dx² = M(x) (small-slope assumption)

where v is the deflection (positive upward), x is along the beam, M is the internal moment, E is Young's modulus, and I is the moment of inertia about the neutral axis.

📊 Moment-Area Theorems

Theorem 1 (slope): The change in slope between any two points A and B on the elastic curve equals the area under the M/EI diagram between A and B.

θB/A = ∫AB (M/EI) dx = Area of M/EI from A to B

Theorem 2 (tangential deviation): The vertical distance from point A on the elastic curve to the tangent line drawn at point B equals the moment of the M/EI area between A and B, taken about A.

tA/B = (Area of M/EI from A to B) × (x̄ measured from A)

Critical: tA/B is not the deflection at A — it's the perpendicular distance from A to the tangent at B. Use geometry (similar triangles) to convert to actual deflection.

🔄 Conjugate-Beam Method

The conjugate beam has the same length as the real beam, loaded with the M/EI diagram. Then:

  • Slope on real beam = Shear on conjugate beam: θ = V*
  • Deflection on real beam = Moment on conjugate beam: v = M*

Support transformations (Real → Conjugate):

Real BeamConjugate Beam

Fixed (v=0, θ=0)Free (V*=0, M*=0)
Free (v≠0, θ≠0)Fixed (V*≠0, M*≠0)

Ch 10: Force Method (Consistent Deformations)Hibbeler 10.1–10.2, 10.4

🤔 Why Indeterminate Structures?

Statically indeterminate structures have more unknowns than equilibrium equations. They are common in modern construction because they:

  • Carry loads more efficiently (smaller stresses, smaller deflections than determinate equivalents).
  • Have redundant load paths — if one member fails, alternative paths still carry the load.

Tradeoffs: Indeterminate structures are sensitive to support settlement, temperature changes, and fabrication errors, which can induce significant internal stresses.

🎯 Concept: Consistent Deformations

The force method (also called the method of consistent deformations or the flexibility method) chooses one or more support reactions or internal forces as redundants. The structure is then made determinate by removing these redundants. Compatibility of displacement at the released supports forces the redundants to take on values consistent with the actual structure.

📐 General Procedure (Single Redundant)

  1. Identify a redundant (e.g., a roller reaction RB). Remove it. Call the remaining structure the primary structure.
  2. Compute ΔB: Apply the actual loads to the primary structure. Compute the deflection at the released support B (using moment-area, conjugate beam, or virtual work).
  3. Compute fBB: Apply a unit load at B in the direction of the redundant. Compute the deflection at B per unit load — this is the flexibility coefficient.
  4. Compatibility equation: Real beam has ΔB = 0 at the support. Therefore:
0 = ΔB + RB · fBB → RB = −ΔB / fBB
  1. Find remaining reactions using the three equilibrium equations.

🔢 Multiple Redundants (Matrix Form)

For an n-times indeterminate structure with redundants R1, R2, …, Rn, the compatibility equations form a matrix system:

[f] {R} = −{Δ} (flexibility matrix equation) where: fij = deflection at i caused by unit load at j Δi = deflection at i caused by the actual loads

Maxwell's reciprocal theorem: fij = fji, so the flexibility matrix is symmetric.

🌡️ Settlement, Temperature, and Fabrication Errors

If a support settles by amount Δs, the compatibility equation becomes:

−Δs = ΔB + RB · fBB

The negative sign accounts for downward settlement (with positive R upward). Always check sign conventions carefully.

📌 Worked Example: Propped Cantilever with UDL

Problem: Cantilever fixed at A, roller at B, length L, UDL w. Find RB.

Step 1: Choose RB as redundant. Primary structure: cantilever fixed at A.

Step 2: ΔB due to w on cantilever: ΔB = wL⁴/(8EI) downward.

Step 3: fBB due to unit upward load at B on cantilever: fBB = L³/(3EI) upward.

Step 4: Compatibility: 0 = −wL⁴/(8EI) + RB·L³/(3EI). Solve: RB = 3wL/8 (upward).

Step 5: Equilibrium: Ay = wL − 3wL/8 = 5wL/8; MA = wL²/2 − (3wL/8)(L) = wL²/8.

The force method generalizes to influence lines for indeterminate beams: by Müller-Breslau, the IL shape is the deflected shape when the constraint is released and a unit displacement applied. Computing actual ordinates requires the force method.

Ch 12: Moment Distribution MethodHibbeler 12.1–12.3

🧠 The Big Idea

Hardy Cross's moment distribution (1930) is a displacement method that bypasses solving simultaneous equations. Instead, it iteratively "locks" and "unlocks" joints, distributing unbalanced moments until equilibrium is reached. It's perfect for hand analysis of continuous beams and frames without sidesway.

📋 Sign Convention

Clockwise moments acting on the member at a joint are positive. Counterclockwise moments are negative. This applies to both fixed-end moments and the moments developed during distribution.

🔧 Three Key Quantities

1. Stiffness factor K: The moment required at the near end to rotate it by 1 rad while the far end is fixed.

K = 4EI/L (far end fixed) K' = 3EI/L (far end pinned, modified stiffness)

2. Distribution factor DF: The fraction of an applied moment at a joint that goes into a particular member.

DFi = Ki / Σ K (Σ over all members at the joint) DF = 0 at a fixed support (infinite stiffness) DF = 1 at a pin/roller end (cannot resist moment beyond) ΣDF = 1 at every interior joint

3. Carry-over factor COF: The fraction of a moment applied at one end that "carries over" to the other end.

COF = +1/2 (far end fixed) COF = 0 (far end pinned, when using K' = 3EI/L)

📐 Fixed-End Moments (FEMs)

FEMs are the moments that develop at the ends of a beam if both ends were fixed. Memorize or carry the table:

LoadingFEMAB (left)FEMBA (right)
Center point load P−PL/8+PL/8
UDL w over full span−wL²/12+wL²/12
Triangular load (max w at right)−wL²/30+wL²/20
Point load P at distance a from left−Pab²/L²+Pa²b/L²
Settlement Δ at right (CW)−6EIΔ/L²−6EIΔ/L²

Sign convention: counterclockwise FEM is negative when acting on the member.

📋 Procedure for Moment Distribution

  1. Compute stiffness factors K = 4EI/L for each member (or K' = 3EI/L if far end is pin).
  2. Compute distribution factors DF = K/ΣK at each interior joint.
  3. Compute fixed-end moments for each loaded span.
  4. Set up table with columns for each member end, rows for FEMs, distributions, carry-overs.
  5. Distribute the unbalanced moment at each joint: ΔMi = −(unbalanced moment) × DFi.
  6. Carry over half of each distributed moment to the far end (if far end is fixed).
  7. Repeat steps 5–6 until the carry-over moments become negligibly small.
  8. Sum each column to get the final end moments.

📌 Worked Example: Two-Span Continuous Beam

Problem: Two equal spans of length L, UDL w on both. Far ends pinned, center support is a roller. Find moment at center support B.

Step 1: KBA = KBC = 3EI/L (using modified stiffness for pin-supported far ends).

Step 2: DFBA = DFBC = (3EI/L) / (6EI/L) = 0.5.

Step 3: FEMs: Use formulas for "fixed–pinned beam with UDL" → FEMBA = −wL²/8, FEMBC = +wL²/8.

Step 4: Net moment at B = −wL²/8 + wL²/8 = 0. No distribution needed!

Wait — but the actual moment at B isn't zero! That's because the beam is symmetric. Let's redo with K = 4EI/L (far ends fixed, conventional approach):

FEMBA = −wL²/12, FEMBC = +wL²/12. Net at B = 0. Hmm, still zero by symmetry of distribution at B.

Ah — the actual moment at B is found by summing column B after distribution. By symmetry of the beam and loading, the unbalanced moment at B is zero from the start, so MB = FEMBA = +wL²/12 (positive on member BA, which means hogging at B). Final MB = wL²/8 if K' = 3EI/L is used (matching the propped-cantilever result from Ch 10).

🏗️ Frames Without Sidesway

If the frame is restrained from lateral movement (or the loading is symmetric), moment distribution proceeds as for beams. The only new feature: at each joint, multiple members converge, so the DF is split among all of them.

🏗️ Frames With Sidesway

When the frame can sway laterally, an additional analysis is needed:

  1. Apply an artificial restraint at the joint that would sway. Compute the reaction R needed to hold the structure (call this Case I).
  2. Apply −R alone (no other loads) and analyze (Case II).
  3. Superpose Case I + Case II.

Most CE 373 problems will be no-sidesway frames.

Modern stiffness-based finite element software (matrix methods, Ch 14–16) is a generalization of moment distribution. Hand moment distribution remains valuable for spot-checking computer results and for quick design estimates.

Practice Problems

Multiple Choice (10 problems)

MCQ 1: Determinacy of a Beam
A continuous beam has 4 supports (1 fixed at the left, 3 rollers) and one internal hinge. How many degrees of static indeterminacy?
(A) Statically determinate (degree 0)
(B) Indeterminate to the 2nd degree
(C) Indeterminate to the 3rd degree
(D) Unstable
Show Solution

Answer: (B)

Reactions: r = 3 (fixed) + 3·1 (rollers) = 6. Internal hinge releases 1 moment equation, giving 1 extra equation. With n = 1 piece (continuous beam) and the hinge: r − 3n − (hinges) = 6 − 3(1) − 1 = 2. Indeterminate to 2nd degree.

MCQ 2: Maximum Shear in a Cantilever
For a cantilever beam of length L with UDL w, the maximum shear force occurs at:
(A) The fixed support, with magnitude wL
(B) The free end, with magnitude wL/2
(C) Midspan, with magnitude wL/2
(D) The fixed support, with magnitude wL²/2
Show Solution

Answer: (A)

Total load = wL. Reaction at fixed support = wL upward. Shear varies linearly from wL at the wall to 0 at the free end. Option (D) confuses shear with the fixed-end moment (wL²/2).

MCQ 3: Wind Load Pressure
If wind speed doubles (V → 2V), the wind pressure on a building changes by what factor?
(A) 2×
(B) 4×
(C) 8×
(D) 16×
Show Solution

Answer: (B)

Wind pressure q ∝ V². Doubling V quadruples the pressure. This is why hurricane categories (rated by V) cause exponentially worse damage as wind speeds rise.

MCQ 4: IL by Müller-Breslau (Reaction)
To draw the IL for the vertical reaction at support B of a simply supported beam (A pin, B roller), you should:

Answer: (B)

Müller-Breslau: For a reaction, remove the constraint and impose a unit displacement in the direction of the constraint. The deflected shape (a straight line for a simply supported beam) is the IL.

MCQ 5: Distribution Factor at a Joint
At an interior joint of a frame, three members meet with K1 = 100, K2 = 200, K3 = 200. The distribution factor for member 1 is:
(A) 0.50
(B) 0.40
(C) 0.20
(D) 1.00

Answer: (C)

MCQ 6: Force Method Compatibility
For a propped cantilever (fixed at A, roller at B) with no load, support B settles by Δ. Using RB as redundant, the compatibility equation is:
(A) 0 = RB · fBB
(B) −Δ = RB · fBB (with downward Δ positive)
(C) Δ = RB/L
(D) Δ = MA·L/(EI)
Show Solution

Answer: (B)

The settled support has displacement −Δ (downward). With RB upward as the redundant, the compatibility equation reads: actual displacement at B = (no-load deflection at B) + RB·fBB. With no other loads, the no-load deflection is 0 in the primary structure (cantilever), so −Δ = RB·fBB.

MCQ 7: Carry-Over Factor
When using K = 4EI/L for a member with the far end fixed, the carry-over factor from near to far end is:
(A) 0
(B) +1/2
(C) +1
(D) −1/2
Show Solution

Answer: (B)

For a beam fixed at the far end, when a moment M is applied at the near end (rotating it 1 rad), the moment induced at the far end is +M/2 — same direction (clockwise on member). COF = +1/2.

MCQ 9: Maximum Moment Location, Series Loads
For a series of moving wheel loads on a simply supported beam, the absolute maximum moment occurs:
(A) At midspan, with the resultant at midspan
(B) Under one of the loads, when that load and the resultant are equidistant from midspan
(C) Under the resultant force
(D) At the support nearest the heaviest load

Answer: (B)

By the absolute-maximum-moment theorem, Mmax occurs under one of the loads when that load and the resultant of the system are placed equidistant from the centerline. Repeat for each load to find which gives the largest M.

MCQ 10: Conjugate-Beam Support
For a real cantilever beam (fixed at A, free at B), the conjugate beam supports are:
(A) Fixed at A, free at B (same)

Common Misconceptions

❌ "Statically determinate is always better."
FALSE. Indeterminate structures have lower internal stresses, smaller deflections, and redundant load paths (preventing total collapse if one member fails). The design tradeoff is sensitivity to support settlement and temperature effects. Most modern buildings are intentionally indeterminate.
❌ "An internal hinge means zero shear at that point."
FALSE. An internal hinge means zero moment, but shear is generally nonzero. Hinges allow rotation but transfer shear and axial force. This is essential for compound beam analysis (Exam I, Problem 2a).
❌ "Maximum shear and maximum moment occur at the same location."
USUALLY FALSE. By dM/dx = V, maximum moment occurs where V = 0. So Mmax and Vmax are typically at different locations. Vmax is usually at supports; Mmax is between supports.
❌ "Influence lines and shear/moment diagrams are the same thing."
FALSE. Shear/moment diagrams show V or M at all points for fixed loading. Influence lines show V or M at one point as load moves. They have completely different uses.
❌ "Maximum moment under series of loads occurs under the resultant force."
FALSE. Maximum moment occurs under one of the loads (call it Pi), positioned so that Pi and the resultant FR are equidistant from midspan. This is a subtle but critical point for FRQ problems.
❌ "The conjugate beam has the same supports as the real beam."
USUALLY FALSE. The conjugate beam swaps supports: fixed↔free, internal pin↔internal roller. Pin/roller supports stay the same. Forgetting to transform supports is the #1 error in conjugate-beam problems.
❌ "Distribution factor sums to 1 at every joint, including end pins."
PARTIALLY FALSE. ΣDF = 1 at interior joints only. At a fixed support, DF = 0 (infinite resistance). At a pin/roller end (released), DF = 1 (the full distribution goes to the only member). Use this to set up the moment-distribution table correctly.
❌ "FEM = wL²/12 always for a UDL on a span."
PARTIALLY FALSE. wL²/12 applies only when both ends are fixed. If one end is pinned, FEM at the fixed end = wL²/8 and FEM at the pin = 0. This corresponds to using K' = 3EI/L (modified stiffness). Always know which formula to use for which boundary condition.
❌ "Force method always uses support reactions as redundants."
FALSE. The redundant can be any unknown — support reaction, internal moment, internal force in a closed loop. Choose the redundant that makes the primary structure easiest to analyze (e.g., reduces a complex frame to a cantilever).
❌ "Live load reduction always reduces live load by 50%."
FALSE. The reduction is governed by the formula L = Lo·[0.25 + 15/√(KLL·AT)] but is limited to ≥50% Lo for one floor or ≥40% Lo for multiple floors. The actual reduction depends on the influence area.
Many of these misconceptions come from over-generalizing simple cases (cantilever, simply supported) to all situations. The remedy is to always sketch the actual support conditions and verify boundary conditions before applying any formula.

The Hardest Parts Explained

🔹 1. Compound Beam Analysis (Internal Hinges)

Why it's hard: Internal hinges create extra "joints" where moment is zero but shear is not. Forgetting to dismember at the hinge leads to incorrect equilibrium equations.

Strategy:

  1. Draw the structure and identify all internal hinges/pins.
  2. Dismember at each hinge — draw separate FBDs for each piece.
  3. Show equal and opposite hinge forces (two unknowns: Hx, Hy) on adjacent pieces.
  4. Apply 3 equilibrium equations to each piece.
  5. Start with the piece that has the most known information (e.g., the "free" end).

Pro tip: If a sub-beam has only 2 reactions on it (e.g., pin at one end, hinge at the other), it's a "simply supported" sub-beam. Solve it first.

🔹 2. Frame Shear and Moment Diagrams

Why it's hard: Frames have multiple members at angles. Sign conventions for V and M depend on the local member axis. It's easy to get confused about which side is the "positive" face.

Strategy:

  1. Define a local coordinate system for each member (e.g., x along the member from start to end, y perpendicular outward).
  2. Always sketch the deflected shape first — concave up means positive moment.
  3. Drawing convention (Hibbeler §4.4, pp. 160–164): "always draw the moment diagram positive on the compression side of the member." This is opposite to the convention some reinforced-concrete designers use (drawing on the tension side to indicate where rebar is needed). Pick one convention per problem and label it.
  4. Verify joint equilibrium: at each joint, the sum of moments from connecting members must equal zero (or equal the externally applied couple).
🔹 3. Influence Lines for Continuous (Indeterminate) Beams

Why it's hard: The IL is no longer made of straight line segments — it's a curved deflected shape. Computing exact ordinates requires the force method.

Strategy:

  1. Use Müller-Breslau to sketch the qualitative shape of the IL.
  2. To find a specific ordinate, place a unit load at that point and compute the function (reaction, shear, moment) using force method, moment distribution, or computer.
  3. For exam problems, often only the qualitative shape is required — focus on identifying the sign and approximate location of peaks.
🔹 4. Choosing the Best Redundant in Force Method

Why it's hard: Different choices lead to different (more or less complex) primary structures. A poor choice can require lots of computation.

Strategy:

  • Continuous beams: Choose the interior support reactions as redundants. Primary structure becomes a simply supported beam.
  • Propped cantilevers: Choose the roller reaction as redundant. Primary structure is a cantilever.
  • Fixed-fixed beams: Choose the end moments as redundants. Primary structure is simply supported.
  • Closed frames: Cut a member and choose the internal moment, shear, or axial as redundants.

Always sketch the primary structure to verify it's both stable and easy to analyze.

🔹 5. Setting Up the Moment Distribution Table

Why it's hard: Many bookkeeping steps: stiffness factors, distribution factors, FEMs, signs, carry-overs, iterations. One mistake propagates through.

Strategy:

  1. Draw the beam with all spans, supports, and loads labeled.
  2. Make a table with one column per member end (e.g., "AB", "BA", "BC", "CB", ...).
  3. Row 1: Distribution factors (DF = 0 at fixed ends, DF = 1 at pin/roller ends, DF = K/ΣK at interior joints).
  4. Row 2: Fixed-end moments. Use Hibbeler's table — verify signs (CW positive on member).
  5. Row 3 onward: Iterative balancing. At each interior joint, sum the entries above the line, apply the negative of that sum × DF, and write the new entry in each member's column at the joint.
  6. Carry over half of each balancing entry to the far end of the same member (only if K = 4EI/L; not if K' = 3EI/L was used).
  7. Repeat until carry-overs are negligible (typically 4–6 iterations).
  8. Sum each column to get the final end moments.

Pro tip: Always check that ΣM at each joint = 0 in the final answer.

🔹 6. Sign Conventions Across Methods

Why it's hard: Different methods use different sign conventions:

  • Internal forces (Ch 4): Positive M = sagging (concave up); positive V = clockwise on segment.
  • Moment distribution (Ch 12): Positive M = clockwise on the member at the joint.
  • Slope-deflection equations: Positive M and θ = clockwise.

Strategy: When solving a problem, pick one convention and stick to it. When converting between methods, be very careful about sign translations.

🔹 7. Tributary Areas for Two-Way Slabs

Why it's hard: Two-way slabs distribute load to all four edges via 45° crack lines, creating triangular and trapezoidal tributary areas.

Strategy:

  1. Determine if slab is one-way (L2/L1 > 2) or two-way (≤ 2).
  2. For two-way, draw 45° lines from corners. The shorter sides get triangular tributary areas; longer sides get trapezoidal.
  3. Peak load intensity on a beam = (slab pressure) × (perpendicular distance from beam to nearest 45° line). For square slabs, this is ½ of the side length.
  4. Compute total load = area of triangle/trapezoid × pressure, then express as equivalent beam loading.
Mastery of these "hard parts" comes from doing many problems. After Exam I and II, focus your remaining practice on Force Method (Ch 10) and Moment Distribution (Ch 12), since these will likely be new on the final.

Memory Anchors & Mnemonics

📌 Anchor 1: "3-48-384" Standard Deflections

Memorize these three deflection formulas and you cover ~80% of beam deflection problems:

  • Cantilever + tip P: δ = PL³/(3EI)
  • Simply supported + center P: δ = PL³/(48EI)
  • Simply supported + UDL w: δ = 5wL⁴/(384EI)

And bonus: cantilever + UDL → δ = wL⁴/(8EI). Pattern: more support = larger denominator = less deflection.

📌 Anchor 2: "wL²/8 vs wL²/12" — Pinned vs Fixed
  • FEM at fixed end of UDL beam: wL²/12 (both ends fixed) or wL²/8 (other end pinned).
  • Moment at midspan of simply supported UDL beam: wL²/8.

Memory device: "8 looks like a pin (top circle), 12 has a fixed look (no circle)."

📌 Anchor 3: Conjugate Beam Support Swap (Hibbeler Table 8.2)

The full correspondence between real and conjugate beam supports:

  • Real fixed end ↔ conjugate free end
  • Real free end ↔ conjugate fixed end
  • Real pin or roller end ↔ conjugate pin or roller end (stays the same)
  • Real internal pin/hinge ↔ conjugate internal roller (allows shear discontinuity = slope discontinuity in real beam)
  • Real internal roller ↔ conjugate internal pin/hinge

Memory device: "Real boundaries flip — Free ↔ Fixed; Pin/Roller stay; Internal hinge ↔ internal roller." See Hibbeler Table 8.2 for the full correspondence.

📌 Anchor 4: "DF Joint Sums to 1"

At every interior joint in moment distribution: ΣDF = 1. This is the easiest sanity check on your DF table.

At a fixed end: DF = 0. At a pin/roller end (using K = 3EI/L): DF = 1.

📌 Anchor 5: "COF = 1/2 (when K = 4EI/L)"

The carry-over factor is +1/2 when the far end is fixed. The +1/2 means the far-end moment is in the same rotational sense as the applied moment.

If using modified K' = 3EI/L (far end pinned), then COF = 0 — no carry-over.

📌 Anchor 6: "Müller-Breslau: Remove → Displace → Sketch"

For drawing influence lines:

  • Reaction: Remove the support, apply unit upward displacement.
  • Shear: Insert sliding device, displace ends in opposite directions by 1.
  • Moment: Insert hinge, apply unit rotation (relative).

The deflected shape is the IL (to a scale factor).

📌 Anchor 7: "Equilibrium → Compatibility → Constitutive"

Three pillars of any indeterminate analysis:

  1. ΣF = 0, ΣM = 0 (equilibrium of forces and moments).
  2. Displacements at supports/joints must match physical reality (compatibility).
  3. Force ↔ deformation relation (M = EI·κ, etc.).
📌 Anchor 8: "Indeterminacy: r vs 3n"

For a coplanar structure: r = number of reactions, n = number of bodies (rigid pieces).

  • r < 3n: Unstable
  • r = 3n: Determinate
  • r > 3n: Indeterminate (degree = r − 3n)

Subtract 1 from r for each internal hinge (it releases 1 moment equation).

📌 Anchor 9: "Wind Pressure ∝ V²"

Doubling wind speed quadruples the pressure. Triple wind = 9× pressure. This is why hurricane categories matter so much.

📌 Anchor 10: "M_max Where V = 0"

Locate where shear crosses zero — that's where bending moment is at a local extremum (often the maximum). This is the fastest way to find Mmax on V/M diagrams.

Mnemonics are aids, not substitutes for understanding. Use them to speed up exam time, but always know why each formula or rule works.

🏆 Score-Maximizing Tactics

  • Don't get stuck. If a problem is taking more than the allotted time, move on. Come back at the end.
  • Skip parts, not problems. If you can't solve part (a) but can do parts (b) and (c) using assumed answers, do them anyway. Partial credit adds up.
  • State assumptions. "Assuming small deflections, linear elastic behavior, EI constant..." sets the proper analytical context.
  • Use the table on the inside back cover of Hibbeler for FEMs and standard deflections. It's open-book — use the resources!
  • Sanity check magnitudes. A 10-m beam with 1-kN load should not deflect by 1 m. A small calculation error in a formula often produces wildly wrong answers.
  • Box your final answers. Like the Exam I solutions, draw a box around each numerical result. This helps the grader and yourself.
